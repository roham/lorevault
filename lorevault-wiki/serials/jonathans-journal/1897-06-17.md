## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He had gone to a room he should not have been in. He knew this afterward. He could not account for why he had gone there or how he had arrived — he remembered leaving his room, and he remembered being in the room, and the interval between the two was absent from his memory.

The three women: he recorded what he could. Their dress was correct for the period. Their manner was composed. He felt, in their presence, something that he could not name in terms of fear because it was not exactly fear. He could not name it in terms of desire, though it was closer to that. He was precise about this distinction in his notes afterward: it was not fear because fear involves a judgment that something is dangerous; he was aware of the danger without the judgment functioning. His analysis ran correctly while the capacity to act on it did not.

The Count arrived. He spoke the phrase.

*This man belongs to me.*

Jonathan wrote this in his journal because it was the most precise legal formulation he had received since arriving at the Castle. It was a claim of ownership — not tenancy, not use, not license, but ownership. He had no counter-instrument. He had no title. He had arrived as a professional and was now, in the Count's formulation, a belonging.

He wrote in his journal: *17 June — the three women. The phrase. No counter-instrument.*

He noted: he had not, at the time, thought of Mina. He noted this carefully, because it was the one fact about the encounter that he found most difficult to record.

## Tether Note

*The ownership claim* — Jonathan's solicitor's register finally meets its match: a claim stated in the correct legal register (ownership: "belongs to me") for which he has no counter-argument. Jonathan's professional response — to note the claim, note the absence of counter-instrument, note the title — is the document that makes the horror precise. The three women are recorded as phenomena (dress, manner, the absence of shadow, the dual emotion) but the key line is the Count's legal claim. Jonathan has been a professional witness to the Count's property transactions throughout the journal. He is now within the inventory.

The detail that most troubles Jonathan — that he did not think of Mina at the time — is noted in one sentence and not expanded on. The journal does not analyze it. The reader is left with it.
