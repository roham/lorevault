## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He knew the relevant provisions.

A British subject in difficulties abroad had the right to seek consular assistance. The consulate would receive a letter; the consul would make enquiries; the consul had certain powers in cases of this kind. He had advised clients on this. He knew the instrument.

He began the letter. He addressed it formally and set out the situation in the plain declarative language appropriate for a consular communication.

On the third sentence, he stopped.

He did not know the address of the nearest consulate. He had not thought to bring a directory; he had not thought he would need one. He thought: I could ask the Count. The Count had, on several occasions, provided Jonathan with information about the postal service in this region.

He put the pen down. The page remained blank past the salutation.

He thought: even if I had the address, the letter would go through the Count's hands. Every letter had gone through the Count's hands. He had established this in May, when he had understood what the letters were for.

He wrote in his journal: *4 June — consulate letter. Salutation only. Address unknown.*

## Tether Note

*The available instrument* — Jonathan reaches for the legal right that should protect him: consular assistance, a real provision, an instrument he knows well. He cannot use it because he lacks the address, and even if he had the address, the delivery mechanism is controlled by the person from whom he needs protection. The instrument is available in principle and inaccessible in practice — the vocabulary of access without the key, in a different register.

This is the solicitor's situation made precise: every legal instrument Jonathan has — property law, consular rights, postal provisions — is either irrelevant to his situation or is accessible only through the Count.
