## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He had sent the letters — or rather: the letters had been sent. He was no longer certain how to think about the difference.

He had expected, in a vague and uncalibrated way, a reply. Mr. Hawkins would have received the letter. Mina would have received the letter. Someone would write back. He had given the Count's address for correspondence. He had assumed there was a mechanism for letters to arrive here.

He waited for three weeks from the date of the sent letters before he permitted himself to name the waiting. Nothing came.

He thought: perhaps replies have come and the Count has them. He thought: perhaps no reply was ever going to arrive, and the sent letters were never intended to produce one. He thought: the letters I sent were not communications; they were documentation. They establish a record that I was here voluntarily and content. A reply would only complicate the record.

He wrote in his journal: *23 May — no letters.*

He thought: I am writing the wrong record. He did not know where to put the right one.

## Tether Note

*The record problem* — Jonathan articulates for the first time the function of the letters he was made to write: documentation, not communication. The letters were never intended to receive replies. This is the moment Jonathan's analysis catches up to the structural reality: he has been producing a legal record of his own willing presence, not maintaining a correspondence. The solicitor's understanding of document function applied, belatedly, to his own documents.

The parallel with Watson's sealed notation (bs1-c03) — a private record alongside the official one — deepens here: Jonathan is realizing he is writing the official record and the private record is going unwritten.
