## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He wrote a brief.

Not to anyone — there was no one to write to — but in the form of a brief: the document type he had been trained to produce, which summarised a situation in the shortest possible form sufficient to support a decision. He had written dozens of briefs. He knew how to do it.

He wrote: *Summary of material facts, May 3–30, 1897.* He listed the dates. He listed what had been observed. He wrote in the abbreviated professional prose that left out emotion and inference and retained only the facts with sources attached.

When he finished, he read it. It took eleven minutes to read. He read it twice.

It was legible. As a document, it was entirely coherent. The sequence of events from May 3 to May 30, written in professional summary, formed a complete and internally consistent account.

He had not expected it to be legible. That was the thing that frightened him.

He burned it. Not because it was wrong: because it was right, and because a document that was right and complete and legible was a document that someone might find. He did not want it to be found.

He wrote in his journal: *1 June — summary brief. Burned.*

## Tether Note

*The legible document* — Jonathan's professional reflex is to construct a brief — the solicitor's canonical form for presenting facts in support of a decision. The brief is coherent; the sequence of events from May 3 to May 30 is legible as a professional document. The discovery that his captivity has a legal summary that holds together is more frightening than any of its individual components. Burning it is not denial: it is recognizing that a legible document of this situation is itself dangerous.

The parallel with the letter-documentation theme: Jonathan has previously recognized that the letters he wrote were documentation, not communication (May 23). Now he produces documentation that he immediately destroys — the first active suppression of his own record.
