## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He tried the south door at dawn.

He had tried it before — May 15, at the end of the systematic door-testing — and it was locked then. It was locked now. He stood in front of it for a long time and studied the mechanism: the escutcheon plate, the keyhole, the set of the deadbolt in its housing. He was not a locksmith. He was a solicitor. He had the vocabulary of access without the key.

He could write a brief for the door. He could describe, in technical language, what was required to open it. He could cite the relevant provisions of property law as they pertained to a lessee's right of egress. None of this would open the door.

He tried the handle. He tried it again. He tried it a third time.

He wrote in his journal: *27 May — south door. Dawn. No different.*

He did not write what he had thought about the lock for the hour he stood in front of it. He did not write that the hour had not felt like an hour. He did not write that he had, at some point during the hour, understood that the door would be locked in the same way until someone else unlocked it, and that the someone else would not be him.

## Tether Note

*The vocabulary of access* — Jonathan has professional command of how access works — the law of ingress and egress, the instruments of conveyance — and zero practical ability to apply this command to his own situation. The solicitor's expertise is relevant to every aspect of the Count's affairs and none of his own. The door-testing pattern (May 14: one per day; May 15: eleven more unmentioned; now May 27: repeating a locked door as if the mechanism might have changed) shows the gap between method and outcome.
