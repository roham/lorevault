## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He wrote two letters to Mina on the same day.

The first was the Count's letter — the prepared text, in Jonathan's hand, with his signature. It said he was well, that the business was proceeding, that he hoped to be home by the end of June. He addressed the envelope. He left it on the writing desk, as the Count had instructed, for the Count to post.

The second letter he wrote on a blank sheet from the back of the casebook he kept for legal notes. He wrote: *I am not well. I cannot say more. Do not write here: write to the Exeter office, marked HOLD. Destroy this letter after you have memorized it. I am well enough. I am not well.* He read it back. He crossed out *I am well enough* because it was not true and because he had already said it in the other letter. He folded the second letter and put it in his coat pocket, beside the letter he had not posted on May 13th.

He thought: I am now producing two records. One that can be sent, and one that cannot.

He thought: Watson keeps something like this. He didn't know why he thought of Watson. He had met Watson once, at Exeter, when Holmes's colleague had stopped by the office about an unrelated matter. Watson had been carrying a notebook he never opened.

He wrote in his journal: *20 May — letters.*

He did not specify: how many.

## Tether Note

*The double record* — Jonathan is now maintaining two parallel accounts of the same situation: the official letters (the Count's record, in Jonathan's hand, deliverable) and the private record (Jonathan's record, unsendable, accumulating in his coat pocket). This is the same structure as Watson's sealed notations in bs1-c03 — the public record and the thing "sealed per his request." Cross-Pane thread: the practice of keeping a private truth alongside an official one is a Lattice-level behavior visible across Baker Street and Gothic Horror.

The Watson reference is load-bearing for the cross-Universe lore layer: Jonathan has met Watson once; he doesn't know why Watson comes to mind. The reader who holds both bs1-c03 and the May 20 installment will see the connection Jonathan can only feel.
