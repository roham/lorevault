## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

The Count brought three prepared letter-forms.

One was to Mr. Hawkins, at the Exeter office: saying that business was progressing well, that the Count had been generous with his time, that Jonathan expected to stay another month beyond the original arrangement. One was addressed to Mina, at her aunt's, at the address Jonathan had given in his first letter home: saying the same, essentially, with different words. The third letter was addressed to a solicitor's firm Jonathan did not recognise, at a London address he could not place.

The Count explained, carefully, that he wished the letters to be in Jonathan's hand. That this was the custom with his English correspondents, who were accustomed to Jonathan's particular style. Jonathan understood this to mean: your handwriting is known. Your handwriting will be trusted.

He copied the letters. He signed them.

He sat afterward and thought about the third letter. He had copied the text without attending to it — it was something about the transfer of goods, nothing that concerned him directly. He thought: the first two letters confirm that I am here voluntarily. If the first two are to be believed, then the third, which I signed and cannot now read, must also be believed. If the third is not to be believed, neither are the first two.

He wrote in his journal: *17 May — letters.*

He did not write: whose.

## Tether Note

*The borrowed hand* — Jonathan's handwriting is being used to build a record of his willing presence. The Count's insistence on Jonathan's "particular style" names what is actually happening: Jonathan's written voice is being borrowed without acknowledgment. The third letter (unidentified firm, unread) is the Lampblack: the letter Jonathan cannot retrospectively evaluate, because to evaluate it is to evaluate the others. The whole record is now entangled.

Cross-Pane resonance with bs1-c11 "The Coded Message": Holmes's cipher symbol recurs six years later in unpublished Watson correspondence, also without explanation. Both: a piece of writing that travels beyond the figure who produced it, with the figure unable to trace where it went or what it was used for.
