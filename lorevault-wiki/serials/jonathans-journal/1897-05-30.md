## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He tried to find the legal category.

Not for the castle, not for the property transaction — those he could categorize. The Count was engaged in a freehold acquisition of English real property; the Carfax estate was the named target; the title examination had begun in 1884 with R.M.S. and continued through Mr. Hawkins's office. That was all regular. Unusual in its scale and the time taken, but regular in its instrument.

The legal category for what was in the central box: this was what he could not find.

A living man could own property. A dead man's estate could be administered. There was no provision in the Conveyancing Act 1881, or in any instrument he had studied, for a man who occupied a state between the two. The law assumed mortality. The law assumed that a person either was alive and therefore capable of holding title, or was dead and therefore subject to administration. The law did not have a category for the figure in the box.

He wrote two pages. He tore them out.

He wrote in the journal: *30 May — no applicable category.*

## Tether Note

*The limit of the instrument* — Jonathan reaches the bound of his professional vocabulary. The Conveyancing Act's mortality assumption is the wall: the law was built on the premise that persons have one of two states. What Jonathan found in the chapel is not administrable under either. The two torn-out pages are not documented; only the conclusion survives in the journal. The torn pages are the third thing the May 29–30 sequence has failed to preserve: the complete reasoning, the blank May 29 date, the torn argument.

Cross-note: R.M.S., who examined the freehold in 1884, presumably also encountered the Count. R.M.S. left a professional notation, not a legal category for what the Count was. R.M.S. may have arrived at the same conclusion and chosen to document only what was documentable.
