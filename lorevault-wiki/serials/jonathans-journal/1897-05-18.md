## Lampblack Detail — daemon annotation (not in Stoker)

He found the library by accident — a door in the south corridor that had been unlocked all along. He had been trying locked doors; this one opened.

The books were English, as Stoker notes, and there were many of them. Law. History. Architecture. Maps and surveys. Volumes of newspapers, bound and dated. Jonathan went to the newspapers first — a clerk's reflex, to find the date.

The most recent bound volume was dated 1886.

He went to the books. History of England, 1882. Survey of London streets, 1884. A legal handbook, third edition, 1885. He checked the magazines. 1886.

He sat down at the library table and did arithmetic. Eleven years.

He thought: whoever assembled this library stopped needing it in 1886. He thought: I can tell what they were studying. England. Its law. Its streets. Its properties. They were preparing something. The preparation ended — or reached its conclusion — eleven years ago.

He wrote in his journal: *The library: extensive English. Most recent dated 1886.*

He did not write: what that means.

## Tether Note

*The gap in the library* — the echo element is the specific year: 1886. Jonathan is doing what he does — making notes without yet arriving at conclusions. The record ends in 1886, which means the preparation ended (or matured) in 1886. The collector who looks at the Dracula timeline will notice that 1886 is within the period of the Count's preparation to move to England. Jonathan has found the archaeological record of the plan without naming it.

The solicitor's instinct: when he enters a room with documents, he goes to the dates first. This is the Spine-event for Jonathan as clerk — the professional habit applied to the personal horror.
