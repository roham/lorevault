# LoreVault Flavor-Text Voice Doctrine

**Status:** Binding on every Set, every card, every drop. Subordinate to `MULTIVERSE-COSMOLOGY-DOCTRINE.md` §5 and §6 (especially Decisions 6, 14, 15).
**Date:** 2026-04-24
**Companion:** `research/R5-mtg-flavor.md` (compression and archetype source); `research/R2-worldbuilders.md` (Pratchett, Le Guin, Pokémon restraint).
**Authority:** Canon Council voice-lead has rewrite power on any flavor text up to 24h before Set lock. Founder has none, per Decision 14.

The bar is unambiguous: flavor text is the difference between a mid product and a Pratchett-caliber one. Each line must sit alongside the best Magic: The Gathering quotes and not flinch — *as poetic, as precise, as meaningful, as heart-hitting.* Compression is the discipline. Voice is the craft. Spine is the truth.

---

## 1. The Five Voices of LoreVault

One narrator-register per Universe. Within a Universe, individual figures inflect the register (Holmes is colder than Watson; the Wolf is hungrier than the Stepmother). Across Universes, the registers must read as five different *literatures*, not five different *moods*. A collector reading a flavor text with the card art masked should know which Pane it came from inside two lines.

### 1.1 Baker Street voice — *Watson chronicling*

**Narrator:** John H. Watson, M.D., or — when the Set explicitly requires it — Mycroft, Mrs. Hudson, Lestrade, the *Strand* sub-editor. Sherlock himself is quoted; he is not the narrator. (See Decision 6 — one narrator per Set; Watson is the default first-pick.)

**Syntactic signature.** Past-tense reportage of the recent past. Sentences open with a clause of time or weather ("It was the November of '94…", "On the morning of the inquest…"). Two-clause structure: observation comma deduction. Em-dashes for the moment when reasoning turns. Semicolons for civilised hesitation. Adjectives are restrained and period-precise — a watch is *gold-cased*, not *gleaming*. Emotion is implied by the omission of the obvious — Watson never says he is afraid, he says the fire was lit and they did not approach it.

**Vocabulary register.** *Favored:* singular, peculiar, evidence, indicated, remained, instrument, the clock, the lamp, the hour. *Forbidden:* awesome, instinct, vibe, gut, totally, energy, frankly. No exclamation marks except inside quoted dialogue and never twice in a Set.

**Examples (in voice):**
> *"Holmes had not slept. He had only become more accurate." — Watson, casebook, March 1895*

> *"He told me where the body would be found. I went. The body was found." — Watson, casebook*

### 1.2 Enchanted Kingdom voice — *bone-rhyme, Grimm tradition*

**Narrator:** the unnamed teller. The voice of *the woodcut on the wall, written by no one*. When attributed, it is to anonymous archetypes — *a charcoal-burner*, *the third sister*, *the hangman's wife*. Pre-Disney. The forest is older than the king and the king will die first.

**Syntactic signature.** Folk-tale rhythm — short declarative sentences, the structure of three. Internal rhyme or assonance in two-line couplets. Ends mid-thought, on a held image, the way woodcut captions do. Verbs in the simple tense — *the wolf came; the door held; the door did not hold*. Witches speak in conditionals (every line a contract). Stepmothers speak in third-person passive ("a girl was given an apple" — never "I gave the girl an apple"). Wolves speak in imperatives.

**Vocabulary register.** *Favored:* bread, ash, iron, crow, debt, oath, the seventh, the youngest. *Forbidden:* magic (used as noun — the power is named for what it does), curse (overused; specify the binding), wonder (mush), suddenly. Articles do heavy work — *the* wolf, never *a* wolf, when the figure is canon.

**Examples (in voice):**
> *"Three loaves she gave the road. The road ate two and kept the third for winter."*

> *"He had seven and a half teeth. The half was the one that mattered."*

### 1.3 Wonderland voice — *Liddell logic, nonsense-as-truth*

**Narrator:** Carroll-adjacent, but never Carroll himself — the chronicler is a *garden chronicler*, a *court recorder of the Queen of Hearts*, an *unsigned correspondent of the Cheshire estate*. The wit is logic-puzzle wit, not joke-wit. A pun is forbidden (Decision 5 / R5 Rule 3). A reversal is mandatory.

**Syntactic signature.** Three-beat structure: statement, restatement, snap. Or: question, answer, second question that breaks the answer. Sentences loop and bite their tails. Definitions are offered and immediately undone. Mathematics is used the way sentiment is used elsewhere — earnestly, and slightly wrong. Second-person address is allowed only from the Queen, only as threat. Italics are used for *categorical* words — *time*, *size*, *court*, *rule* — to mark them as suspect.

**Vocabulary register.** *Favored:* category, exactly, smaller, larger, the rule, the rule before that, the verdict, court, tea. *Forbidden:* whimsy, magical, dreamy, sweetheart, lol, weird (too modern; *peculiar* is allowed). No exclamation marks at all — the Queen shouts in punctuation only via dashes.

**Examples (in voice):**
> *"The verdict came first. The trial took longer, because the verdict had to be defended."*

> *"I asked the cat where the door was. The cat said, 'Where it isn't is also a door, but a less convenient one.'"*

### 1.4 Gothic Horror voice — *epistolary fragments*

**Narrator:** the document. The voice of letters, journal entries, telegrams, ship's logs, opera-libretti marginalia, and the case-notes of physicians who did not survive. First person, present-tense or recent past, written by a witness on the brink. Stoker, Shelley, Leroux, Stevenson — these figures' source texts *are* mode-3 in-world artifacts, and our voice honors that. Mode-3 dominates at ~35% in Set composition; mode-1 at ~50% (signed letters).

**Syntactic signature.** Sentences begin mid-thought, as if continuing a previous page. Dates and locations head the line. Time-markers are exact and obsessive — *3.14 AM*, *the second Tuesday of November*, *the third night since*. Dracula speaks in centuries; Frankenstein's creature speaks in negations (he is *not-this*, *not-that*); Hyde speaks in present-tense imperatives; the Phantom in incomplete sentences interrupted by music. Fragments are not stylistic — they are *testimony broken off*.

**Vocabulary register.** *Favored:* I record, I write, I confess, the hour, the lamp, the hand, the body, the door (always *the door*, never *a door*). *Forbidden:* spooky, creepy, vibes, chill (modern temperature word), shadows (cliché — name what casts them).

**Examples (in voice):**
> *"3.14 AM. The lamp went down twice and I did not light it the second time. — Mina H., journal, fragment"*

> *"I have been the answer for three hundred years. The question, when it came, was smaller than I expected. — Dracula"*

### 1.5 Greek Myth voice — *bronze-tongue, oracular, fated*

**Narrator:** the chorus, or — when a figure speaks — the figure as already-quoted-by-Hesiod, the voice already in citation. Past tense even for unfolding events — *Persephone descended* is acceptable for a card depicting her descent. The Fates have already spoken; the card is the moment they were proved right.

**Syntactic signature.** Two-sentence structure where the second collapses the first. Hesiod-cadence — short statement, then the consequence that was always going to follow. Ordinal time markers (the *third* spring, the *seventh* libation). Abstract nouns capitalised when they are gods doing the noun's work — *Hunger*, *Memory*, *Return*. Ellipsis-openings allowed (per R5 §7 archetype 5). Aphorism is the dominant mode; the gods do not chat.

**Vocabulary register.** *Favored:* bronze, libation, the third, the loom, return, exile, given, taken, *moira* (used sparingly, untranslated, as proper noun for fate). *Forbidden:* destiny (use *moira* or *the share-out*), epic (mush), legendary (we don't tell, we cite), totally any modern intensifier.

**Examples (in voice):**
> *"He chose glory over length. The choice had been made for him in the cradle; he discovered it on the field. — chorus, on Achilles"*

> *"…and so the third spring. She ate the seeds because she was hungry, and was hungry because she had eaten the seeds. — fragment, attributed Hesiod, disputed"*

## 2. Shell Modulation — voice is universe-bound, figure is shell-bound

**Decision (binding):** *the voice does not change with the shell*. Per Decision 3 (Shells and Parallels are orthogonal axes), the universe-narrator stays constant; the *figure inside the voice* changes chassis. Watson, in BS-1 PRIME, narrates an 1895 Holmes. Watson, in BS-2 CYBER, narrates a Holmes-of-the-cyberdeck — but he narrates him in *the same case-file register*. Watson is a Lampblack constant; his prose is the residue. The deduction is about quantum traces or biometric fakes; the voice is gold-cased pocket-watches and lit lamps.

This is the load-bearing rule: **the voice is how the Pane recognizes itself across shells.** If CYBER-Sherlock spoke in Gibson-prose and PRIME-Sherlock in Doyle-prose, there would be no Lampblack — there would be two unrelated franchises wearing the same name. The voice *is* the Spine made audible.

**Worked example — same Sherlock Moment, two shells:**

*BS-3 SHELL-01 PRIME (Watson chronicling Reichenbach, 1891):*
> *"He had told me, the night before, that the cataract was loud enough to drown a smaller voice than his. I had not understood him at the time. — Watson, casebook, May 1891"*

*BS-3 SHELL-02 CYBER (Watson chronicling Reichenbach, datafall, 2079):*
> *"He had told me, the night before, that the cataract of packets was loud enough to drown a smaller signal than his. I did not understand him at the time. — Watson, signed log, datafall, May 2079"*

The case-file cadence holds. The clauses match. The shell-substitution is one noun (*cataract* → *cataract of packets*) and one date. The Lampblack — *I had not understood him at the time* — is identical. The collector who owns both Moments reads them as the same figure refracted, because Watson's prose is constant.

**Worked example — same Wolf Moment, two shells:**

*EK-2 SHELL-01 PRIME (the door, the woodcutter's house, twilight):*
> *"He had seven and a half teeth, and the half was the one that mattered. The door held until the half stopped mattering."*

*EK-2 SHELL-05 HOLLOW (the door, after, the seventh winter):*
> *"He had seven and a half teeth, and the half had survived the burn. The door, this time, was already open."*

Same couplet structure. Same articles. Same scarce-and-iron register. Shell-substitution is two phrases. The Wolf is *the same figure*; the world is what changed. That is the Lattice rendered correctly.

**Exception — never:** the only narrator-shift permitted is *within* a Universe, between Sets, per Decision 6. Watson voices BS-1 and BS-3; Mycroft voices BS-2; Moriarty's posthumous letters voice BS-4. The *register* (Baker Street voice) does not change. The *speaker* may.

## 3. The 12 Quote Archetypes for LoreVault

Extending R5's MTG archetype list and binding it to our five voices. These twelve are the canonical forms; Sets must mix at least four archetypes per twenty cards (Mosaic Test, R5 Rule 10, §5 of cosmology doctrine).

### A1 — The Deduction

**Definition.** Two clauses: one observation, one inference; the inference is heavier than the observation.
**When.** Baker Street primarily; rare in Greek Myth (where the chorus deduces from fate); never in Wonderland (which forecloses inference).
**Form.** ≤ 22 words. Attribution mandatory: *"— Watson, casebook, [date]"* or *"— Holmes, quoted by Watson"*. Em-dash before attribution; period inside the quote.
**Good 1:** *"The mud on his left boot was not from the path he claimed. He had told the truth about the wife. — Watson, casebook, July 1894"*
**Good 2:** *"He looked first at the clock. Whatever he was about to lie about, it had a time on it. — Watson, casebook, October 1888"*
**Bad:** *"Sherlock used his powers of deduction to figure out the mystery!"* — paraphrases the mechanic, modern register, exclamation mark, names "deduction" as a thing instead of doing it.

### A2 — The Prophecy

**Definition.** A statement of a future event in past tense, as if the future has already occurred and the card is the moment it was confirmed.
**When.** Greek Myth (native); Gothic Horror (a letter that predicts its writer's death); Enchanted Kingdom (the witch's contract). Never Wonderland — Wonderland denies coherent time.
**Form.** ≤ 24 words. Attribution to oracle, chorus, or named seer. Past-tense framing of future fact. Capitalize the abstract noun when it is the thing acting (*Memory*, *Return*).
**Good 1:** *"He returned. He was not recognized. The dog knew him; the dog died. — chorus, on Odysseus"*
**Good 2:** *"On the seventh spring she was hungry, and ate. The seeds had already been counted. — fragment, on Persephone"*
**Bad:** *"Persephone will descend to the Underworld in the autumn!"* — future tense breaks the rule; exclamation mark; explanatory; tells you what the card depicts.

### A3 — The Lover's Letter

**Definition.** First-person address from one figure to another, signed, with implicit doom in the margin.
**When.** Gothic Horror (Mina to Jonathan; Christine to the Phantom); Greek Myth rarely (Penelope to Odysseus); Baker Street very rarely and only for Irene Adler.
**Form.** ≤ 30 words. Always signed. Salutation can be omitted — start mid-letter for fragment effect. Date-line acceptable as opening.
**Good 1:** *"My dearest — the lamp has gone down twice and I am keeping it down. He is in the room and I am not afraid. Come anyway. — Mina H."*
**Good 2:** *"I have been told you are dead. I have been told this by a man who lies. I will write again on Tuesday. — Watson, to Holmes, May 1891"*
**Bad:** *"Roses are red, my love is true, Mina misses Jonathan, do you?"* — rhyme-mush, wink, modern, paraphrases the relationship instead of being it.

### A4 — The Curse

**Definition.** A binding stated as if already in force; the consequence is named, the cause is implied.
**When.** Enchanted Kingdom (native — witches, queens, godmothers); Greek Myth (the Furies' kind); Gothic Horror (the family curse).
**Form.** ≤ 18 words. Couplet structure preferred. Conditional grammar (*if you*, *whoever*, *whichever child*). Unsigned — the curse outlives the curser.
**Good 1:** *"Whoever eats the apple sleeps. Whoever names the apple wakes. The apple has no name."*
**Good 2:** *"Iron for the hand that took. Salt for the mouth that named. Neither for the one who watched."*
**Bad:** *"You are cursed forever, mwahaha!"* — villain-voice, exclamation, modern theatricality, fails to name the binding's actual mechanism.

### A5 — The Riddle / Apple-Test

**Definition.** A binary moral choice posed as a riddle; the right answer is the one that costs the chooser something.
**When.** Wonderland (riddle form); Enchanted Kingdom (apple form); Greek Myth (the Sphinx form).
**Form.** ≤ 20 words. Question or framed-choice structure. The answer is never given; the card *is* the answer in some other dimension.
**Good 1:** *"One door asks your name. The other asks who is missing. The one who chooses cannot leave. — Wonderland court record"*
**Good 2:** *"Two apples were offered. One was poisoned. She asked which; the witch answered honestly; she ate it anyway."*
**Bad:** *"What's red, round, and full of poison? An apple! Don't eat it!"* — joke punchline, exclamation, breaks the fourth wall by hailing the reader.

### A6 — The Logbook

**Definition.** Dated entry from a working document — ship's log, doctor's notes, expedition journal, case file. The document does not know it will be famous.
**When.** Gothic Horror primarily (Demeter's log; Frankenstein's notebook); Baker Street (Watson's casebook); Greek Myth rarely (the steward's tally).
**Form.** ≤ 28 words. Date-stamp opens. Verbs are clinical and clipped. The horror is in the omission.
**Good 1:** *"4 November. Captain has not appeared since the watch change. The wheel was lashed. The lashing was new. — log of the Demeter"*
**Good 2:** *"Day 47. The creature spoke. I had not designed it for speech. I have not slept. — V. Frankenstein, notebook"*
**Bad:** *"Today was crazy! The vampire ate the captain and stuff!"* — modern intensifier, "and stuff", paraphrases plot, breaks period.

### A7 — The Hesiod Catalog

**Definition.** A short list of lineages, monsters, or fates, given in cadence, ending on the one that breaks the pattern.
**When.** Greek Myth (native); Enchanted Kingdom (the cadence of begetting); never Baker Street or Wonderland.
**Form.** ≤ 30 words. List-of-three structure with the third item turning. No commas of confusion — semicolons separate items.
**Good 1:** *"Of Night were born Sleep; and Death; and Memory, who is worse than either. — fragment, attributed Hesiod"*
**Good 2:** *"From the king came three sons: the cruel one; the kind one; and the one who watched. The one who watched outlived them."*
**Bad:** *"Greek myths have like, gods and monsters and stuff, it's wild."* — modern voice, uncertain register, no compression, no turn.

### A8 — The Pamphlet / Newspaper Cutting

**Definition.** A printed in-world artifact — a *Strand* article, a court bulletin, a market notice — voiced in period press register.
**When.** Baker Street primarily (the *Strand*, *The Times*, the police bulletin); Gothic Horror occasionally (the *Whitby Gazette*); never Greek Myth or Wonderland.
**Form.** ≤ 30 words. Headline-and-lede structure. Period-precise typography in the prose (a "shilling", a "fortnight", a "constable"). Attribution to the publication, with date.
**Good 1:** *"REICHENBACH: ENGLISH GENTLEMAN MISSING. The body has not been recovered. The local guides have not been paid. — The Times, 8 May 1891"*
**Good 2:** *"For sale, by the executors: gold-cased pocket-watch, gentleman's, lightly worn. Provenance not declared. — Strand classifieds, March 1892"*
**Bad:** *"BREAKING NEWS: Sherlock Holmes is DEAD!!!"* — modern web-headline, uppercase, exclamation, no period detail.

### A9 — The Wisecrack-That-Lands

**Definition.** A line that produces a smile and then a cold thought one second later. Wit, not laugh. Per R5 Rule 3, no puns. The mechanism is misdirection of expectation, not joke structure.
**When.** Wonderland (native — Cheshire, Hatter); Baker Street (Holmes only, sparingly — Watson is too earnest); never Greek Myth.
**Form.** ≤ 18 words. Setup-snap or three-beat structure. Italics permitted on the categorical word that is being undermined.
**Good 1:** *"I asked the cat for directions. The cat replied, 'I am not lost. *You* are. Forgive the asymmetry.'"*
**Good 2:** *"He said the case was elementary. He had been wrong about a great many elementary things that week. — Watson, casebook"*
**Bad:** *"Why did Alice cross the chessboard? To get to the other queen!"* — pun, joke structure, fourth-wall breach, exclamation.

### A10 — The Last Word

**Definition.** A final utterance, given complete and unembellished, dated to the moment of its falling silent.
**When.** All five Universes use this archetype, but the register shifts: Baker Street uses Watson's recall; Greek Myth uses chorus; Gothic Horror uses the final letter; Wonderland uses the verdict; Enchanted Kingdom uses the woodcut caption.
**Form.** ≤ 16 words. The quote is the whole flavor; attribution is *"— [name], last words"* or *"— [name], at [event]"*. No commentary.
**Good 1:** *"'I owe him this much: I will not be the one who looks first.' — Holmes, at Reichenbach"*
**Good 2:** *"'I built the door. I never said I was on the right side of it.' — V. Frankenstein, last entry"*
**Bad:** *"'Goodbye cruel world!' — Romeo or whoever, dramatically"* — generic, mocking, attribution is comedic, no specificity.

### A11 — The Anonymous Witness

**Definition.** A line spoken by an unnamed bystander — a scullery maid, a chorus member, a crossing-sweeper, a third sister, a court page. Lower-class or marginal voice; sees what the principals miss.
**When.** All five Universes. Highest leverage in Greek Myth (chorus is native), Enchanted Kingdom (the third sister, the charcoal-burner), and Baker Street (Mrs. Hudson, the boy in buttons).
**Form.** ≤ 22 words. Vernacular permitted in the source register (a Cockney "weren't" in Baker Street; a Doric "doth" never — we are not Tudor). Attribution to *role*, not name.
**Good 1:** *"He came down them stairs three at a time, and his coat was buttoned wrong. That's how I knew. — the housemaid, 221B"*
**Good 2:** *"They counted the libations and there were eleven. There should have been twelve. No one said. — chorus, second-rank"*
**Bad:** *"Some random person saw stuff happen, idk."* — modern, dismissive of its own narrator, abdicates the witness's authority.

### A12 — The Echo

**Definition.** A flavor text on Card B that quotes — verbatim, partial, or inverted — a flavor text on Card A from a previous Set or Universe. The collector who owns both reads them as a chord.
**When.** All Universes; reserved for Rare and above; one Echo per Set maximum (Mosaic Test discipline). Echoes are the highest expression of Lampblack.
**Form.** ≤ 24 words. The quoted/inverted phrase is italicised inside the new flavor text. Attribution names the Echo deliberately ("*after Watson, casebook, March 1895*").
**Good 1 (Card A, BS-1):** *"Holmes had not slept. He had only become more accurate. — Watson, casebook, March 1895"*
**Good 2 (Card B, BS-2 CYBER, two Sets later):** *"The deck had not slept. *It had only become more accurate.* — signed log, datafall, after Watson"*
**Bad:** *"Like Watson said in another card, Holmes was super smart."* — explains the echo, names the meta, paraphrases instead of quoting, modern register.

## 4. Length and Rhythm Rules

**Word counts per tier (binding):**
- **Common:** ≤ 12 words. One sentence preferred. No attribution required if voice is unmistakable.
- **Rare:** ≤ 20 words. Two sentences maximum. Attribution required if voice ambiguous between figures.
- **Legendary:** ≤ 30 words. Up to three sentences if rhythm justifies. Attribution mandatory.
- **Ultimate:** ≤ 40 words including attribution and dateline. The tier with the most room — and the one most likely to fail by overuse of the room. *Ultimate flavor that uses 40 words must earn each one.*

**Median target across a Set:** 18 words. Per R5 Rule 1.

**The em-dash rule.** The em-dash is reserved for the *turn* — the moment when reasoning, image, or moral inverts. One em-dash per quote, maximum. Two em-dashes in twenty words is a tell that the writer is afraid of the period.

**Attribution rules.**
- **Signed by character:** when the line is a direct quote from a named figure. Format: *"— Holmes, casebook"*, *"— Mina H., journal"*, *"— Hesiod, fragment 47"*.
- **Unsigned:** when the line is the universe-narrator speaking (Wonderland court record, Greek chorus, the woodcut on the wall). Used for ~25% of cards. The voice itself does the work.
- **Document-attributed:** for in-world artifacts. *"— log of the Demeter, 4 November"*, *"— The Times, 8 May 1891"*, *"— Strand classifieds, March 1892"*. Mode-3 — see Cosmology §5.

**The "earn the second sentence" rule.** If a flavor text has two sentences, the second must *turn* or *deepen* the first. Never restate. Never explain. The second sentence is permitted to: (a) invert the moral of the first, (b) reveal the speaker did not see what the reader sees, (c) name the consequence the first sentence left implicit, (d) cite or echo a prior Set. It is forbidden to: paraphrase, soften, summarise, or hedge the first.

**The compression rule (binding, per R5 §4 and Cosmology §5).** Flavor text never paraphrases the art and never paraphrases the mechanic. The art *shows*. The mechanic *does*. The flavor *means*. A Reichenbach Ultimate's flavor never says "Holmes falls" — the art shows him falling, the mechanic enacts the fall, and the flavor must add the *third dimension*: what Watson did not understand the night before, what the cataract was loud enough to drown, what the half-tooth was for. Three dimensions or the card is two-dimensional and we do not ship two-dimensional cards.

## 5. The Forbidden Register

Things flavor text must never do, with the bad example each violation produces.

**Modern slang / Twitter snark.** The Lattice does not know the year is 2026.
> *Bad:* *"Holmes was, like, the OG detective. No cap. — some Strand reporter probably"*

**Joke punchlines.** Wonderland is wit; it is not a comedy club. A pun ends a brand.
> *Bad:* *"Why don't vampires go to barbecues? They don't like steaks!"*

**AI-tells.** "In a world where…", "imagine if…", "perhaps you wonder…", "what if…". These openings come from no human in the Lattice; they come from the wrong machine.
> *Bad:* *"Imagine a world where Sherlock Holmes had a cyberdeck instead of a magnifying glass…"*

**Explanations of the art.** The art is on the card. The flavor adds, never narrates.
> *Bad:* *"As Sherlock raised his pipe and looked into the fog, he could see the body lying just beyond the lamp."*

**Generic fantasy mush.** Wind whispering, ancient trees, mysterious shadows. Mush is what bad flavor reaches for when it has not earned a specific image.
> *Bad:* *"The wind whispered through the ancient trees as the mysterious figure approached the castle's shadowed gates."*

**Author-voice intrusions.** No founder voice (Decision 14). No "we" addressing the collector. No knowing wink.
> *Bad:* *"We at LoreVault think this version of Holmes is the most fascinating one yet — see if you agree."*

**Fourth-wall breaches.** The Lattice does not know it is being read.
> *Bad:* *"You probably weren't expecting Sherlock to be a robot, were you?"*

**Paraphrasing the mechanic.** The card's text box says what it does. The flavor never repeats it.
> *Bad, on a card that draws three cards on Reichenbach:* *"When Holmes falls, you draw three cards."*

**Modern intensifiers and filler.** Totally, literally, basically, kinda, sorta, like, honestly, frankly. None pass.
> *Bad:* *"Watson was, honestly, like, basically the best chronicler ever, frankly."*

**Brand-voice marketing register.** No "epic", no "legendary" used as adjectives (we *are* legendary; we don't claim it), no "ultimate showdown", no "the battle of the century".
> *Bad:* *"Witness the LEGENDARY, EPIC final showdown between Holmes and Moriarty!"*

## Appendix — The Mosaic Test, applied

Voice-lead reads all 20 cards' flavor in sequence at every Set lock. Before lock, ask:

1. Could a fan reading these twenty quotes blind, without art, name the Universe? If yes for at least 18: pass. If not: rewrite.
2. Are at least four archetypes represented? If three or fewer: rewrite the weakest two cards.
3. Does any quote paraphrase its own mechanic? Cut that line; rewrite from compression rule.
4. Does any quote violate Forbidden Register §5? Rewrite, no exceptions.
5. Does at least one quote in the Set Echo a prior Set's quote (A12)? If not, the Set has no Lampblack-by-quote; consider whether one card should carry an Echo.
6. Read the twenty quotes aloud. Do any two sound identical in rhythm? If yes, rewrite one. The Set is a *mosaic*, not a *wallpaper*.

If the Mosaic Test fails after two rewrites, the Set is held. Per Decision 6 and Decision 14, the founder cannot override this. The Canon Council voice-lead has final word.
