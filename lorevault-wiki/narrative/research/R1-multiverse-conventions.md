# R1 — Multiverse Construction Conventions

## 1. Marvel MCU + What If…? + Spider-Verse

**Mechanic.** Post-*Avengers: Endgame* (2019), Marvel codified the "Sacred Timeline" in *Loki* S1 (2021), then shattered it. Branches form when characters defy fixed nexus events; *Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness* (2022) added the "incursion" rule from Jonathan Hickman's *Secret Wars* (2015): when two universes' boundaries collide, both die. Animated tracks (*What If…?*, 2021–24; *Into the Spider-Verse*, 2018; *Across the Spider-Verse*, 2023) run parallel, with *Spider-Verse* introducing "canon events" — fixed beats every Spider-shell must endure.

**What lands.** Recognition arbitrage. Tobey Maguire and Andrew Garfield in *No Way Home* (2021) generated cultural electricity precisely because audiences brought 20 years of prior text. Spider-Punk, Spider-Gwen, Peni Parker work because they're Spider-Man under transformation pressure — same engine, alien chassis.

**Failure mode.** Fatigue and lore tax. *The Marvels* (2023) and *Quantumania* assumed audiences had homework done from Disney+ shows; box office punished the assumption. Anything-goes branching collapsed stakes — if every dead character is alive somewhere, no death matters.

**Steal-rule.** Canon events. Each shell varies aesthetics but obeys a fixed list of beats the character must hit, or they stop being that character.

## 2. DC Multiverse

**Mechanic.** DC numbers earths. *Crisis on Infinite Earths* (Wolfman/Pérez, 1985–86) collapsed an unwieldy lattice into one timeline; *Infinite Crisis* (2005), *Final Crisis* (Morrison, 2008), *Flashpoint* (2011, triggering the New 52), *Convergence* (2015), *Dark Nights: Metal* (2017), *Doomsday Clock* (2017–19), and *Dark Crisis on Infinite Earths* (2022) each rebuilt or restored the lattice. Grant Morrison's *The Multiversity* (2014–15) canonized 52 numbered Earths with house-style identities — Earth-10 (Nazi-victory), Earth-23 (Black Superman), Earth-3 (evil Crime Syndicate).

**What lands.** Taxonomic specificity. Fans memorize Earth-2's Justice Society (1940s heroes aging in real time) versus Earth-Prime (the reader's Earth). Earth-numbering gives collectors a coordinate system.

**Failure mode.** Reboot exhaustion. Continuity died and was reborn so often (post-Crisis, Zero Hour 1994, New 52, Rebirth, Infinite Frontier) that "what counts" became a barrier to entry. Casual readers can't tell which Batman origin is current.

**Steal-rule.** Numbered shells with house identities. Each parallel has a one-line genre signature, not just a "what if".

## 3. Stephen King's Dark Tower

**Mechanic.** Roland's quest for the Tower (eight novels, 1982–2012) is the keystone holding King's bibliography together. *'Salem's Lot* (1975) Father Callahan reappears in *Wolves of the Calla* (2003). *Insomnia* (1994), *Hearts in Atlantis* (1999), *It* (1986), *The Stand* (1978), *Black House* (Straub co-write, 2001), and *Mr. Mercedes* all leak into the Tower's mythos. The glue word is "ka" — destiny — and the universe contains "many worlds" reachable through doors, todash space, and the Beams.

**What lands.** Discovery economy. Reading *Insomnia* and finding Crimson King references rewards loyalty without gating new readers — a *Salem's Lot* reader needs zero Tower knowledge.

**Failure mode.** Authorial self-insertion. King wrote himself into Books V–VII (*Wolves*, *Song of Susannah*, *The Dark Tower*, 2003–04); critics found it tonally fatal. The mythology consumed the storytelling.

**Steal-rule.** Tether, don't gate. Cross-references reward the obsessive but never block the casual.

## 4. Neil Gaiman's *Sandman*

**Mechanic.** *The Sandman* (Vertigo, 1989–96) anchors reality on the Endless: Dream, Death, Destiny, Destruction, Desire, Despair, Delirium — older than gods, embodied functions. The Dreaming is a place every sleeping mind visits, so every mythology is real *somewhere*: Norse, Christian, Egyptian, DC superhero, Shakespearean fiction (Issue #19, "A Midsummer Night's Dream", won the World Fantasy Award, 1991). *The Books of Magic* (1990–91) and *Lucifer* (Carey, 2000–06) extended the cosmology.

**What lands.** Functional anchors. The Endless aren't characters first — they're roles. Stories slot into "a Dream story" or "a Death story" the way a song slots into a key.

**Failure mode.** Dependency on a singular voice. Spin-offs and the Netflix adaptation (2022– ) struggle when Gaiman's prose-rhythm is absent; the architecture works, the texture doesn't reproduce.

**Steal-rule.** Cosmic functions over cosmic personalities. Anchor the multiverse on roles (Dream, Death) so any mythology can dock.

## 5. Discworld (Pratchett)

**Mechanic.** Forty-one novels (1983–2015) on a flat disc on four elephants on a turtle (Great A'Tuin). One world, but Pratchett ran it as five recursive sub-series: City Watch (Vimes), Witches (Granny Weatherwax), Death (Mort, Susan), Wizards (Rincewind, Unseen University), and Industrial Revolution (Moist von Lipwig). Each sub-genre satirizes a different real-world institution: police procedural, folk-horror, mortality, academia, capitalism.

**What lands.** Onboarding by genre, not chronology. New readers pick a sub-series matching their taste — *Guards! Guards!* (1989) for police-procedural fans, *Mort* (1987) for mythology fans — and crossover novels (*Thud!*, 2005; *Reaper Man*, 1991) reward completists.

**Failure mode.** Author-bound. After Pratchett's death (2015), the estate refused continuation; Discworld effectively closed because its voice was non-transferable. Tonal consistency was a feature, not a system.

**Steal-rule.** Sub-genre as on-ramp. Don't make the audience swallow the whole world; let them enter through one room.

## 6. *Once Upon a Time* (ABC, 2011–18)

**Mechanic.** Public-domain fairy-tale characters live in a single shared land (the Enchanted Forest). The Evil Queen's curse banishes them to "the Land Without Magic" — Storybrooke, Maine, where they have amnesia and modern jobs. Across seven seasons the show absorbed Wonderland, Neverland, Oz, Camelot, Agrabah, the Underworld, and an "Untold Stories" realm, eventually reframing the cosmology as multiple "realms" connected by portals.

**What lands.** Cross-IP collision under one rule-set. Hook meets Rumpelstiltskin meets Frankenstein because all are public-domain artifacts re-skinned to one continuity. The "happy endings" mechanic (each character has one to find/lose/protect) gave every shell a portable goal.

**Failure mode.** Sprawl outpaced rules. By Season 5 (Underworld) the cosmology was so layered that long-lapsed viewers couldn't re-enter. Ratings collapsed; ABC reset cast and timeline for S7 (2017–18) and didn't recover.

**Steal-rule.** Public-domain remixing demands a single rule that *every* character pays. "Happy endings" was that tax.

## 7. Pokémon (anime + games + cards)

**Mechanic.** Generations as soft reboots: Gen I (Kanto, 1996), Gen II (Johto, 1999), through Gen IX (Paldea, 2022). Each region contains some legacy species and some new — *species itself is the multiverse unit*. Regional variants (introduced Gen VII, *Sun/Moon*, 2016) explicitly canonize parallel-shell logic: Alolan Vulpix (Ice) versus Kanto Vulpix (Fire), Galarian Ponyta (Psychic) versus Kanto Ponyta (Fire). The TCG (Wizards 1999, Pokémon Company 2003– ) prints alternative-art versions of identical species; the anime ran 25+ seasons re-introducing species per region.

**What lands.** Species-as-shell. Pikachu remains Pikachu across every generation, but a Hisuian or Paldean variant validates the parallel-realities reading without breaking continuity.

**Failure mode.** Power-creep and Pokédex bloat. With 1,000+ species, completionism — the original tagline ("Gotta Catch 'Em All") — is abandoned. New collectors enter generation-locked.

**Steal-rule.** Variant as canon, not bonus. Regional-variant Pokémon get stat blocks, types, and flavor; they're not skins.

## 8. The Witcher → Cyberpunk 2077 (CD Projekt RED)

**Mechanic.** *The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt* (2015) canonized Sapkowski's "Conjunction of the Spheres" — a cataclysm that crashed multiple realities into the Continent, importing humans, monsters, and elves from elsewhere. Same studio's *Cyberpunk 2077* (2020) is set in Mike Pondsmith's separate IP. Easter eggs (a Witcher gwent card in 2077; a robotic horse skeleton in *Phantom Liberty*, 2023) flirt with shared reality without committing. Sapkowski's *Wiedźmin* novels (1986–2013) keep the books' continuity adjacent but distinct from the games.

**What lands.** Two universes, one studio voice. Players know they're in CDPR space — moral grey, dialogue-heavy, melancholic — without forced continuity. The Conjunction works *inside* Witcher because it explains why Slavic folk monsters and elves coexist.

**Failure mode.** *Cyberpunk 2077*'s 2020 launch bugs damaged the studio's "voice = trust" advantage — proving that universe-architecture credit is borrowed, not banked.

**Steal-rule.** Diegetic causal explanation for cross-genre collision. "Conjunction of the Spheres" lets a single in-world event license everything from werewolves to elves.

## 9. Star Wars EU → Disney Canon Split

**Mechanic.** From *Splinter of the Mind's Eye* (Foster, 1978) through Timothy Zahn's *Heir to the Empire* (1991) and the New Jedi Order (1999–2003), the Expanded Universe accreted ~400 novels, comics, and games over three decades. April 25, 2014: Lucasfilm rebranded it "Legends" and declared only the films and *The Clone Wars* canon. The new canon (Disney, 2014– ) rebuilt with films, *Rebels*, *The Mandalorian* (2019– ), *Andor* (2022– ), and a tightly licensed novel line. Some EU concepts (Thrawn, Coruscant) were reabsorbed; most (Mara Jade, Jacen Solo, the Yuuzhan Vong) remain Legends.

**What lands.** A clean reset gave Disney creative freedom and gave Legends a martyr aura. Older fans treat Legends as a "true" alternate; new fans don't have to read 400 books.

**Failure mode.** Trust damage. The 2014 decanonization felt like confiscation; "Legends" branding pacified loyalists but never reconciled them. *Rise of Skywalker* (2019) and the sequel-trilogy continuity disputes proved that purging a multiverse doesn't end debates — it relocates them.

**Steal-rule.** If you split, never erase. Brand the prior layer ("Legends") so nothing collectors bought becomes worthless.

## 10. Borges & Ted Chiang

**Mechanic.** Jorge Luis Borges, "El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan" (1941): a novel that *is* a labyrinth — every choice is taken, all branches coexist, time forks rather than flows. Ted Chiang, "Story of Your Life" (1998, basis for *Arrival* 2016): linguistic relativity collapses linear time; the protagonist experiences a life simultaneously rather than sequentially. Chiang's "Anxiety Is the Dizziness of Freedom" (in *Exhalation*, 2019) introduces the "prism" — a quantum device letting two branched selves communicate, weaponizing branching as a moral instrument: every alternate-you is real and the question becomes which version of yourself you owe.

**What lands.** Branching is meaningful only when it hurts. Chiang's prism users grieve the road not taken because the road not taken talks back.

**Failure mode.** Almost none in the literary register, because the stories are short and the rule-set is closed. The risk is dilution when imitators borrow the conceit without the moral weight (cf. weaker MCU branches).

**Steal-rule.** Branches must matter morally, not just visually. A parallel that produces no regret, loss, or dilemma is decoration.

## Cross-cutting rules

- **Shells need a fixed canon spine.** Marvel's Spider-Verse "canon events," Pokémon's species identity, and Discworld's sub-series voices all enforce: vary aesthetic, hold core. A character without a fixed beat list is a costume, not a self.
- **Cross-references reward, never gate.** The Dark Tower model (King) and Pratchett's sub-genre on-ramps prove that Easter-egg payoff for completionists must not block first-time entry. Marvel's Disney+ homework problem and DC's reboot-tax show the inverse.
- **A diegetic mechanism for collision is non-negotiable.** Witcher's Conjunction of the Spheres, Sandman's Endless, *Once Upon a Time*'s curse, and DC's Crisis events all give the multiverse an in-world cause. Multiverses without a stated mechanic feel like marketing.
- **Numbered/named taxonomy increases collector value.** Earth-23, Alolan, Earth-Prime, Sacred Timeline — fans operationalize fandom through coordinate systems. Unlabeled parallels can't be discussed, traded, or ranked.
- **Branches require moral weight.** Chiang's prism, *Spider-Verse*'s canon-event grief, and *Endgame*'s sacrifice rules all show: if a parallel is consequence-free, the universe deflates. Stakes scale with what a branch costs.
- **Author-bound worlds end with the author.** Discworld (Pratchett's voice), Sandman (Gaiman's prose), and the Dark Tower (King's metaphysics) all hit ceilings tied to a single creator. Worlds engineered to outlive their authors need transferable rule-sets, not transferable voice.
- **Never erase prior canon, rebrand it.** Star Wars Legends and DC's pre-Crisis archive show that older material survives reframing far better than deletion. A purge breaks trust; a relabel preserves it.
