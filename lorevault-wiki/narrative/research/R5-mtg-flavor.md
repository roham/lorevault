# R5 — MTG Flavor Text Anatomy

A primary-source decomposition of what makes Magic: The Gathering flavor text land. Built for LoreVault's narrative rubric.

---

## 1. Voice rules — three modes and when each is used

MTG flavor text operates in exactly three voice modes. Modern sets (Dominaria onward, 2018+) deploy them in roughly the proportions below; early sets (Alpha, Antiquities, Legends) leaned almost entirely on mode 2.

**Mode 1 — Attributed first-person quote.** The most common modern form. A named character speaks, attribution lives below the em-dash.
- *"For every living person there are generations of dead. Which realm would you rather rule?"* —Liliana Vess
- *"Of course you should fight fire with fire. You should fight everything with fire."* —Jaya Ballard, task mage (*Sizzle*, Mirage; later reprints)
- *"Someday, someone will best me. But it won't be today, and it won't be you."* —Akroma (*Akroma's Memorial / Last Word*)

Used when the card depicts a character action and the design wants to imprint a voice. ~55% of modern flavor text per the Top 20 retrospective.

**Mode 2 — Unattributed narrator (omniscient world voice).** The Alpha default. No speaker, no quotation marks. World-establishing.
- *"One bone broken for every twig snapped underfoot."* (*Llanowar Elves*, Alpha)
- *"He raged at the world, at his family, at his life. But mostly he just raged."* (*Raging Goblin*, multiple sets)

Used when the card is iconic enough that a speaker would shrink it. ~25% in modern.

**Mode 3 — In-world textual artifact.** A book excerpt, prophecy, song lyric, ritual chant, inscription, or letter. Italicized and titled.
- *"From the Necromancer's Handbook"* attributions (Mirage block).
- Tablet inscriptions on artifact lands (Urza block).
- Children's rhymes (*Phyrexian Hulk*).

Used when the card is an artifact, location, or ritual where a speaking witness would feel wrong. ~20% in modern; rising with set themes that lean on archaeology (Lost Caverns of Ixalan, March of the Machine: Aftermath).

## 2. Length discipline

**Alpha-era (1993–1995): the one-liner doctrine.** Median ~10 words; cap ~20. *Llanowar Elves'* "One bone broken for every twig snapped underfoot" is 10 words and remains the canonical model. The original templating guide allowed only two short lines.

**Tempest–Onslaught era (1997–2003): voice expansion.** Median climbed to ~18 words as named characters (Jaya, Karn, Ertai, Volrath) gained recurring quote space. Cap ~35.

**Modern era (2018–2026): controlled inflation.** Per Mark Rosewater on Blogatog (April 2024), "We are making a conscious effort to prioritize having more cards with flavor text." Median ~22 words. Hard cap ~40 words; longer than that and the card-frame readability collapses below mobile resolution (the Arena client renders at ~10pt).

**Operating bound for LoreVault: 8–30 words. Median target: 18.** Below 8, you usually have an aphorism that wants more setup. Above 30, you are writing prose, not flavor.

## 3. Character voice canon — quotes fans attribute on sight

Each canonical voice has a syntactic signature: a measurable, repeatable structural tic.

**Yawgmoth — the philosophical menace.** Syntactic signature: declarative present tense, abstract noun, moral inversion.
- *"It is not enough to live. One must matter."*
- *"Compleation is mercy."*
Pattern: short main clause + period + counter-claim. No metaphor. No first-person.

**Urza — the burdened architect.** Signature: long compound sentence, regretful past tense, plural subject.
- *"We are the sum of our choices, no matter how small."*
- Often references "the others" — Mishra, Tawnos, Xantcha — by absence rather than name.

**Karn, Silver Golem — the gentle parable-collector.** Signature: third-person fable opening, moral implied not stated.
- *"I am reminded of the fable of the silver egg. Its owner cracks it open to see what jewels it holds, only to discover a simple yellow yolk."* (Tempest)
Always begins "I am reminded of…" or "There is a story…"

**Liliana Vess — the velvet barb.** Signature: second-person address, double meaning, sexualized power.
- *"When you find yourself teetering on the edge of oblivion, mine will be the hands taking pleasure in giving you the final push."*
- *"Bodies are so much more cooperative with the soul out of the way."*
- *"A demonic pact is all fun and games until the bill comes due."*
Always "you" + a verb of consequence.

**Sorin Markov — the aristocratic exhaustion.** Signature: clinical detachment, century-scale time markers.
- *"The vampires of this world don't know the pleasures of hunger. They gorge themselves without savoring the kill."*
- *"For centuries my creation kept this world in balance. Now only her shadow remains."*

**Niv-Mizzet — the arrogant dragon-savant.** Signature: imperative or rhetorical question; never explains; mathematics or formulae as flex.
- The *Niv-Mizzet, the Firemind* (Guildpact) flavor text is a literal equation that, when rotated, spells "NIV-MIZZET = 1." The voice is that of a dragon who refuses to write English when math suffices.

**Ral Zarek — the impatient genius.** Signature: lightning metaphors, contempt for slowness.
- *"The most direct path between two points is a bolt of lightning."*

**Jaya Ballard — the firebrand humorist.** Signature: punchline structure (setup → twist), almost always about fire.
- *"Of course you should fight fire with fire. You should fight everything with fire."*

## 4. The art ↔ flavor ↔ mechanic triad — the compression rule

The rule, attributed to the Magic creative team and reinforced by Rosewater in "Add Text to Flavor" (Making Magic, 2002-03-25): **flavor text must add the third dimension that art and mechanics cannot.**

- **Art shows.** *What does it look like.*
- **Mechanic does.** *What does it do in play.*
- **Flavor means.** *Why it matters in the world / what someone in the world thinks of it.*

If art shows a knight charging and the mechanic is "first strike, +2/+0," the wrong flavor text is "A knight charges, striking first." That paraphrases the art and narrates the mechanic. Both jobs already done.

The right flavor text adds belief, witness, or consequence:
- *Glorious Anthem*: *"The trumpets' clarion call gilds even the most peasant heart."* Art is an angel raising a trumpet; mechanic is +1/+1 to all creatures. Flavor names what the buff *means* socially: it makes commoners feel noble.
- *Pacifism* (Rosewater's case study): art shows a roaring monster; mechanic prevents attack. Flavor explicitly contrasts gentleness with the visual ferocity.

**The compression rule for LoreVault:** if your flavor text would make sense without the art, rewrite it. If it would make sense without the mechanic, rewrite it. The flavor must be the *unscored note* — the harmony only audible alongside the other two.

## 5. The 10 best flavor texts in MTG history (per fan canon)

Drawn from the official Wizards "Top 20 Flavor Text" community vote (2013), CelJaded Vol 2, Cardmarket Top 20, and persistent r/magicTCG threads.

**1. *Llanowar Elves* (Alpha, 1993)** — *"One bone broken for every twig snapped underfoot."*
*Technique:* threat-by-arithmetic. The reader does the multiplication and arrives at the dread themselves. 10 words.

**2. *Sizzle* (Mirage)** — *"Of course you should fight fire with fire. You should fight everything with fire."* —Jaya Ballard, task mage
*Technique:* takes a cliché, reverses the half-thought, exposes the speaker. Voice is the entire payload.

**3. *Last Word / Akroma's Memorial* (Onslaught/Time Spiral)** — *"Someday, someone will best me. But it won't be today, and it won't be you."*
*Technique:* the boast made specific by who it dismisses (you, the player).

**4. *Hatred* (Exodus)** — *"I'll flay the skin from your bones and the bones from your soul."* —Greven il-Vec
*Technique:* the curse-ladder. Each clause makes the impossible more impossible.

**5. *Inspiration* (Onslaught)** — *"Day 31: I have succeeded in my time reversal experiment. / Day 30: I might have a problem here."*
*Technique:* the journal-entry prank — the form *is* the joke.

**6. *Manabarbs* (multiple)** — *"I don't know why people say a double-edged sword is bad. Sounds twice as good to me."* —Kamahl, pit fighter
*Technique:* literal-mindedness reveals character.

**7. *Killing Wave* (Avacyn Restored)** — *"I come looking for demons and I find a plane full of angels. I hate angels."* —Liliana Vess
*Technique:* expectation-reversal as character flag. Three sentences, three reversals.

**8. *Fodder Cannon* (Tempest)** — *"1. Procure a goblin. (Cousins are often a good source.) 2. Insert goblin into cannon. 3. Find another cousin."*
*Technique:* the manual-as-horror. Bureaucratic rhythm masking atrocity.

**9. *Brink of Disaster* (Onslaught)** — *"You always have a choice. That doesn't always mean you have a good choice."*
*Technique:* the philosophical aphorism. Two sentences, second one collapses the first.

**10. *Yawgmoth's Vile Offering* (Dominaria)** — *"It is not enough to live. One must matter."* —Yawgmoth, recorded in *The Antiquities War*
*Technique:* the villain's thesis statement, made unsettling because most readers half-agree.

## 6. Failure modes — when MTG flavor went wrong

**Over-explanation (the worst sin).** Cards from late 90s / early 2000s where flavor narrated the mechanic verbatim. *Phantom Warrior* originally read close to "A warrior who cannot be blocked." This is the failure Rosewater calls out in "Add Text to Flavor": flavor text that translates the rules box.

**Generic fantasy mush.** Any flavor text containing the words "ancient evil," "darkness rises," or "the prophecy foretold" with no specific noun. Rosewater singled this out: "If your flavor text would fit on any card in any fantasy game, it isn't doing its job." Common in mid-block filler commons.

**Pun-driven cards.** Rosewater in "The Write Stuff" (Making Magic, 2002-03-18): *"Research shows that players dislike puns."* Unfinity (2022) deliberately broke this rule for a comedy set; the response confirmed the rule — fans tolerated puns only because the entire set was framed as not-canon.

**Modern-snark drift.** Post-2018, a recurring complaint on r/magicTCG and from Rosewater himself: too many cards reading like Marvel-movie quips. Voice flattening — every character starts to sound like Jace Beleren after a Whedon rewrite. *Redirect*'s "It's actually quite simple, but since you've only recently begun to walk upright…" works because Jace is *characterized* as condescending. The same line on a generic mage flattens the world.

**The recurring-character overuse trap.** Rosewater warned in "The Write Stuff": *"Constant character focus weakens atmosphere."* When Jaya Ballard's quotes appeared on 30+ cards across Mirage and Time Spiral, by reprint #20 the voice had lost its sting. Rotate.

## 7. Mark Rosewater's writing rules

Compiled from "The Write Stuff" (2002-03-18), "Add Text to Flavor" (2002-03-25), and persistent Blogatog answers (2014–2024).

1. **A big idea in as few words as possible.** Concision is non-negotiable. Rhythm and flow matter more than completeness.
2. **Variety serves variety of player.** Lyrical, descriptive, insightful, humorous — all four belong in every set, in roughly that priority order.
3. **No puns.** Player research is unambiguous. Reserved for explicitly comedy sets (Un-sets, Unfinity).
4. **Don't paraphrase the rules.** Flavor text exists *independently* from gameplay; if you can delete the mechanic and the flavor still describes the card, you've failed.
5. **Match the color philosophy.** Red is impulsive emotion. White is moral certainty. Black is dark humor and self-interest. Blue is curiosity and condescension. Green is patience and natural law.
6. **Attribution adds authenticity.** Either to a named character or to an in-world source ("The Necromancer's Handbook," "Song of All," "Tablet of Eight").
7. **Rule of Three.** Comedy structure: setup, expectation, twist. Drama structure: claim, qualifier, reversal.
8. **Real-world quotes only in basic sets.** Expert expansions must use original Magic-world voices.
9. **Make one group happy upsets another.** Acknowledged constraint. Don't over-optimize for one demographic.
10. **The mosaic test.** Each card is one tile. Read alongside its neighbors, do the voices form a world or a noise floor?

## Voice Toolkit — 10 reusable archetypes for LoreVault

Each archetype is an inheritable structural pattern. Use the example as the calibration anchor; don't copy the words.

**1. The Warning** — second-person imperative, single concrete consequence, no metaphor.
*Example:* "Beware the unblinking eye that watches from beyond the mirror." (*Puppet's Verdict*, Mirrodin) Pattern: *"Beware [specific noun] that [specific verb] [specific place]."*

**2. The Boast** — first-person dismissal, the specific listener is the punchline.
*Example:* "Someday, someone will best me. But it won't be today, and it won't be you." Pattern: future concession + present denial + you.

**3. The Testimonial** — third-party witness, past tense, single observed detail.
*Example:* "He raged at the world, at his family, at his life. But mostly he just raged." Pattern: list of three + reduction to one.

**4. The Curse** — escalating impossibility, parallel clause structure.
*Example:* "I'll flay the skin from your bones and the bones from your soul." —Greven il-Vec. Pattern: [verb] [noun A] from [noun B] and [noun B] from [noun C].

**5. The Artifact-Reading** — italicized in-world document, partial quote.
*Example:* "...and on the eighth day, the silence was broken not by speech but by a single struck bell." —*The Antiquities War*. Pattern: ellipsis-opening, ordinal time marker, sensory detail.

**6. The Song Fragment** — two short lines with internal rhyme or assonance, ends mid-thought.
*Example:* "Steel for the hand, blood for the field / Neither the holder, neither shall yield." Pattern: trochaic foot, slant rhyme.

**7. The Philosophical Aphorism** — two sentences; second collapses first.
*Example:* "You always have a choice. That doesn't always mean you have a good choice." Pattern: claim → qualifier that retracts the comfort.

**8. The Villain's Thesis** — declarative present tense, abstract moral inversion.
*Example:* "It is not enough to live. One must matter." —Yawgmoth. Pattern: "It is not enough to [common good]. One must [uncommon ambition]."

**9. The Manual / Procedure** — numbered or imperative steps; horror in the bureaucratic.
*Example:* "1. Procure a goblin. 2. Insert goblin into cannon. 3. Find another cousin." Pattern: neutral verbs + escalating moral stakes the form refuses to acknowledge.

**10. The Reversal Joke** — three beats; expectation, expectation, snap.
*Example:* "I come looking for demons and I find a plane full of angels. I hate angels." —Liliana Vess. Pattern: setup, complication, deadpan declaration.

Use no more than three of any single archetype across a set of 60 cards. The mosaic test (Rule 10) demands it.
