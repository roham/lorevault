# R6 — IP Brand Management: How Multi-Year Franchises Sustain Continuity

**Scope:** Operational discipline, not storytelling craft. Who decides what, how often they ship, how they fence off non-canon, and how each system has actually broken.

---

## 1. Wizards of the Coast / Magic: The Gathering

**(a) Team structure.** Run out of WotC's Renton, WA studio. The "Magic Story" team is a sub-unit of the broader Creative Studio. Reporting line: VP of Franchise/Studio (Aaron Forsythe on design; Jess Lanzillo as VP of Magic) → Head of Worldbuilding (Doug Beyer historically; Emily Teng on narrative leads). Below that: per-set **Worldbuilding Lead**, **Set Creative Lead**, **Art Director** (Cynthia Sheppard, Ovidio Cartagena rotate), and a contracted writers' bench. Each plane (Ravnica, Innistrad, Kaladesh, Eldraine) has its own **World Guide / Style Guide** — a living internal bible locking visual rules, costume codes, language, magic system, demographics. Nothing ships without art-director sign-off against the guide.

**(b) Cadence.** Four "Premier" sets per year (the Standard cycle), plus 2–4 supplemental products (Commander, Modern Horizons, Universes Beyond). Each set: ~250–300 cards, ~50K–100K words of story across web fiction, flavor text, novellas. A canon increment every ~13 weeks for 30+ years (1993 → present). 2-year development pipeline; story locks ~12 months out.

**(c) Safety valves.** "Universes Beyond" (LotR, Doctor Who, Final Fantasy, Marvel) is explicitly **non-canon to the Multiverse story** — marketing vessel, not story vessel. "Un-sets" (Unfinity, Unstable) are silver-bordered and outside continuity. Commander precons revisit planes without advancing the spine. Three-tier (canon / adjacent / silly) lets WotC ship volume without polluting the main arc.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Continuity collapse via roster bloat.* By 2018 the Gatewatch (5 named planeswalker protagonists) was unsustainable; "War of the Spark" (2019) force-killed core characters to reset complexity. Lesson: a permanent recurring protagonist roster scales poorly under quarterly cadence.

## 2. The Pokémon Company

**(a) Team structure.** **The Pokémon Company (TPC)** is the JV (Nintendo + Game Freak + Creatures Inc., founded 1998) created *specifically* to enforce franchise continuity across game / anime / TCG / movie / merch. Tsunekazu Ishihara has been President/CEO since founding. TPC owns the **Pokémon Franchise Bible** controlling species design, evolutionary rules, regional taxonomy, and canonical look across mediums. Game Freak invents new Pokémon and core lore; OLM, Inc. produces the anime under TPC oversight; Creatures Inc. produces the TCG; TPC International (TPCi) handles Western localization.

**(b) Cadence.** One new **Generation** every ~3 years (Gen 1 = 1996, Gen 9 = 2022). Each Generation: a new **Region** (Kanto, Johto, Hoenn, Sinnoh, Unova, Kalos, Alola, Galar, Paldea), ~80–100 new species, refresh of TCG/anime in lockstep. TCG ships ~4 expansion sets/year; the anime aired weekly 1997–2023. The most metronomic franchise pipeline in entertainment.

**(c) Safety valves.** Spin-off games (Mystery Dungeon, Snap, GO) live in **adjacent universes**. Anime continuities have been reset (Ash → Liko/Roy, 2023). The TCG itself is treated as **mechanical, not narrative** — flavor text is Pokédex-style, not arc-driven, which protects it from continuity collisions.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Quality erosion via cadence rigidity.* Sword/Shield (2019) and Scarlet/Violet (2022) shipped on schedule but visibly under-finished — Generation cadence locked to retail windows with no slip valve. When Game Freak can't ship, TCG and anime are calendar-coupled and can't pause. Cadence enforcement = cadence imprisonment.

## 3. Marvel Studios under Kevin Feige

**(a) Team structure.** Feige = President, Marvel Studios; reports directly to Bob Iger post-2015 (extracted from Ike Perlmutter's Marvel Entertainment reporting line). The continuity body is the **Marvel Studios Parliament** (informal): Feige + Louis D'Esposito (co-president), Victoria Alonso (former EVP of production, departed 2023), Brad Winderbaum (TV), Nate Moore (features), Stephen Broussard. This group greenlights every script, casting, and inter-film callback. There is no whole-MCU writers' room — writers' rooms exist per project; the parliament owns the through-line. The **Sacred Timeline** (introduced in *Loki* S1, 2021) is partly an in-universe metaphor for this body.

**(b) Cadence.** Phase 1 (2008–2012, 6 films), Phase 2 (2013–2015, 6), Phase 3 (2016–2019, 11), Phase 4 (2021–2022, 7 films + 7 Disney+ series), Phase 5 (2023–2025), Phase 6 ongoing. Pre-COVID rhythm: 3 films/year, ~$200M+ budgets. Post-Disney+ (2019): +3–4 streaming series/year — which broke the cadence.

**(c) Safety valves.** "What If…?" is explicitly multiverse/non-canon. *Deadpool & Wolverine* uses the TVA frame to import Fox-era characters without disrupting the main timeline. The **Multiverse** concept itself is a structural escape hatch. Pre-2019 Marvel Television (Agents of SHIELD, Netflix Defenders) was ring-fenced as "loosely connected" — Feige refused to let TV plots constrain films.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Volume collapse of a single canon.* Phase 4 over-extended: 7 series + 7 films in 24 months created prerequisite-viewing problems audiences rejected. *The Marvels* did $206M global on a ~$220M budget. Feige publicly admitted in 2024 that Disney+ volume diluted theatrical. Phase 5/6 has been re-tightened. Lesson: continuity attention is a finite per-audience budget; exceeding it produces revolt.

## 4. Lucasfilm Story Group (post-Disney)

**(a) Team structure.** The **Lucasfilm Story Group (LFSG)** was created in 2013 after Disney's $4.05B Lucasfilm acquisition (2012). ~6–10 members; founding leads included Pablo Hidalgo, Leland Chee (the "Holocron" database keeper since 2000), Kiri Hart, Rayne Roberts, Matt Martin. Sits under Kathleen Kennedy. Reviews every script (films, series, novels, comics, games) for canon alignment. April 2014: LFSG declared the entire **Expanded Universe** (300+ novels, 1991–2014) to be "Legends" — non-canon — to clear runway for the sequel trilogy.

**(b) Cadence.** Sequel trilogy 2015/2017/2019; anthologies 2016/2018; theatrical cadence collapsed after 2019. Disney+ era: ~2 series/year (Mandalorian, Boba Fett, Obi-Wan, Andor, Ahsoka, Acolyte). High Republic publishing initiative (2021) ships cross-medium novels/comics quarterly. Multiple film projects announced and cancelled.

**(c) Safety valves.** "Legends" is the entire pre-2014 canon as a parking lot. *Star Wars Visions* (anime anthologies) is explicitly non-canon. LEGO Star Wars is a tonal sandbox. The **High Republic** is a temporal sandbox set ~200 years before the Skywalker Saga, structurally insulated from the films.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Authority without conviction.* Post-Last-Jedi backlash (2017), Lucasfilm leadership has been visibly inconsistent in defending canonical decisions. Patty Jenkins's *Rogue Squadron*, Rian Johnson's trilogy, Taika Waititi's film — all announced and abandoned. The body that *decides* canon has had its decisions overridden by box-office anxiety. Lesson: a canon committee is only as strong as the executive willing to back its calls in public.

## 5. Wizarding World / Pottermore / Wizarding World Digital

**(a) Team structure.** Originally Rowling + agent Neil Blair + Bloomsbury/Scholastic editorial. Pottermore launched 2011 as Rowling's direct-to-fan platform (rebranded Wizarding World 2019, then consolidated under Warner Bros. Discovery). Editorial control has remained with Rowling personally for any new lore — **there is no canon committee**. Heyday Films (David Heyman) produces films; Warner Bros. owns film rights. Rowling's tweets and Pottermore essays became canon by fiat, not by review.

**(b) Cadence.** Books: 7 novels 1997–2007. Films: 8 films 2001–2011, then 3 *Fantastic Beasts* prequels (2016/2018/2022). Cursed Child stage play 2016. Pottermore essays: irregular Rowling posts. Main-line content effectively halted post-2022; *Fantastic Beasts* 4 and 5 are on indefinite hiatus. HBO TV reboot announced 2023.

**(c) Safety valves.** Cursed Child is ambiguously canon ("the eighth story"). Pottermore essays were quietly walked back when controversial. Hogwarts Legacy (2023, Avalanche Software) was ring-fenced as an 1890s-set spin-off — temporal sandbox to avoid continuity collisions.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Single-author bottleneck plus author-as-liability.* Rowling's continued personal authorship of all canon means every retroactive addition (Dumbledore's sexuality, blood-type rules, North American magical history) generated controversy without a buffering body. Combined with Rowling's public political stances (2019 onward), brand and creator became inseparable, with no committee able to operate independently. Lesson: a franchise that never built a canon committee cannot decouple from its founder.

## 6. DC's Multiverse Curators (Geoff Johns Era)

**(a) Team structure.** DC has historically assigned a **Chief Creative Officer** or **Editor-in-Chief** for continuity. Geoff Johns served as CCO of DC Entertainment 2010–2018, after a long writing run including *Infinite Crisis* (2005–06), *Final Crisis* (2008–09), *Flashpoint* (2011), and architecting the New 52 reboot. Below the CCO: **Group Editors** per imprint (Batman, Superman, Justice League) holding day-to-day continuity authority. No formal "story group" — continuity is managed via editor-in-chief power and periodic line-wide events.

**(b) Cadence.** ~5–6 monthly issues × 30+ active titles = ~150 issues/month of canon output. Major **line-wide reset events** every 5–10 years: *Crisis on Infinite Earths* (1985), *Zero Hour* (1994), *Infinite Crisis* (2005), *Flashpoint* (2011 → New 52), *DC Rebirth* (2016), *Dark Crisis* (2022). Each is a continuity-cleanup operation disguised as a story.

**(c) Safety valves.** The **Multiverse** itself (52 numbered Earths since 2006) is the structural valve — inconvenient stories park on Earth-3, Earth-X, etc. *Elseworlds* (1989–2010) and *Black Label* (2018–) are explicit out-of-continuity prestige lines. DCAU is a separate continuity. The most explicit structural use of "official non-canon" of any major franchise.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Reset addiction.* Reboots generate sales spikes but each one alienates long-time readers. By the New 52 (2011) cumulative resets had created the "I gave up tracking what's canon" problem at scale; DC Rebirth (2016) was an explicit apology for it. The safety valve became the failure mode — every reset is a debt instrument that compounds.

## 7. CD Projekt Red — Witcher & Cyberpunk

**(a) Team structure.** Narrative authority sits with the **Senior Narrative Director / Story Director** per franchise. Witcher: Marcin Blacha (Story Director); historically Sebastian Stępień (Creative Director on W3). Cyberpunk: Igor Sarzyński (Lead Quest Designer), with Mike Pondsmith (creator of the 1988 *Cyberpunk* TTRPG) maintaining creator-veto consultation rights via contract. Critically, CDPR licenses *Witcher* IP from Andrzej Sapkowski under a 1997/2002 buyout — Sapkowski is **not** a story-group member and has stated he does not consider the games canon to his books.

**(b) Cadence.** Witcher: 3 main games (2007, 2011, 2015) + DLC + Witcher 4 in development for 2025+. Cyberpunk 2077 (2020), Phantom Liberty expansion (2023), sequel ("Project Orion") in pre-production. Netflix *Witcher* 2019–present (4 seasons), animated *Nightmare of the Wolf* (2021), *Cyberpunk: Edgerunners* (2022). Cadence: ~5–7 years per major game; ~1–2 transmedia adjacent products/year.

**(c) Safety valves.** Games and books are **separate continuities** sharing characters and lore — Geralt's game arc is post-books in CDPR's framing but Sapkowski rejects this. Netflix series is yet a third continuity. *Edgerunners* is canon to Cyberpunk 2077 and shipped to deliberately reframe the game's reputation post-launch. Each medium runs its own continuity ledger.

**(d) Failure mode.** *Launch quality vs. multi-medium narrative ambition.* Cyberpunk 2077's catastrophic 2020 launch (removed from PSN, refunds offered) showed even a strong narrative IP can be blown by execution debt — the company tried to ship across game/anime/Netflix while the base game wasn't done. Lesson: a multi-medium narrative strategy doesn't survive a foundation product that doesn't ship clean.

## Operating Model for LoreVault

**Closest analog: Pokémon Company organizational structure (small canonical authority, distributed production), running a Magic-style quarterly main-line cadence, with DC's Multiverse safety-valve baked in from drop one.**

**1. Adopt the TPC structure, not the WotC structure.** TPC's genius was creating a small body whose *only job* is to enforce the bible across all production partners. WotC requires a 50-person internal Creative Studio. LoreVault, with a small team and AI-augmented production, cannot afford a Studio-scale group. It can afford a 2–3 person **Canon Council** whose only role is review/approve/reject against a written bible — and a network of AI-augmented producers (writers, artists, drop designers) operating against that bible without negotiating it. Authority concentrated, production distributed.

**2. Build the Bible before the second drop.** WotC's per-plane World Guide and TPC's Pokémon Bible are both built **before** content scales. LoreVault's bible should lock: cosmology rules, named-character canon, visual/aesthetic language per region or arc, technology/magic constraints, and a "what we will never do" list. Without it, AI-augmented production amplifies inconsistency at machine speed — the failure mode is *not* AI hallucination, it's AI fluency producing 10× more contradictions than a human team can audit.

**3. Quarterly main-line cadence; everything else is sandbox.** Magic ships 4 Premier sets/year — the upper bound for "canon increments" a small team can defend. Pick **one canonical drop per quarter** that advances the spine. Everything else — collaborations, special editions, holiday drops, partner IPs — ships as **explicitly non-canon** with a different visual or naming convention. Universes Beyond was a $200M+ success precisely because it didn't pretend to be canon. The drop tier system *is* the safety valve.

**4. Pre-build the multiverse escape hatch.** DC's lesson: every long-running franchise eventually needs to retcon. Bake the multiverse / parallel-arc structure in from drop 1 — not because you'll use it immediately, but because the only thing more expensive than an escape hatch you didn't use is one you have to retrofit after a continuity break.

**5. Founder must not be the canon body.** Rowling's failure is the most relevant warning: a small team where the founder is also the canon authority *cannot scale and cannot decouple from founder risk*. The Canon Council should be empowered to override the founder on canon questions from day one, in writing. Single highest-leverage operational decision.

**6. Cadence discipline beats volume.** Marvel's Phase 4 collapse and Pokémon's Sword/Shield erosion are the same lesson from opposite directions: ship less, ship clean, defend the schedule. The canonical drop ships on its date even if scope shrinks — sandbox tier absorbs the overflow, never the spine.

**Net:** TPC's organizational model + WotC's tiered cadence + DC's structural humility (multiverse from day one). Avoid the Lucasfilm and Wizarding World patterns entirely — both prove that canon authority without an empowered, durable committee is a brand timebomb.
