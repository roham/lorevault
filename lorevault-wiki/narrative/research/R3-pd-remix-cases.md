# R3 — Public-Domain Remix Casework

Nine PD-character remixes: what shipped, commercial outcome, structural insight for LoreVault.

---

## 1. ABC's Once Upon a Time (2011-2018)

**(a) Conceit.** Disney-fairytale figures trapped in contemporary Maine, memories wiped. Storybrooke frame toggles flashback fantasy / present-day melodrama each episode. ABC and Disney shared a parent — the show could lift Disney's *visual* canon (Maleficent's silhouette, Belle's gown) on top of underlying PD Grimm/Perrault/Barrie text.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** Pilot 12.93M viewers, second-best fall drama debut of 2011. S1 averaged ~11.8M live; S7 ~3.2M before cancellation. 156 episodes / 7 seasons. Spin-off *Wonderland* (2013-14) cancelled after 13 episodes.

**(c) What worked.** Two engines: the *curse-breaking* mystery (S1) and the *character-pairing recombinator* — every season, two figures who never met in source text are forced to interact. Disney visual layer gave casual viewers free shorthand.

**(d) What failed.** Bloat. By S4 the show had Frozen, Brave, Oz, Camelot, Hades, Jafar competing for arc real estate. Each new IP-injection paid a ratings bump and a coherence cost. S7 reboot halved the audience.

**(e) Lesson.** Cross-IP collision is the engine, but the multiverse needs a *meta-rule* constraining who can meet whom. Lock cross-pollination physics *before* stacking IPs.

## 2. BBC Sherlock (2010-2017)

**(a) Conceit.** Moffat/Gatiss yanked Holmes into 2010s London — smartphones, NHS politics, on-screen text overlays. Faithful to Conan Doyle's *plotting logic* (Study in Pink = Study in Scarlet beat-for-beat), unfaithful to setting. 90-minute episodes as mini-films.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** S1 finale 9.2M UK viewers. S3 premiere 12.7M consolidated — BBC's most-watched drama since 2001. Sold to 200+ territories. S4 IMDb 7.9 vs S1's 8.9. RT: S1-S2 ~95%+, S4 ~58%.

**(c) What worked.** The *recipe*: source plot logic + present-day texture + second-screen visual language (mind palace, on-screen text). Made the audience feel smart for spotting Doyle references without requiring it.

**(d) What failed.** Once Moriarty died (S2), the show kept escalating supervillains (Magnussen, Eurus, secret-Holmes-sister) toward parody. "High-functioning sociopath" ate the mystery format. Three-year gaps broke audience habit.

**(e) Lesson.** The *interpretive frame* (Holmes-as-sociopath + mind-palace visual) is the load-bearing IP, not the character name. Once locked, escalation pressure breaks it. Build escalation ceilings into lore, not just season arcs.

## 3. CBS Elementary (2012-2019)

**(a) Conceit.** Recovering-addict Holmes in NYC, female Watson (Lucy Liu) as sober companion, Jonny Lee Miller as tattooed Sherlock. Procedural CBS format (24 eps/season) instead of BBC's three-films-per-series.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** Pilot 13.4M; 154 episodes / 7 seasons — *more total Holmes-Watson cases than any TV show ever, including 1980s Granada Brett.* Average ~7M live early, declining to ~3M. Metacritic 65 for S1, ~55-60 thereafter.

**(c) What worked.** Liu-Watson *partnership equality* — she becomes a detective in her own right. Removed the homoerotic-subtext baggage. Procedural cadence let viewers drop in cold.

**(d) What failed.** No cultural footprint. No memes, no cosplay, no SDCC panels by S4. Procedural format suppressed mythology-density.

**(e) Lesson.** *Volume without density is invisible.* CBS shipped 154 hours of competent Holmes and produced no canon-level fan attachment. Mythology density — the thing fans collect, theory-craft — is the actual collectible. Fungible procedural content is an annuity, not a property.

## 4. Penny Dreadful (Showtime, 2014-2016)

**(a) Conceit.** Victorian-horror PD ensemble: Frankenstein, Creature, Dorian Gray, Van Helsing, Jekyll, Wolf Man — plus an original protagonist (Eva Green's Vanessa Ives) as connective tissue. *LXG* logic as prestige cable horror.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** Premiere 1.05M same-day. ~0.8M live average; Showtime claimed ~5M cumulative per episode by S2. 27 episodes / 3 seasons. Cancelled by *creator choice*. Reboot *City of Angels* (2020) cancelled after one season. RT: S1 80%, S2 96%, S3 89%.

**(c) What worked.** Eva Green anchoring a borrowed cast. Every PD character *recosted* into a single tonal palette. Treated PD as raw material, not fan-service.

**(d) What failed.** Under-watched. S3's abrupt ending (Vanessa's death) felt unearned. *City of Angels* stripped the original cast and PD-ensemble conceit — and died, proving the ensemble was the product.

**(e) Lesson.** An **original anchor character** inside the PD ensemble does more work than the IPs. Borrowed names attract; the original keeps people. Without the anchor, the multiverse is a museum gift shop.

## 5. The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen (Alan Moore, 1999-2019; film 2003)

**(a) Conceit.** Comic: Mina Murray, Allan Quatermain, Captain Nemo, Hyde, Hawley Griffin as Victorian Avengers. Moore exploded outward — *Black Dossier* and *Century* annotated hundreds of PD characters into one timeline. Film: 2003 Sean Connery vehicle that bowdlerised the conceit.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** Comic: ABC/DC, 1999-2019. *Volume 1* won Bram Stoker and Eisner; consistent Top-100 GN sales for two decades. Film: $179M global on $78M budget, RT 17% / Metacritic 30. Ended Connery's career; killed franchise plans. 2017 Fox TV reboot scrapped pre-pilot.

**(c) What worked (comic).** Moore's *annotated multiverse* doctrine: every panel sourced to a published Victorian text. Jess Nevins's annotation guides became companion volumes. Layered fandom — casual readers got the team-up, scholars got citations.

**(d) What failed (film).** Stripped literary specificity, added Tom Sawyer for US market, broke Mina's competence, leaned on CGI. Inverse of the comic's decision-tree.

**(e) Lesson.** *Annotation density is a feature, not a bug.* If a PD-collectibles product surfaces the source text — every card cites its origin — it converts content into a literary game. Stripping specificity kills it.

## 6. Wicked (Maguire 1995 novel; 2003 musical; 2024-2025 films)

**(a) Conceit.** *Single-character revisionism.* Take one PD character (Wicked Witch of the West, PD since 1956) and rewrite her as protagonist. Antagonists are undertold and over-marketable.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** Novel: 4M+ copies. Broadway musical opened 2003, $1.6B+ Broadway gross by 2024 — second-highest-grossing musical ever, behind Lion King. Global musical gross: $5B+ across Broadway/West End/tours. *Wicked* (2024) film: $756M global on $150M budget. Among the most lucrative single-character PD remixes ever.

**(c) What worked.** Sympathetic-villain frame is a cheat code: licenses invention (source never explained Elphaba's interior life) without contradicting canon. "Defying Gravity" extracted IP velocity by becoming an emancipation anthem detached from Oz.

**(d) What failed.** Maguire sequels (*Son of a Witch*, *Lion Among Men*, *Out of Oz*) sold ~10% of the original. Readers wanted Elphaba, not Oz.

**(e) Lesson.** **Sympathetic villainy beats heroic loyalty as a value-add layer.** Wicked Witch and Maleficent (Disney 2014, $758M) are the two most successful PD-character pivots ever — same move. For LoreVault: the antagonist register inside each PD universe (Moriarty, Mordred, Iago, Wolf, Stepmother) is undermonetised IP territory.

## 7. Castlevania (Netflix, Adi Shankar / Warren Ellis, 2017-2021)

**(a) Conceit.** Adapt a Konami game whose source is a Stoker derivative + PD Vlad/Dracula tradition. Ellis wrote it as Romanian-history-flavoured prestige anime — political theology with vampires. *Nocturne* (2023-2025) extended into the French Revolution.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** S1 (2017) #1 Netflix top-10 in 19 countries; S4 (2021) global top-10 in 50+ countries. RT 91-100% across all four seasons. *Nocturne* S1: 19.6M views in launch week. One of the only "good" video-game adaptations until *Arcane*.

**(c) What worked.** Ellis treated Dracula as a *grieving widower* (Lisa Tepes's burning kicks the whole series). Re-anchored horror IP on emotional logic. PD-Dracula skeleton gave him license without lawyers; Konami licensing handled game-canon.

**(d) What failed.** Ellis fired in 2020 over conduct allegations — *creator-risk* failure. *Nocturne* required full creative-team handoff; S1 visibly struggled to find the new voice.

**(e) Lesson.** Animated long-form is an underrated *retention* surface for PD properties — production economics are forgiving. *One creator's voice is your single point of failure.* Diversify authorship early.

## 8. Bridgerton (Shondaland / Netflix, 2020-present)

**(a) Conceit.** Julia Quinn's Regency romances adapted with alt-history aesthetic — colourblind casting, modern pop covers as string-quartet arrangements, Black Queen Charlotte. Not strictly PD-character, but *Regency genre conventions* are public domain (Austen's rules, ballroom etiquette, marriage-market) and the show treats those conventions as the actual IP.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** S1 (Dec 2020): 82M households in 28 days — Netflix's most-watched series ever at the time. S2: 627M hours / 28 days. S3: 91.7M views, top-10 in 92 countries. *Queen Charlotte* (2023): 503M hours / 28 days. Netflix cited Bridgerton-attributed signups in 2021 shareholder letters.

**(c) What worked.** Treated Regency *aesthetic* as the asset, not the source novels. Lady Whistledown framing worked like reality-TV confessional. *Less* a literary adaptation, *more* a genre-revival platform.

**(d) What failed.** S3 (RT 84% audience, Metacritic 67 vs S1's 76) showed formula wear by book three. *Queen Charlotte* was the highest-rated entry by critics — suggesting the core show is the weakest version of the format.

**(e) Lesson.** *The genre is the IP, not the characters.* Lock a visual + tonal register and characters become interchangeable. Bridgerton's Regency = LoreVault's potential "Victorian gaslight" or "Grimm forest" *aesthetic universes* — players buy into world-tone first, collect characters within it.

## 9. Sandman (Netflix, 2022-2025)

**(a) Conceit.** Adapt Gaiman's 1989-1996 DC/Vertigo comic — itself a multiverse layering Greek myth, Shakespeare, biblical apocrypha, fairy tales (Cain and Abel, Lucifer Morningstar, Fates, Norse gods) under an original character (Dream). Netflix live-action, 11+5 episodes / two seasons, ended 2025.

**(b) Commercial outcome.** S1 (Aug 2022): 198M hours / 69M households in 28 days, top-10 in 89 countries. RT 87%. S2 (2025) launched amid backlash from Gaiman sexual-assault allegations (mid-2024); reviews mixed (~RT 75%); Netflix announced pre-release that S2 would be the final season. Closed at two seasons against a six-season plan.

**(c) What worked.** Gaiman-as-showrunner kept tonal fidelity — "Sound of Her Wings" and "24/7" were near-direct translations. PD-mythology layer gave casting flexibility without continuity cost.

**(d) What failed.** *Creator-as-property risk again.* Allegations made promotion impossible. Earlier failed Sandman adaptations (1991-2010s) failed for opposite reasons: too literal, too expensive, or strip-mined for action when the source is a tonal mood piece.

**(e) Lesson.** Tone is the moat — Sandman's PD-myth layer would not work in any other register. *Separate the IP from the author.* Castlevania + Sandman together: a single creator's reputation can void a property overnight. Lore must be ownable by the *property*, not a person.

## Failure-Mode Taxonomy

Six recurring failure modes across the nine cases. Most failures combine two or more.

**1. Bloat / Cross-IP Inflation.** *Once Upon a Time, late-Sherlock.* Each IP injection pays a short-term attention bump and a long-term coherence tax. Without a meta-rule limiting who can meet whom, the multiverse collapses into soup. Symptom: audience can't summarise the show in one sentence by S4. Fix: lock cross-pollination physics before adding the third universe.

**2. Reverence / Source-Worship.** *2003 LXG film, most "literal" Sherlocks, Disney's 2020 Mulan.* Treats PD source as sacred; refuses to interpret. Result: a competent but inert museum exhibit. Audience asks "why does this exist instead of the original?" Fix: every adaptation needs an explicit interpretive thesis — Holmes is a sociopath, Dracula is a grieving widower, Elphaba is a civil-rights heroine.

**3. Cynicism / Knowing-Wink Density.** *Late Penny Dreadful, City of Angels, parts of Cruella.* Treats source as punchline. Wink-to-camera frequency exceeds sincerity budget; audience can't invest in characters being mocked. Fix: pick *one* meta-frame (camp / sincere / satire) and hold it.

**4. Procedural Anonymity / Density Failure.** *Elementary.* Volume without mythology density. Episodes interchangeable; no fan can defend a "best episode." Property never accumulates the references that breed cosplay, theory-craft, collectibles fandom. Fix: every shipped unit must add to a checkable mythology stack — episodes / cards / chapters need *canon-position*, not just chronology.

**5. Creator-Risk.** *Castlevania (Ellis), Sandman (Gaiman), Whedon's late-2010s collapse.* Property's tone is identified with one author; reputation crash voids the property. Acute when showrunner is also public face. Fix: house-style lore bibles + multi-author rotation from year one. Property must be ownable absent any single name.

**6. Joke-Density Mismatch / Tonal Drift.** *Once Upon a Time S5+, mid-Sherlock, MCU phase 4.* Tonal register drifts faster than audience expectations. A property that started as romantic fantasy reads as broad comedy by S4. Symptom: "it doesn't feel like the show anymore." Fix: codify joke-to-stakes ratio in the bible; treat drift as defect, not evolution.

Seventh, lower-frequency: **legal/IP overlap collisions** — when "public domain" turns out to have studio-owned aspects (Disney's Pooh redesign, Conan-Doyle estate's late Holmes lawsuits). Less common since the 2024 Mickey-Mouse PD precedent, but worth a clearance pass per character.
