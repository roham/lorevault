## WHY

The August 27 – September 2 arc covers the period when Jonathan is alone in Exeter and the Whitby horror is silently underway. Mina is at Whitby. Lucy is visibly ill. The Count is at Carfax. None of this is available to Jonathan. He is noting the train departure time, distinguishing kinds of silence, counting fourteen days, reviewing the first ordinary month.

The September 2 entry is the arc's fulcrum: "Lucy is not well." Mina's letter names the symptom — sleeping badly, pale, Dr. Seward attending — and Jonathan notes it accurately and adds nothing further. He has never met Lucy Westenra. He does not know what it means that she is pale and sleeping badly in a clifftop churchyard town where the sea air is supposed to help.

The reader knows. Jonathan does not. The solicitor files the information beside the postcard that said Lucy looked well on August 29. Three days and something has changed. He sets the letter beside the postcard and writes his reply.

The counting habit entries (August 30, September 1) develop the post-Castle arc of the habit itself: it has lost its original function (counting toward a self-set terminus) and persists without that function. Jonathan notices this and keeps counting anyway. "I am still counting. This is worth knowing" (August 30). By September 1 he calculates 41 days since leaving — "he had not calculated this far forward from inside" — and the count functions as recalibration rather than survival.
