# R3 — Onboarding Journey: First 12 Seconds to First $9

**Author:** R3 — Onboarding Journey Designer

**Companions:** `G1-gap-synthesis.md`, `A3-ux-surface.md`, `A4-market-intel.md`, `MULTIVERSE-COSMOLOGY-DOCTRINE.md`, `EXEMPLAR-CARDS.md`, `TOPSY-REGISTER.md`.
**Thesis:** The lore is the onboarding. Every surface in the first ninety seconds either leaves Lampblack on the visitor or the visitor leaves. No tutorials. No pop-ups. No wallet. The product teaches the Lattice by showing one Pane clearly enough that the visitor feels a second one waiting.

**Desktop (1440×900).** Same payload, but the card sits right-of-centre at native 2:3, the pullquote to its left in a 4-column serif block, a single thin gold rule separating. No emoji world-picker. No bottom nav at all until after the first pack opens. No wallet prompt, ever. No countdown timer. No leaderboard. No market stat. No daily-streak strip. A single surface, one Moment, one voice.

**The Pratchett-tier line** — the line you want to screenshot, the one that makes a BookTok user pause before scrolling — lives below the third Moment, in small italic grey: *"The cane in the corner of the frame is not the one Watson carried home."* That is the landing page's Echo. It answers no question. It opens one.

## 2. The 90-Second Test — From Hand-on-Back-Button to Curious

The hostile user is a 28-year-old BookTok reader who clicked through from a friend's Tumblr re-blog. They have never heard of LoreVault. They assume it is another NFT grift. Their thumb is on the back gesture. The test is: what happens at t=0, t=15, t=45, t=75, t=90 that converts them from suspicion to *"I need to see what's under this."*

**t=0 — Land.** The Watson-Holmes Moment fills the screen. No header chrome. The pullquote appears one second after load, fading in from 70% opacity. The tab title reads simply **LoreVault — 221B**. Not "LoreVault — Home." They are not home. They are in a room.

**t=0–15 — The Pane claims them.** Scroll delivers the Persephone frame. The line *"She is here. And there. And there."* is the first moment the Lattice teaches itself without naming itself. The word "shell" has not been spoken. The concept has been *shown*: one figure, multiple places, same pattern. This is the LATTICE in-practice, arrived at before the word exists.

**t=15–45 — The Echo strikes.** Scroll again and the screen reveals a third card: the Cheshire Cat DREAM-ARCANA, the grin held while the body dissolves. The line underneath: **"The grin arrived before the body. It is still here."** No explanation of ARCANA-parallel. No legend. No icon glossary. The visitor is now three Panes into something they cannot name, and the product has not once asked them to name it. Below the three frames: a small link — **Why these three?** — which expands inline to a 40-word reveal: *"Five Panes hold the Lattice. You have seen three. A figure is the same across all of them, the way a song is the same in two languages. What survives the translation is called **Lampblack**."* That is it. That is the doctrine delivery. One word introduced. No glossary. No diagram.

**t=45–75 — The offer.** The button, which has been pinned to the bottom of the viewport since t=0, now reads **Open the first Pane — a Sample pack, on us**. Tap it. No sign-up wall. No wallet. An email field appears inline, single input, with the placeholder *"where the Pane should send its letter."* (This is the Pratchett-grade register arriving for the first time — not joke, not pun, register.) They type. They do not create a password. They do not sign a Terms-of-Service scroll. A magic link is sent. They tap through from their inbox on the same device. Twelve seconds from email entry to pack in hand.

**t=75–90 — The reveal primes.** The screen cuts to black. The Footnoter's voice — text only, serif, centred — appears one line at a time:

> *The Lattice is not a place you go.*
> *It is a place that remembered you were coming.*
> *Shall we begin.*

No question mark. The music fades in under the third line. The visitor is now 90 seconds in, they have learned that (a) there are five Panes, (b) figures exist in multiple Panes, (c) Lampblack is what proves it, (d) there is a narrator who is dry and slightly sincere, (e) they have a pack waiting. The word *NFT* has not been used. The word *wallet* has not been used. The phrase *pull probability* has not been used. They have not seen a stat tile or a leaderboard.

**How LATTICE is taught without lecturing.** By triplication. Three Moments of the same type of thing (three cards, three Panes, three figures) demonstrate the grammar of the multiverse the same way a child learns plural: see three, infer the pattern. The *word* Lattice enters only once — in the small-print Echo-reveal — after the thing it names has already been felt. Every piece of doctrine language (Lampblack, Pane, Spine, Echo) enters this way, downstream: the phenomenon first, the word second, the definition never. This is R2-per-doctrine §1: the world precedes the story. The visitor learns it the way a child learns weather.

## 3. Sample-Pack Reveal — 12–30 Seconds, Frame by Frame

This is the emotional conversion moment. It must function at 375px width, held in one hand, thumb ready. The Courtyard "pack rip" animation is the steal target (A4 §3) — but stripped of every wallet signal. The music register is single-instrument: a cello held on an open string, then a second note added. No synth. No percussion. No fanfare. This is a *letter being opened*, not a chest.

**Frame 1 (0.0–2.0s) — The envelope.** Full-bleed black. An envelope appears centre-screen, parchment-cream, sealed with a lampblack-pigment wax stamp showing the Lattice glyph (five small circles arranged around one larger). A single serif line fades in above: **A letter from the Pane.** No counter. No "swipe to open." The user taps once — anywhere.

**Frame 2 (2.0–4.5s) — The seal breaks.** The wax cracks. The crack-sound is a single low cello pluck. The envelope opens slowly; a warm amber light spills from inside. Haptic: one soft tick on tap, one longer tick on seal-break. The Footnoter's voice appears in cream serif under the envelope: *"It has been waiting for you. Which is to say, it has been waiting."*

**Frame 3 (4.5–8.0s) — The first card enters from inside the envelope.** A single 2:3 frame slides out, still face-down. Face-down is matte-cream with the Lattice glyph and a thin gold rule. Five soft pinpricks of light appear behind the card, one by one, spaced across one second — the five Panes, silent. The user taps to flip.

**Frame 4 (8.0–12.0s) — The card turns.** A real flip animation, 3D axis on the horizontal, not a dissolve. Back-to-front takes 0.8s with a gentle card-on-wood sound. The card settles. It is a **Common from Baker Street — a Watson first-case moment, not Holmes himself.** (Deliberate: the first pull is never the name, it is the witness. This protects the chase — Holmes is still ahead of them.) The card frame: 2:3 portrait of Watson at a writing-desk, pen poised, a casebook open. Serif pullquote centred: *"I have decided to begin. I have not yet decided where."*

**Frame 5 (12.0–16.0s) — The anatomy reveals.** The card holds still, then four micro-animations layer in sequence, each 0.7s apart, each telling the user what one chip means without captions: (a) the elemental chip pulses once, bottom-left, reading **REASON**; (b) the serial ticks up one number and settles, reading **#14,207 / 15,000**; (c) a small gold underline draws under the pullquote; (d) a faint lampblack smudge resolves in the upper-right corner, too subtle to register consciously the first time but permanent on the card.

**Frame 6 (16.0–22.0s) — The lore note.** Below the card, a serif line fades in slowly, two seconds per clause: *"Watson's first case was the wrong one. He did not yet know Holmes; he only knew a man who was not Holmes and behaved as though he were."* This is the Echo. The iceberg 1:2:4 ratio lives here — one visible Surface (Watson's desk), two Echoes (the wrong first case; the not-Holmes figure), four Deep gestured-at unsaids (which case, which man, which day, which ledger).

**Frame 7 (22.0–26.0s) — The Footnoter's close.** Cream serif, slow: *"One of five. Four more are in your letter. The rest of them are in the Lattice."* The number-of-cards-in-the-pack is now known (five). The visitor has not been told the tier of what they hold. The word Common has not appeared. That will come later. The product holds the mystery longer than the market norm.

**Frame 8 (26.0–30.0s) — The choice.** Two buttons rise, both pill, both gold-rule, both serif: **Open the next** (primary) and **Hold this one a moment** (secondary — distinctive because collectibles onboarding never offers this). Tapping "Hold this one a moment" saves the card to a single-card view with a lore note, a "share this quote" primitive (CEO's `ShareCard.tsx` retained per G1), and nothing else. No stats. No market. No burn. No battle. Just the object.

**What the card LOOKS like at the reveal moment.** 2:3 portrait, 85% art / 15% frame. Typography: serif title at 18pt, pullquote at 14pt italic, serial and elemental chip at 9pt tracked. No emoji anywhere. No purple-pink gradient. The palette is tobacco-amber, oxblood, gold-cased glint, lampblack-black at corners. The card reads as *painted*, not *rendered*. It reads as *object*, not *row*. This is the exemplar design system per `EXEMPLAR-CARDS.md` promoted to canonical first-surface.

## 4. First Card Surface — Teaching the Shell System Without Doctrine

The visitor has pulled their first card. They know nothing of the eight-shell taxonomy, the five parallels, the Spines, the Laws. The card surface must teach by *doing*, not telling.

The 4-layer Canon Council toggle from the prototype becomes a single pill at the top-right of the card view, reading **What else am I looking at?** Tapping it does not open a modal of doctrine. It reveals, one line at a time, four facts about the card in plain English, each with a tiny visual cue drawn on the card itself:

1. **This is Watson in his own time. The product calls that PRIME.** — the card art faintly underlines the date on the open casebook.
2. **This version bears the elemental mark REASON. Others in this Pane bear it too.** — the REASON chip at the card's base glows once.
3. **He exists in four other Panes you haven't seen yet. Each one feels different.** — four small silhouettes of Watson appear for half a second at the top of the frame, then fade. Only silhouettes. No reveals.
4. **The mark in the corner is Lampblack. It travels with him.** — the lampblack smudge upper-right pulses gently once.

That is the whole doctrine lesson. The words *Pane*, *shell*, *elemental*, *Lampblack* each appear exactly once, attached to the exact thing they name. The visitor closes the toggle. They can re-open it. There is no mandatory tutorial overlay. There is no "skip tutorial" link — because there is no tutorial. The card is the tutorial. Everything the system will ever ship teaches itself through the same four-line mechanic on the card itself. A Series-3 AETHER-Ultimate teaches AETHER by *being* AETHER, with four lines when asked.

**What's banned on the first-card surface.** No battle stats. No Power/Intelligence/Mystery/Legend/Charm rows. No burn-for-XP CTA. No 30/90-day price chart. No floor price. No volume. No Legacy Score. No market CTA. No "list for sale." No "offer trade." Market exists post-auth in Phase 2, never during first session, per G1 §4.

**What exists on the first-card surface.** The card, the lore note, the "What else am I looking at?" toggle, a **Continue opening your letter** primary, a quiet **Hold this one a moment** secondary, the share-this-quote primitive (ShareCard). That is five surfaces. Not thirteen modules. Five.

## 5. First Lore-Pull — Where the Iceberg Lives

The iceberg is not a module; it is a *texture* that appears wherever the product holds still long enough to be looked at. The first lore-pull strikes in three places, in this sequence, during the first session:

**Pull one — in the card itself (Frame 6 above).** The line *"Watson's first case was the wrong one"* is the first Echo. It strikes inside the reveal ritual, while the visitor's defenses are down. This is the iceberg-in-the-object — the line that hints at a case file the product will never fully show.

**Pull two — in the pack-close screen.** After all five cards open, a closing screen shows the Footnoter's first full paragraph, not centred this time but left-aligned like a letter:

> *Five from one Pane. That was deliberate. The Lattice tends to send a Pane's cards in a cohort — as if the letters from one country arrive on the same Tuesday. The cards you are holding know each other. They do not, yet, know you. Give them a week.*

This is the iceberg as *time* — the promise that something will happen in a week. The one-week pact is Dracula-Daily cadence (A4 §7 opportunity-1), installed at first-session.

**Pull three — in the empty state of the collection view.** When the visitor opens their Collection for the first time (now populated with five Baker Street cards), the empty-state of the *other four Panes* reads: **"Enchanted Kingdom — letter is drafting." / "Wonderland — the cat has a question for you." / "Gothic Horror — a clocktower is counting." / "Greek Myth — a seed has not yet been eaten."** Each is a Deep-layer tease, 8–12 words, no CTA, no timer. They are not locked content behind a paywall — they are pre-echoes of drops that will happen. This is how the product teaches that the Lattice is larger than what it has shipped, without claiming what has not shipped yet.

**Where the iceberg does NOT live.** Not in a "Lore" tab. Not in a `/codex` glossary. Not in a pop-up tutorial. Not in a help-centre article. The moment the iceberg becomes a navigable top-level surface, it stops being an iceberg and becomes a wiki — which is the Pottermore failure mode (per Cosmology Doctrine §4 / R6 §5). The iceberg must *find the visitor*, never the other way around.

## 6. Day 1–3 Nurture — The Footnoter's First Three Letters

The visitor gave their email. They opened five cards. They closed the tab. They did not come back. The Footnoter now has three sends to earn them, one per day, arriving at the same local time they first landed (the product passively logs this — it is the only behavioural datum the system captures pre-purchase). Subject lines are sentence-case, six to ten words, no emoji, no exclamation mark, no symbol characters. The from-name is **The Footnoter — LoreVault**. All three emails render in plain text first, image second, so that BookTok Gmail-users reading on mobile see the voice before the art.

### Day 1 — subject: "The cane in the corner"

> Dear collector,
>
> You held five cards yesterday, all of them from Baker Street. One of them had a line about a cane Watson did not bring home. I owe you the next piece of that.
>
> The first cane was lost on the train from Portsmouth, somewhere between the tunnel at Hindhead and the station at Haslemere. The man who found it has not, to date, returned it. He is not a figure we have shipped. He is not a figure we will ship next month. He is, however, a figure.
>
> This is how the Lattice works — not every thread resolves. Some of them carry on quietly, in the margin of somebody else's case. The Lampblack holds them.
>
> You have four Panes still to hear from. The next one writes on Sunday.
>
> — The Footnoter
> (who is, as always, slightly behind on correspondence)

*Design:* plain serif on cream, one thin gold rule at top, no buttons. At the bottom, in small grey, a single quiet link: *"Your five cards are here whenever you want them."* No CTA. The invitation is structural, not imperative.

### Day 2 — subject: "A cat in a parlour in London"

> There is a parlour in London, currently empty, that has reported a grin where no cat is.
>
> The Cheshire estate has not commented. The grin has not, to date, been removed. The landlord has been writing increasingly formal letters to an address that does not, strictly speaking, exist.
>
> This is one of the things that happens when two Panes brush against each other: something is left behind that does not, properly, belong. A grin, a cane, a tally-mark on a wall. We call it Lampblack. It is the only physics the Lattice has.
>
> You have not yet seen a Wonderland card. When you do, you will recognise the grin. That is by design.
>
> — The Footnoter
> (writing from the parlour in question, which is not, strictly, a parlour)

*Design:* same template. Below the signature, a single faint thumbnail — the grin, half-rendered — with the caption *"You will see this again."* No link. No button. Let it sit.

### Day 3 — subject: "Before you decide"

> I have written to you twice and not asked for anything. I will ask now, once, and then not again this week.
>
> There is a pack on the table for you. It is small — the Sampler, not a drop — and it is free. If you want it, the door is where you left it. If you do not, the Footnoter keeps writing anyway; the Lattice pays me by the sentence, and the sentence rate has not been renegotiated since the Restoration.
>
> What I would like you to know, before you decide, is that we do not sell anything to anyone who has not been inside a Pane for longer than ninety seconds. The Sampler is not a teaser. It is the first room of the house. You will know, after you open it, whether this is a house you want to be in.
>
> The next letter is a Wonderland one, and it will arrive with or without you.
>
> — The Footnoter

*Design:* same template, but the closing link now reads *"Open the Sampler — still on us."* It is the only CTA in three days. It is soft because the voice is soft. The button is gold-ruled pill, small, right-aligned.

**Cadence and escalation.** If Day 3 converts — pack opens — the sequence ends; the user enters the Sunday-Wonderland cadence (A4 §7 opportunity-1 applied to all five Panes on rotation). If Day 3 does not convert, the Footnoter drops to weekly, not daily. No re-engagement pressure. No "we miss you." No discount offer. No urgency. The Lattice has a long calendar; it does not beg. Per A4 §6 threat-counter, this is the opposite of Snap's over-generous monetisation drift.

## 7. The "I'm In" Moment — Ritual of Conversion to $9

The visitor has opened the Sampler. They have three Baker Street Commons, one Watson-Rare (the Sampler guarantees at least one Rare, because A4 §1's "authenticated scarcity + pull drama" requires *pull drama*), and one Enchanted Kingdom Common — the one-per-Sampler cross-Pane tease. They can feel the shape of the system. They have shared a pullquote. They have re-opened the Cheshire tease email once. They are, in retention-analytics terms, "warm." But they have not spent.

**The moment.** It is not a paywall. It is not a "complete your set" nudge. It is a *next letter*. On the morning of the fourth day — chosen because it lands after three organic Footnoter letters and on the cultural Sunday-morning rhythm of the Dracula-Daily archetype — the Footnoter sends a different kind of note, subject line: **"The first real drop is on Thursday."**

The body is short:

> Dear collector,
>
> The Sampler was the introduction. The first *drop* is Series 1, Set BS-1, *221B* — twenty cards, voiced by Watson, shipping Thursday at 4pm London time. A Sampler-pack cost you nothing. A BS-1 pack costs nine dollars. There is no subscription. There is no season pass. There is a pack, a letter, and the cards that are in it.
>
> I am telling you on Sunday so that you have four days to decide without being rushed. I am not sending a second reminder. If you are reading this on Friday morning, the drop has already opened; the cards are still there.
>
> The 221B pack contains five cards. One is guaranteed Rare. One will be from a shell you have not yet seen.
>
> — The Footnoter

**Why this converts the $9.** Because the visitor has now spent four days inside a register they have never experienced from a collectibles product. They have been treated as a reader, not a whale. They have been shown the work, not sold the work. They have learned — through the Sunday-Thursday cadence — that this product ships on a *calendar* and not a countdown. The $9 feels less like a transaction and more like a **subscription to a correspondence**. That framing is Dracula-Daily's moat and LoreVault's steal.

The checkout itself is three taps. Card on file (via Stripe Link-style email-lookup, no wallet), confirm, pack lands in the inbox at 4pm Thursday with a Footnoter note opening it. No confetti. No toast. No level-up sound. The reveal ritual is the reward. They have now paid $9 to open a letter. If the cards inside are *as moving as the Sampler was*, they will buy the next one on Sunday without the Footnoter asking. This is the LTV unlock.

**The ratio we hold to.** For every $9 we extract, the Footnoter has delivered four letters. For every pack we ship, one card in it is an Echo of a card in another Pane. The 1:2:4 is not a marketing tool — it is the conversion mechanic.

## 8. Anti-Onboarding — Six Patterns to Refuse

The collectibles and NFT space has spent five years teaching users that onboarding is a minefield. LoreVault's onboarding is defined as much by what it *refuses* as what it ships. These six patterns are banned from the first session, the first week, and the first drop.

1. **Wallet-creation pop-up-of-death.** Banned. No MetaMask. No WalletConnect. No seed-phrase screen. No "save your 12 words." No key management surfaced to the user *ever*. Flow custodial account + email magic-link is the whole auth surface. A visitor on their first session must never see the word *wallet*. Per A4 §4 failure-3: consumers don't buy chain; they buy feelings about a story.

2. **"Connect your wallet" as the first CTA.** Banned. No wallet connection, ever, as the entry gesture. The entry gesture is *open the first Pane.* Wallet export — for the eventual power user who wants to self-custody — lives buried in Settings, available after the second drop, never presented proactively. Flow is infrastructure, not pitch.

3. **Marketplace-first IA.** Banned per G1 §3.2. No market tab in the first session. No "browse cards." No floor price. No 7D volume. No market cap stat tile. No portfolio P&L. Marketplace is deferred to Phase 2 post-auth. The visitor will not see a $-sign anywhere until the Sunday note — and then only as the price of a single pack, never as a market surface.

4. **Explainer-video-of-doom.** Banned. No 90-second "what is LoreVault" YouTube embed. No animated walkthrough. No mascot narrator. No "watch how it works" CTA. The card *is* the explainer. The Footnoter's letter *is* the walkthrough. Text and image do the work video used to do, because text is faster on 375px and respects the reader.

5. **Retention-chrome-before-ritual.** Banned. No daily-login strip on the post-pack home. No streak counter. No XP bar. No daily challenge. No weekly challenge. No collector-pass tier bar. No season progress meter. No "Season 1: Twilight of the Gods · 29d 23h 59m 59s" countdown. The first week has *two* surfaces: the five cards you own, and the next letter you have not yet received. That is the entire retention apparatus. Cadence is the loop; not FOMO.

6. **IP-roll-call-as-seduction.** Banned. The first sentence a visitor ever reads is not "Sherlock Holmes. Dracula. Zeus." — that is the A3 jarring-moment-5 Fiverr pitch deck. The first sentence is **a single quote from a single figure in a single frame.** The visitor meets Watson and Holmes first. They meet Persephone second. They meet the Cheshire Cat third. They do not meet the *category* until they have met three members of it. Per A4 §5 archetype mapping, name-drop onboarding maximally repels the Dracula-Daily Diaspora, the Pratchett-Discoverer, and the Multiverse Lorehead — all three of whom the product needs. One Moment seduces. Three names evict.
