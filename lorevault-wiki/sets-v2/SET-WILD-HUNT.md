# THE WILD HUNT
## A LoreVault Set — 20 Moments across Cosmological-Variant Panes

**Set premise:** The Wild Hunt is the most persistent pan-European mythological event: a spectral procession of hunters, hounds, and the dead that rides the night sky during the dark of the year. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, it rides every night. In other Panes, it rides on specific dates. Every culture near the European tradition has its own leader, its own hounds, its own rules for what happens if you see it or hear it or join it. This Set collects 20 such figures.

**Voice register:** Enchanted Kingdom, inflected toward the Norse-North. The unnamed teller. The woodcut caption. Where the figure is ancient enough to be a force rather than a person, the voice goes further back — the scratch on the stone, not the ink on the page. Where a figure is literary (Goethe's Erlking; the Charlemagne-cycle), the voice acknowledges the literary layer without hiding behind it. Flavor text is not ornamentation; it is the third dimension the art cannot carry.

**Architecture key:**
- **Pane** = which cosmological variant
- **Tier** = I (Surface) / II (Echo) / III (Deep)
- **Lampblack Residue** = the cross-Pane recognition handle
- **Pratchett Tweak** = the "sounds right but isn't exactly the same" adjustment
- **Flavor text** = 1–3 sentences in the figure's register, conforming to Flavor-Text Doctrine

---

## The Five Panes of The Wild Hunt Set

**1. The Wild Hunt Eternal Pane** — the Hunt rides every night; the leader rotates by moon-phase and season; no night is safe; the hounds know every road.

**2. The Lattice-Standard Pane** — the Hunt rides on Old Year's Day (January 6) and Samhain (November 1); it is dormant on every other night, though the hounds can be heard if you listen near running water.

**3. The Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane** — the Hunt is in tension with the Long Night gift-bringers; they share the dark of December in an uneasy truce that neither side has ever formally acknowledged; the white horse is claimed by both.

**4. The Titans-Held Pane** — the Hunt predates the Olympians, who were never born in this Pane; it is led by proto-gods and the dead of an older age; the hounds are larger here, and the horns older.

**5. The Old Ones Persist Pane** — the Hunt is led by the oldest sky-gods; El rides in his mountain-form; Anu takes the winter quarter; the Baal Cycle is incomplete because the Hunt interrupted it.

## Cross-Pane Lampblack Anchors

Before the Moments, three structural residues that hold the Set together across all five Panes. Every collector who owns cards from different Panes should recognize these anchors as the same thing wearing different clothes.

**The Eight Hoofbeats.** Sleipnir's eight legs are the sound of the Hunt before the Hunt arrives. In every Pane, the advance sound is a gallop with too many beats. The specific number varies by tradition — eight (Sleipnir), an uncountable many (the Furious Host), three-twice (the hounds running in pairs) — but the wrongness of the rhythm is constant. Too many beats for one horse. Not enough pause between them. Something coming.

**The White Animal.** A white hound, a white horse, a white stag. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, all three appear. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, the white horse is shared between Wodan's Hunt and Sinterklaas's procession and neither will say plainly which came first. In the Titans-Held Pane, the white animal is older than naming. It is the thing the Hunt is chasing, and also the thing the Hunt is. No tradition in which the white animal is caught. The Hunt continues because of this.

**The Note of the Horn.** One specific pitch, heard before the Hunt is visible. Described in Norwegian sources as a sound that cannot be transcribed; in Welsh sources as the crying of the Cwn Annwn heard first very loud and then quieter as they approach; in German sources as the howling wind that is not wind. In the Old Ones Persist Pane, scholars at Ugarit wrote down the frequency in cuneiform and then burned the tablet. The note survives. The notation does not.

### MOMENT 01 — Jölvir (the Furious One)
**Figure:** Odin in his hunt-heiti
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** I (Surface)

The All-Father on Sleipnir, collecting what winter is owed. The Norse Wild Hunt — *Åsgårdsrei*, the Asgard-ride — follows him through the darkest quarter. He is not hunting for pleasure. He is gathering the unquiet dead: those who were not properly buried, those who died at the wrong hour or in the wrong place and have not found their way to Hel. This is a service. The service requires that he be terrible, because the unquiet dead do not come willingly, and the living who see him do not understand the distinction between his errand and their own destruction.

He is named "Jölvir" in this card rather than "Odin" because the Set uses the lesser-known *heiti* as a Pratchett-grade adjustment: the terror is primary; the All-Father is secondary. The name means "the Yule-being" or, in the older glosses, "the terrifying one of the season." In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, this name is used first. The other names are known. They are used with more caution.

**Lampblack Residue:** The eight-legged horse. The single eye catching starlight. The two ravens (Huginn and Muninn circle the Hunt even at night — Thought and Memory following the dead-gathering, because both are required for the dead to be known). The spear Gungnir raised to direct, not to fight. The broad hat and gray cloak of the wanderer who is always already there.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Jölvir" rather than "Odin." In this Pane, the Yule-terror name is primary. Odin is the name used when he is a god. Jölvir is the name used when he is winter.

**Art direction:** Black sky; Sleipnir's eight hooves against a field of winter stars, four legs extended in stride and four in the moment of landing simultaneously — conveying the sense of a creature that occupies two positions in space. A tall figure in gray, the hat brim shadow-deep, one point of light where the eye catches reflection. The ravens barely visible, nearly part of the dark. Below: bare winter oaks, central European silhouette. No color in the scene except the light of the eye, the light of the stars, and the white of the horse's breath in cold air.

**Flavor text:** *"He is not hunting the living. He is herding the dead. Try to remember that when you hear the hooves."*

### MOMENT 02 — Wuodan (the December-Rider)
**Figure:** Wodan in the Low German / old Dutch orthography
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns
**Tier:** II (Echo)

In the Netherlands and Low Germany, the old name for Odin is Wodan — and in the Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane, Wodan rides on the night of December 5 specifically. The same night that Sinterklaas makes his rounds. The same white horse. The same rooftops. The children leave hay in their shoes, and both Wodan's horse and Sinterklaas's horse are said to eat it, but no child has ever seen which horse eats first, because looking is the thing you are not supposed to do.

Jan de Vries and other scholars of the Dutch tradition have argued, carefully, that Sinterklaas absorbed the Wodan Wild Hunt complex during Christianization: the nocturnal riding, the omniscience about children's behavior, the chimney-descent, the list of the good and the bad — these fit the All-Father's attributes more precisely than they fit a fourth-century bishop of Myra. The Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane holds both truths simultaneously. They are the same old wound in the culture. Neither side has agreed to resolve it, and the wound is what the Pane runs on.

**Lampblack Residue:** The white horse (four legs; not Sleipnir — Sleipnir has eight; Wodan's December horse is the one that looks ordinary). The hay in the shoes. The night of December 5. The not-looking.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Wuodan" rather than "Wodan" — the older Dutch orthographic form, from the period when the compound was closer to the surface. The extra vowel makes the etymology visible: *Wōðanaz*, "the one possessed by divine fury," before the fury was re-dressed as episcopal charity.

**Art direction:** A Dutch canal city at night, late autumn — bare trees, canal black and still. A tall figure on a white horse crossing a bridge, seen from below and behind. In the far distance, a steamship's lights at the harbor mouth: Sinterklaas arriving. The two figures at opposite ends of the frame, neither looking at the other. The horse eats something from a rooftop as it passes.

**Flavor text:** *"The children leave hay for the horse. They believe it is the bishop's horse. The horse does not correct them."*

### MOMENT 03 — Hernus (the Windsor Ghost)
**Figure:** Herne the Hunter
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The spectral hunter of Windsor Great Park. The antlers growing from his head — not a helmet, not a trophy, growing. The chain hanging from the oak. The first textual appearance is Shakespeare's *Merry Wives of Windsor* (c.1597), where he is already described as a ghost who has always been there. The question of whether Herne is pre-Christian — a survival of a Celtic or pre-Roman antlered deity reaching back to the Gaulish figures, to the Cernunnos type — is actively contested. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, both layers walk simultaneously: the folk-ghost layer and the possible ancient layer are the same figure at different depths of time, and the depth is not visible from the surface.

In the Lattice-Standard Pane, Herne leads the Hunt on the Long Night nights — Old Year's Day and Samhain — through Windsor and outward. He rattles his chains. The rattling is not warning. The rattling is not threat. The rattling is simply what he does, the way a clock ticks without intending to keep time.

**Lampblack Residue:** The antlers. The chain hanging from the oak, or around his neck — the tradition holds both. The oak. The winter/forest setting. The ambiguity of his origin as a feature, not a defect.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Hernus" — a Latinized form that does not appear in any historical source, which is precisely the point. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, Hernus is the name the Romans would have given him if they had named him, which implies he was there during the Roman period, which the historical record does not confirm. The name is a claimed antiquity, which is how most antiquities work.

**Art direction:** Windsor Great Park, night, winter. An ancient oak — the kind of oak whose width requires three people to embrace — with chains hanging from the major limbs. Standing in the oak's roots: a figure whose silhouette is human from the shoulder down and antlered above, the antlers bare winter-branch complexity, not stylized. The castle visible in the far background, lit. No hounds visible. The hounds are what you hear, not what you see.

**Flavor text:** *"He does not care whether you believe he is ancient. He cares whether you can hear the chain."*

### MOMENT 04 — Gwynn mab Nudd (Lord of Annwn)
**Figure:** Gwyn ap Nudd
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** I (Surface)

The Welsh lord of Annwn — the Otherworld, the underworld of Welsh tradition — and leader of the *Cwn Annwn*, the Hounds of the Underworld: white bodies, red ears. Gwyn ap Nudd rides the Welsh hills every night in the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane. He is not collecting punishment. He is collecting. The *Cwn Annwn* run ahead of him, and their howling is heard loud at a distance and quieter as they approach — the inversion of what sound does naturally, which tells you what is happening to the distance between you and them. When the howling is very quiet, it is because they are very close.

In *Culhwch and Olwen* — one of the oldest preserved Welsh narratives — Gwyn ap Nudd fights Gwythyr ap Greidawl every May Day over the maiden Creiddylad, and will do so until Judgment Day. He is a combatant in a seasonal war that has no resolution because resolution would end the year. He is both the death-shepherd and the force that makes spring necessary. He is the person that winter needs in order to work.

**Lampblack Residue:** The white hounds with red ears. The Welsh mountain terrain above the cloud line. The Otherworld-boundary that travels with him. The eternal May Day combat. The name "White" (*Gwyn*) carried by a figure of darkness.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Gwynn mab Nudd" — the "mab" form of son rather than the anglicized "ap." A single consonant cluster that puts another half-generation of distance between this figure and the modernized tradition. He is the *son* of Nudd; the lineage is part of the name.

**Art direction:** Welsh mountain terrain at night, the fog below the peaks. On the ridge: a pale horse barely visible against slightly lighter sky, and ahead of it, a pack of white hounds with red ears that seem lit from within by no visible source. Behind the hounds: a rider, face not visible, the outline tall and contained. Below the fog line: ordinary Wales. Above it: not.

**Flavor text:** *"The white hounds are far from the worst thing about meeting Gwynn mab Nudd. The white hounds are the announcement."*

### MOMENT 05 — Carnunos (the Antlered Boundary)
**Figure:** Cernunnos
**Pane:** Titans-Held
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The antlered lord of Gaul. Attested on the Gundestrup Cauldron, on the Pillar of the Boatmen in Paris (1st century CE, the only inscription that carries his name), and in the visual language of La Tène art across Continental Celtic territory. He sits cross-legged. He holds a torc in one hand and a ram-headed serpent in the other. Animals surround him in attitude not of fear but of recognition.

In the Titans-Held Pane, Cernunnos predates the Olympians — who were never born in this Pane — and functions as an ancient primordial rather than a named deity: the lord of wild animals, the boundary between settlement and wilderness, the intelligence that ensures the great herds move when they should move and winter comes when it should come. In this Pane, he leads the Hunt not as a collection of the dead but as the annual driving of the herds — the Hunt as the agricultural motion of the world, the cycle of chase and pasture that keeps things alive. He is not a hunter. He is the edge of the territory where hunters are guests.

**Lampblack Residue:** The antlers (the connecting tissue to Herne, to the white stag, to the whole antlered-figure archetype the Set carries). The torc. The ram-headed serpent in the left hand — the specific iconographic detail that separates him from decorative antlered figures and places him in the Gaulish tradition.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Carnunos" — a hypothetical Proto-Celtic reconstruction, more guttural than the attested Gaulish form, one vowel shift toward an older mouth. The sound of the name before the name was written down.

**Art direction:** Gundestrup Cauldron visual language as foundation — the flat, stylized, richly zoomorphic Gaulish plate-work — but animated: the figures are mid-movement, the animals in full run around the seated central figure. The animals' eyes show not terror but the recognition of something that belongs to the same category they do. Cernunnos himself is in motion only in the eyes.

**Flavor text:** *"He is not hunting the animals. He is the edge of the world where animals are themselves and humans are the guests."*

### MOMENT 06 — Cailleach Daoraidh (the Deep-Cold)
**Figure:** the Cailleach
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** III (Deep)

The blue-faced Scottish winter hag. She is not a witch. She is winter personified: a cosmogonic force who dropped boulders from her apron to make the Scottish Highlands, who wades through seas, who has lived through more repetitions of the world than she can name. In some traditions, she leads the Wild Hunt specifically in the deep winter months. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, she has the Hunt from November 1 through February 1 — the darkest quarter precisely, the period the Irish counted as winter's full sovereignty. She leads because she is the season, and the Hunt is the season made visible and moving.

She is not cruel. She is winter. Winter is not cruel either. The Cailleach Bheur — the sharp one, the cutting cold — is not the name used in this card. "Daoraidh" comes from the Proto-Celtic root *dubros*: deep, dark, the cold that is below cutting, the cold that is structural. The cutting cold wounds. The deep cold is just what the world is during its darkest quarter.

**Lampblack Residue:** The blue face. The staff (*slachdan*) that freezes the ground at each strike. The cosmogonic scale — she does not move through the landscape, the landscape adjusts to her passage. The Samhain-to-Imbolc sovereignty.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Daoraidh" — the deep-cold epithet, from *dubros*, rather than the more familiar "Bheur" (sharp/cutting). The distinction is between the cold that damages and the cold that is the baseline condition of the dark quarter. The Cailleach Daoraidh is not hurting anything. She is simply what that time of year is.

**Art direction:** An enormous old woman striding across the Scottish Highlands at a scale where the mountains are her terrain, not her backdrop. Her cloak is the color of a sky an hour after sunset in November — not black, not blue, something between them. The Hunt follows her like the trailing edge of the cloak: riders, hounds, the whole procession as a function of her movement rather than their own. Ahead of her: spring, months away, not visible from here.

**Flavor text:** *"She does not lead the Hunt to punish. She leads it because winter is the oldest thing there is, and the Hunt is winter made loud."*

### MOMENT 07 — Erlenbaum-König (the Alder-Tree King)
**Figure:** the Erlking
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The German-Danish figure who kills children with a whisper. Goethe's poem (1782) is the famous crystallization: a father rides through the night with a feverish child in his arms; the child insists it can see and hear the Erlking calling; the father insists the child is dreaming; at the journey's end the child is dead. The folk tradition beneath Goethe is older and more ambiguous: the figure who speaks in the register of comfort, who sounds like someone you love, who offers what you are too cold and frightened to refuse.

The etymology is disputed — *elverkonge* (elf-king) or *Erlenbaum* (alder tree), the tree that grows at the water's edge, the boundary tree, the tree whose roots are in two worlds at once. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, "Erlenbaum-König" — the Alder-Tree King — is used as the Pratchett-grade adjustment: this makes him a spirit of the boundary between land and water rather than a king of elves, which positions him in the Wild Hunt's boundary-crossing logic rather than in the Faerie-court tradition.

In the Lattice-Standard Pane, the Erlking is the Hunt-fragment that specializes in the young. The main Hunt rides for the unburied dead. The Erlking rides for the dying who have not yet decided. He is faster.

**Lampblack Residue:** The fog. The calling voice that sounds like someone you love. The child. The terrible tenderness — he is not frightening because he is monstrous; he is frightening because he is gentle.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Erlenbaum-König" — the Alder-Tree King. The variant etymology that puts him at the water's edge, in the boundary-space, rather than in a court.

**Art direction:** A German forest, late 18th century — the Romantic landscape style, the illuminated gap between dark trees, the sense of composition that the Romantic painters used for horror before horror was a genre. A rider with a child in his arms, the horse's motion visible in the blur of legs, the rider's face contracted toward the path ahead. The fog behind them has a shape: not threatening, almost consoling, the shape of something that knows what it wants and is patient. The father's face shows he sees it. He is pretending not to.

**Flavor text:** *"'My father, do you not hear —' The father heard. The father had always heard. The father rode faster, because faster was the only answer he had."*

### MOMENT 08 — Diana Nemorensis (the Grove Diana)
**Figure:** Diana at Nemi
**Pane:** Titans-Held
**Tier:** I (Surface)

Diana Nemorensis is the Diana of the Sacred Grove at Nemi in the Alban Hills, south of Rome — the oldest attested form of Diana, predating the Roman syncretism with Artemis. The priest of the grove held his position by right of combat: any escaped slave who could break a branch from the sacred oak and defeat the priest in single combat became the new Rex Nemorensis, the King of the Grove. He held the title until someone did the same to him. James George Frazer opened *The Golden Bough* (1890) with this image: the priest who could never sleep, the drawn sword in the sacred grove, the perpetual waiting for the challenger.

In the Titans-Held Pane — where the Olympians were never born — Diana is not a Roman deity. She is a pre-Olympian goddess of the wild: older, rawer, closer to the Mistress of Animals archetype, the *Potnia Theron* who governs the wilderness not through law but through being the wilderness's governing intelligence. She leads the Hunt in this Pane not as a death-procession but as the active governance of wild territory — the Hunt is how the wild world administers itself.

**Lampblack Residue:** The silver bow. The moon. The hounds. The Nemi grove. The priest who cannot sleep.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Diana Nemorensis" rather than plain "Diana" — the epithet that specifies the ancient cult at Nemi, the pre-Artemis Diana, the one who predates the syncretism and the literary version. She is the Diana who required combat, not the Diana of the literary hunt.

**Art direction:** The Alban Hills at night, Lake Nemi below — a volcanic crater lake, perfectly circular, black as iron. A woman with a silver bow running at the head of a pack of enormous hounds; the moon full and directly overhead, casting her shadow sharply behind her; the hounds do not look at her, they look ahead. No arrows nocked. She is the direction, not the shot.

**Flavor text:** *"She does not kill. The hounds kill. She gives the direction. This is what it means to lead."*

### MOMENT 09 — The Wisht Hounds (Backward-Headed)
**Figure:** the Yeth Hounds of Devon
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The Devon and Dartmoor ghost-hounds, also called Wisht Hounds — "wisht" being Devonian dialect for ghostly, accursed, the quality of a thing that is wrong in the way that makes you want to look away. In some traditions, headless. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, they are the advance guard of the Hunt: they run ahead of the main procession and their howling is the warning. Their heads are on their shoulders but facing backward — they see where the Hunt has been, not where it is going. They cannot navigate. They can only be aimed.

The specific haunt is Wistman's Wood on Dartmoor: a stunted, ancient oak wood, the trees gnarled and low, the canopy broken. The hounds emerge from the wood when the Hunt is near. The wood is why the Hunt passes through Dartmoor at all — the wood is an old enough place that the Hunt has a path through it, a groove worn in the world over centuries of the same passage.

**Lampblack Residue:** The howling before the Hunt arrives. The backward-facing heads (they know the past; they cannot see the future). Dartmoor. Wistman's Wood. The word "wisht" — which should be used in the card's framing.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Wisht Hounds" rather than "Yeth Hounds" — the Dartmoor variant name that emphasizes the emotional and atmospheric register over the physical description. "Wisht" encodes more of the tradition in one syllable than "yeth" (headless) does.

**Art direction:** Dartmoor, night; the granite tors against a starless sky; Wistman's Wood below, the trees barely taller than a person, heavily weathered. A pack of white hounds emerging from the tree line, each one with its head turned fully backward on its neck, running forward by some mechanism other than sight. In the far distance behind them: a horn's bell, held by no visible hand.

**Flavor text:** *"They can smell where the Hunt has been. They cannot smell where they are going. That is why they run without pausing. That is not the worst thing about them."*

### MOMENT 10 — Rex Catus (the King of Cats)
**Figure:** the King of the Cats
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard
**Tier:** III (Deep)

A specific English folk motif, documented in multiple variants across the British Isles: there is a King of the Cats, and when he dies, a new one takes the title. The standard folk tale form: a traveler returns home and tells his wife that he saw a procession of cats mourning their dead king — and the household cat, hearing this, leaps up and says "Then I am King!" and vanishes into the night. The cat-soul procession runs parallel to the Wild Hunt but operates by entirely different rules. It is not collecting the unburied dead. Its rules have never been explained in any source.

In the Lattice-Standard Pane, the King of the Cats leads the cat-hunt on the nights the main Hunt rides. The cat-procession crosses the main Hunt's path exactly once, at a location no tradition has specified. What happens at the crossing is unknown. Both hunts continue afterward. This is treated, in the Lattice-Standard Pane, as a settled cosmological fact.

**Lampblack Residue:** The crown. The cat-soul procession. The household cat who already knows and was waiting for the announcement. The sudden vacancy in a room.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Rex Catus" — the Latin title deliberately given to an English folk figure. The Romans would have named him; the name implies he was there for the Romans; the implication is probably true; no source confirms it. This is exactly the layer of antiquity that English folk figures carry without documentation.

**Art direction:** An English countryside lane at night, full moon. A procession of cats walking upright in single file, their shadows precise on the moonlit road. At the head: a black cat with a small gold crown, moving with the specific certainty of a figure who has always known this moment was coming. In a farmhouse window at the lane's edge: a tabby cat watching with her chin on the sill. Her expression is recognition, not surprise.

**Flavor text:** *"The household cat has always known. She was waiting for the announcement."*

### MOMENT 11 — Carolus Magnus (the Century-Rider)
**Figure:** Charlemagne as the Sleeping King
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard
**Tier:** III (Deep)

The Germanic sleeping-king tradition places Charlemagne in the Untersberg mountain on the Austrian-German border, sleeping until Germany needs him most. The same tradition attaches to Frederick Barbarossa in the Kyffhäuser, and to Arthur at Avalon, and to Frederick II, and to Sebastian of Portugal, and to Holger the Dane. The pattern is consistent: the great king does not die; he sleeps; he will return; in the meantime his court sleeps with him; his beard grows through the stone table; crows circle the mountain.

In the Lattice-Standard Pane, Charlemagne leads a spectral hunt from the Untersberg on a century-cycle — one night every hundred years, riding out with the Hunt's borrowed horses and hounds to remind the living that the dead can still affect history. He is not the Hunt-leader full-time. He is a Hunt-borrower. He takes what he needs for one night and returns it before dawn. What he does with it on that night has never been the same twice, and the traditions conflict about what happened in which century, which is exactly what traditions about sleeping kings do.

**Lampblack Residue:** The mountain. The crack of light in the rock face. The century-cycle. The crown that is simultaneously a Frankish military crown and something older, as if the iron was worked before the Franks had a name for iron.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Carolus Magnus" — the Latinized form of Charlemagne, using the Roman imperial framing rather than the vernacular German or French versions. This ties him to a longer lineage than the Frankish kingdom: he is the Western heir of Rome, which means he is the heir of everything Rome absorbed, which means his sleeping-king story begins further back than 814 CE.

**Art direction:** The Untersberg mountain at night, early winter, the rock face clean with cold. A crack in the face of the cliff, ten feet tall, with warm light coming through from the other side — warm light in a cold night, the specific wrongness of an interior on an exterior surface. Through the crack: the shapes of horses. Emerging from the crack, one mounted figure in Frankish plate armor, the crown's points just clearing the rock edge. Ravens circling.

**Flavor text:** *"He does not return to rule. He returns to remind. The reminder is the same every century: 'We were here.' The dead find this sufficient."*

### MOMENT 12 — Slaipnir (the Bridge Between)
**Figure:** Sleipnir
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** III (Deep)

The eight-legged horse is not merely Odin's mount — in the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, Sleipnir is himself a cosmological structure: the bridge between the living world and the dead world, the mechanism by which the Hunt can traverse both in a single night. He was born of Loki in mare-form and the stallion Svadilfari — a conception that begins as a trick and ends as one of the great divine animals. His eight legs are iconographically linked to the eight-legged bier (a pallbearer at each corner), marking him as inherently funereal: he is built for carrying the dead.

He can travel to Hel and return. He is the fastest horse in all nine worlds. He can walk on water and through stone. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, he is the reason the Hunt is possible at all — without him, the Hunt would be confined to either the living world or the dead world, and would not be a hunt but a patrol. He is the motion. Odin is almost a passenger.

**Lampblack Residue:** The eight legs, four in each world simultaneously. The gray color. The ability to cross between the nine worlds. The laughter of Loki, somewhere in his lineage.

**Pratchett tweak:** "Slaipnir" — a slight vowel shift from the standard "Sleipnir" that moves the pronunciation slightly west, slightly older, slightly more Proto-Germanic in feel. The horse predates the Icelandic sources that recorded him; this name sounds like it.

**Art direction:** The horse as subject, the rider as almost incidental. An eight-legged gray horse in mid-stride across a sky that is simultaneously the sky and the underworld — the horizon line is both. Four hooves on air, four on ground, and the ground is two things at once. A figure on his back, barely visible, a shape more than a person. The horse's eye is the only source of certainty in the composition: it knows where it is going.

**Flavor text:** *"Loki made him. Loki makes the best things and the worst things, and it is not always clear at the time which is which. Slaipnir is the best thing."*

### MOMENT 13 — The Cwn Annwn
**Figure:** the White Hounds of Wales
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** I (Surface)

White body, red ears. The hounds of the Welsh underworld who run ahead of Gwynn mab Nudd. Their howling obeys the inversion-rule: loud at a distance, quieter as they approach. This is because they are crossing into the living world from the direction of Annwn, and Annwn is very loud from a distance and very quiet when it is right next to you. Hearing them very quietly means they are very close. That is the last information you will receive about where they are before they are upon you.

The red ears are the standard Welsh supernatural animal coloration — the animals of the Otherworld are white with red ears, always, without exception, in every Welsh source. The coloring is the tell. If you see a white animal in the Welsh tradition with red ears, it is of Annwn, and the question is only what it is doing here and what it is for.

**Lampblack Residue:** White body, red ears. The inverted volume of the howling. The Annwn-origin marker.

**Art direction:** Welsh hill country, twilight. Three white hounds in the foreground, their red ears the only warm color in the composition. They are facing the viewer. Behind them: the hills falling into fog. The howling — implied by their posture — should feel like something you would not be able to hear if they were this close.

**Flavor text:** *"In the distance: very loud. Closer: quieter. Almost upon you: silence. Then: the red ears."*

### MOMENT 14 — The Cu Sith (Three Howls)
**Figure:** the Cu Sith of the Scottish Highlands
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The green hound of Scottish Highland tradition. Enormous — the size of a two-year-old steer. Dark forest green, sometimes described as dark as moss, sometimes as dark as the underside of a fern. It does not run in a pack. It does not howl continuously. It gives one howl, then a second howl, then a third howl — and by the third howl it has reached you. Between the howls: silence that is not the absence of sound but the presence of something that has decided not to make any.

If you are inside by the third howl, you are safe. The traditions are consistent on this. Inside. Door closed. The third howl is the arrival, not the warning of the arrival. There is no fourth howl.

**Lampblack Residue:** The green color (unique in the tradition — no other supernatural hound is green). The three-howl sequence. The size. The silence between.

**Art direction:** Scottish Highland glen at dusk — a single farmhouse with one lit window. At the edge of the treeline, two enormous green eyes at a height that indicates something much larger than any dog. The body is not visible yet. The light in the farmhouse window is the compositional counter-point: there, and reachable, if you move now.

**Flavor text:** *"First howl: close the door. Second howl: bar it. Third howl: it is sufficient to have closed the door."*

### MOMENT 15 — The White Stag (the Prey That Is Not Caught)
**Figure:** the White Stag
**Pane:** All Panes (cross-Pane card)
**Tier:** I (Surface)

In every Pane, the Wild Hunt pursues a white stag. In no Pane is the white stag caught. The white stag is the Hunt's form: without it, the Hunt is a host of riders with nowhere specific to go, which is more dangerous than a hunt, because a purposeless host of the dead is not collecting anything, it is simply present. The stag gives the Hunt direction. The stag also, in certain Celtic traditions, leads the dead toward their rest — the prey is simultaneously the guide. The Hunt chases what it needs to follow.

The stag is Tier I in all five Panes because it is the surface thing, the thing you see first if you see the Hunt at all. You see the white stag. Then you see what is behind the white stag. Then you understand that the white stag knew you were here.

**Lampblack Residue:** The white coat. The never-being-caught. The path through the underworld that is also a chase.

**Art direction:** A white stag running in absolute darkness, lit by nothing external — the whiteness is its own source of visibility. The Hunt is behind it, but the Hunt is not visible: only the negative space of the stag's running implies the pressure from behind. The stag's eyes face forward. It knows the path.

**Flavor text:** *"It runs because running is the right thing to do. When it stops running, the Hunt stops riding. Neither has agreed to let that happen."*

### MOMENT 16 — The Witness (the One Who Lived)
**Figure:** the human who sees the Hunt
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard
**Tier:** I (Surface)

Every tradition that documents the Wild Hunt also documents the Witness — the human who saw it and survived to tell. The telling is the mechanism by which the tradition propagates: without the Witness, there is no Hunt in human memory, and the Hunt's cosmological function (sweeping the old year's dead into the proper realm, maintaining the boundary between the living and the dead) is unobserved and therefore, in some traditions, weakened. The Witness is not incidental to the Hunt. The Witness is part of the Hunt's operation.

The Witness is always marked: a white streak in dark hair, appearing after the night they saw the Hunt. In some traditions, they cannot sleep after Old Year's Day. In others, they can only sleep during the day. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, the Witness tells the story every year on the nights the Hunt rides — January 6 and November 1 — and on those nights only. They do not tell it on other nights. They have tried.

**Lampblack Residue:** The white streak. The thousand-yard stare that appears in every portrait of a Witness from every tradition that has portraits. The annual telling on the specific dates.

**Art direction:** A village elder — indeterminate age, the white streak in dark hair prominent in the firelight — telling a story to a group of children seated at their feet. Winter outside the window, the darkness the specific darkness of January. The elder's eyes are not on the children. The children are rapt and looking at the elder. The elder is looking at something in the middle distance that is not in the room.

**Flavor text:** *"They ask what he saw. He tells them. He tells them the same thing every year. They do not quite believe him until they see it themselves, at which point he cannot reach them in time to warn them."*

### MOMENT 17 — The Hooden Horse (the Hunt-Fragment)
**Figure:** the Hooden Horse of Kent
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** II (Echo)

The East Kent horse-skull procession — documented in the Isle of Thanet and surrounding areas, the hoodeners going door-to-door with a horse skull on a pole, the jaw snapping. The etymology of "Hooden" is contested: it may be from "wooden" (wooden horse-head) or it may be from "Wodan" — Odin — which would make the Hooden Horse a direct physical survival of the Wodan Wild Hunt in English folk tradition, carrying the Hunt's energy in a horse-skull, ribboned and domesticated, going house to house.

In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, the Hooden Horse is a Hunt-fragment that got stranded. The Hunt passed through East Kent at some ancient date — the sources do not specify the date, because the sources do not have access to dates that old — and left a piece of itself behind: a horse, dead, but still snapping its jaw because the Hunt's motion never fully released it. The Kent villagers dress it in ribbons to keep it amiable. It has not been amiable since the Hunt left it. The ribbons help anyway.

**Lampblack Residue:** The horse skull. The snapping jaw. The ribbons. The house-to-house procession. The possibility that the name contains "Wodan."

**Art direction:** An East Kent village street, January, post-Christmas quiet. A group of hoodeners — people bundled in winter coats, one person operating the horse-skull-on-a-pole from beneath a sheet — at a cottage door. The skull is decorated with ribbons, one glass eye catching the doorlight. The jaw is open at the moment of the depiction. The door has not yet opened.

**Flavor text:** *"The Hunt moved on. The horse did not notice. Scholars disagree about whether this was obedience or refusal. The horse is not available for comment."*

### MOMENT 18 — The Dead Rider (the Unnamed)
**Figure:** a rider of the pre-Olympian dead
**Pane:** Titans-Held
**Tier:** III (Deep)

In the Titans-Held Pane, the Hunt is not led by Odin — who was never born — or by any named deity of the Northern tradition. It is led by the dead of the pre-Olympian era: the servants of the Titans who died before there was an Elysium to receive them, because Elysium was an Olympian construction and the Olympians never rose. They have been riding since before the cosmological settlement. They do not have divine patrons to define their function. They ride because riding is what they were doing when the era ended, and the era ending did not stop them.

They are not malevolent. They are bored. The combination is more dangerous than malevolence, which at least has direction. A bored rider of the pre-Olympian dead has no agenda, and a host of riders with no agenda travels wherever the terrain is interesting.

**Lampblack Residue:** The pre-Olympian armor — bronze-age Greek plate, the Linear B period, before iron, before names for most of the gods they might have served. The absence of any divine emblem, badge, or dedication on the armor. The extreme age visible in the equipment if not the riders.

**Art direction:** Riders in Mycenaean-era bronze plate — the boar's-tusk helmet, the body armor assembled from overlapping plates — on horses of the same period, galloping through a landscape that is emphatically contemporary. The riders do not appear to notice the contemporary landscape. The landscape's grass is bending in the direction they came from.

**Flavor text:** *"They predate names. They predate most of the gods. They are what was left when the first age ended and no one came to tell them to stop riding."*

### MOMENT 19 — The Horn (the Sound Before)
**Figure:** the Hunt's horn
**Pane:** All Panes (cross-Pane card)
**Tier:** I (Surface)

The Hunt's horn sounds before the Hunt is visible. In every Pane, the horn is heard differently. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, it produces a specific note that cannot be transcribed — not because the pitch is unusual but because there is no notation system that handles what the note does to the room it is heard in. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, it sounds like a hunting horn heard from across a valley, distant and ordinary and wrong only in that there is no hunting on those specific nights in this Pane. In the Titans-Held Pane, it sounds like the lowest note of a bronze instrument that has not existed for three thousand years — a frequency the body remembers before the mind identifies it.

In the Old Ones Persist Pane, an Ugaritic scholar recorded the note's frequency in cuneiform on a clay tablet in approximately 1350 BCE, and then burned the tablet. The frequency itself was not destroyed by burning the tablet. It is still the same frequency.

**Lampblack Residue:** The note. The advance warning. The fact that by the time you hear the horn, the distance between you and the Hunt has already changed from what it was when the horn was blown.

**Art direction:** A horn bell in darkness, held by no visible hand — the horn apparently suspended, the bell facing out toward the viewer, the mouthpiece receding into shadow that is not ordinary shadow. Sound-waves radiating from the bell, each wave rendered in a slightly different color that maps to the different Panes. The visual challenge: make the same note look like five different things at once.

**Flavor text:** *"You will hear it once. The second time, it is closer. The third time: look up, not toward the sound."*

### MOMENT 20 — The Hunt at Rest
**Figure:** the Hunt in its dawn hour
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal
**Tier:** III (Deep)

In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, the Hunt must rest for one hour at dawn each day. This is not mercy — the Hunt is not a thing that offers mercy, and the rest is not offered to anything. It is necessity: the living world and the dead world both require the boundary to be maintained at the transition points of the day, and the Hunt cannot cross that boundary while the boundary is in transition. So it waits on a hill.

During this hour, the hounds lie down. The riders are still. The Hunt-leader dismounts and stands at the highest point, facing east, not because they are waiting for the sun specifically but because east is the direction that will resolve the transition and allow the boundary to be crossed again. In some traditions, this hour is when the Hunt can be bargained with. In others, this is when the Hunt is at its most dangerous, because it has not yet decided where to ride next. The hour of rest is the hour of choosing.

**Lampblack Residue:** The dawn light, specifically the light before the sun crests — gray and specific, not beautiful, not dramatic. The hillside. The hour. The decision not yet made.

**Art direction:** The moment before dawn — the horizon line lit but the sun not visible. A hillside: the Hunt arrayed on it, all stillness. Hounds lying down, not sleeping. Horses standing with their heads lowered. Riders motionless in the saddle. At the top of the hill, one figure dismounted, standing, facing east: the Hunt-leader as silhouette. The composition should convey that the stillness is not peace. The stillness is loaded.

**Flavor text:** *"The Hunt rides all night. At dawn it rests for one hour. This is not mercy. It is necessity. The distinction matters, particularly to those who approach during the hour thinking it is mercy."*

### Lampblack Residue Cross-Set Chain

The white streak in the Witness's hair (Moment 16) should Echo forward to any future Set involving survivors of cosmological events. The term "Daoraidh" (Moment 06) should be used as a recurring epithet for any figure who embodies structural cold rather than active cold. The eight-hoofbeat advance sound (Structural Anchor, above) should appear in any future Set that crosses the Wild Hunt's territory — even if no Hunt figure appears in that Set.

### Cultural-Sensitivity Compliance

The Set distributes the Hunt's leadership across Welsh, Norse, Scottish, Gaulish, English, German-Danish, Italian, and Romano-Italian traditions, ensuring no single tradition is the Hunt's "owner." The Odin/Jölvir card is explicitly framed as one rotation of the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, not the primary or authentic form. The Wodan card's sensitivity flag (Sinterklaas/Zwarte Piet adjacency) is managed by keeping Wodan entirely in the Hunt-context and not engaging the Sinterklaas gift-giver tradition beyond the white-horse tension; no companion figure is depicted. The Cailleach card uses a living-tradition figure (the Cailleach is actively venerated in contemporary Celtic neo-paganism) and is framed as a cosmological force rather than a character, which is the respectful treatment the tradition requires.

The Titans-Held Pane figures (Carnunos, Diana Nemorensis, the Dead Rider) explicitly avoid any framing that would suggest the pre-Olympian tradition as "more authentic" or "more pure" than the named-deity traditions — they are different in cosmological architecture, not in validity.
