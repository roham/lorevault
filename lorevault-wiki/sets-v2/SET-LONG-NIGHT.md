# THE LONG NIGHT
## A LoreVault Set — 20 Moments across Cosmological-Variant Panes

**Set code:** LN-01  
**Research basis:** CLUSTER-J (European Folk-Survivals), CLUSTER-K (Winter-Festival Figures), CLUSTER-B (Northern European)  
**Research date:** 2026-04-26 / 2026-04-27  
**Flavor-text register:** Folk-tale voice (Enchanted Kingdom adjacent) — the unnamed teller, the woodcut on the wall; inflected per figure where the figure's own voice is the more precise instrument  
**Status:** Draft — ready for Canon Council voice-lead review  
**Cultural sensitivity:** Zwarte Piet / blackface imagery EXCLUDED throughout. No living indigenous ceremonial traditions. Coca-Cola Santa visual language EXCLUDED. See per-card flags where applicable.

---

## Set Premise

The Long Night is the threshold when the sun is weakest and the dead walk freely. Every culture in every Pane has a figure who negotiates this threshold — a gift-bringer, a terror, a judge, a companion to the dark. Some arrive by water. Some come down the chimney. Some wait at the door until you open it. Some inspect your work. Some have already decided. This Set collects 20 such figures across 5 Panes: the moments when the Long Night pressed against a door and found out what was on the other side.

The Set is organized around a structural truth common to all five Panes: **the gift is only meaningful because the punishment is real.** Every figure here carries both hands — one that gives and one that takes. The world does not make it through the Long Night by accident. It makes it through because someone, somewhere, left the porridge out. Or opened the door. Or answered the verse-battle well enough.

**Flavor-text voice for this Set:** The Long Night Set uses the **Enchanted Kingdom voice** — the unnamed teller, the woodcut caption, the thing written by no one. Where individual figures speak in their own register (a judge's ledger, a crone's command, a wanderer's conditional), those inflections are permitted within the Enchanted Kingdom frame. The voice is pre-Disney. The winter is older than the saint and the saint will die first.

## The Five Panes of The Long Night

| Pane | What-If Axiom | What This Means for the Dark of the Year |
|------|--------------|------------------------------------------|
| **The Lattice-Standard Pane** | The default register; all pantheons coexist in a modern-register 1880s–1920s world | The old figures persist alongside gas-lamps and steamships; their power is not diminished by modernity, only better documented |
| **The Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane** | The Coca-Cola Santa never happened; the old winter-feast figures hold real power | The bishop's judgment is actual judgment; the companion's threat is actual threat; no commercial overlay has softened the theology |
| **The Wild Hunt Eternal Pane** | The Hunt rides every night; figures from the dark of the year are Hunt-adjacent | Every winter figure is either a fragment of the Hunt, a thing the Hunt left behind, or a threshold-keeper whose job is to negotiate with the Hunt on behalf of the living |
| **The Old Ones Persist Pane** | The younger pantheons never displaced their elders; winter is older, stranger | Christianity, Soviet re-mythology, and Romantic scholarship all failed; the figures have their original names and their original power; the cosmological stakes are not metaphorical |
| **The Pre-Vedic Pane** | The Aryan synthesis never happened; winter figures are Indo-European cattle-cult remnants | The sacrificial theology is intact; the goat is still the fire-carrier; the gift-giver who arrives at the feast is still the god of the dead in disguise |

### MOMENT 01 — Father Christmas
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** The pre-Victorian English spirit of the Christmas season — not a gift-bringer for children but a lord of the feast itself, a personification of midwinter hospitality and mirth. He predates the Victorian conflation with Sinterklaas and the Coca-Cola synthesis by centuries. He is the Holly King made walking: enormous, warm, slightly dangerous, demanding that doors be opened and that tables be laden. He carries a torch against the cold, not a sack of presents. He is invited in; he feasts; he leaves the household warmer than he found it and expects to be remembered in the morning.

**Lampblack Residue:** The holly crown. The warm drink. The permission to enter — he does not come in unless the door is opened, and that rule is older than England.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** The red robe is there but it is old and worn, not bright — a garment that has seen many winters rather than one that was laundered for the season. The beard is real and not cotton. He smells of woodsmoke and cider. His laugh is genuine and slightly unsettling because it is clear he has found something funnier than he is telling you about.

**Art-direction note:** Green holly crown against a snow-bright night; a farmhouse door half-open with firelight spilling out; the figure of Father Christmas on the step — large, in long holly-and-scarlet robes, tankard in hand, caught mid-laugh. Not threatening. More than threatening.

**Flavor text:** *"He doesn't come in unless you open the door. That's the rule. That's always been the rule."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — the unnamed householder who has lived through enough Long Nights to know the rule by rote.*

### MOMENT 02 — Sintereclaus
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** The Bishop of Myra, arriving by steamboat on the dark canal of a Dutch city where Christmas belongs to the Christ-feast and the night of December 5 belongs entirely to him. His vestments are silver-white against the fog; his mitre rises above the canal-lamp's light. He has his book. He has always had his book. In this Pane he is not a figure of benevolent distribution — he is a judge, and the gifts he brings are the evidence of a verdict already reached. He has read every name in the book. He knows who deserves them.

**Lampblack Residue:** The bishop's staff. The book of names. The white horse waiting on the deck behind him. The arrival by dark water, always dark water.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** His name in this Pane is rendered in the old Low Dutch orthography: *Sintereclaus* — the consonants slightly heavier, the sound older, the word carrying its weight in a way the modern spelling no longer does. The steamship brings him from Spain, but in this Pane no one knows why Spain and no one has ever asked.

**Art-direction note:** Fog on black canal-water; a wooden steamboat with a single brass lantern at the prow; the bishop standing at the bow in silver vestments, book under one arm and staff in hand; the white horse's muzzle visible just behind him; a city of dark gabled houses on both banks.

**Flavor text:** *"The book does not lie. It has never lied. The children know this. That is why they are afraid."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — the unnamed canal-side observer who says this every year and means it every year.*

### MOMENT 03 — Schmutzlein
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** Not the servant. The necessary shadow. In the Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane, Schmutzlein is not subordinate to Sintereclaus — they are the twin-god pair, the two faces of winter judgment: gift and consequence, the light and the dark that makes light meaningful. He is sooty not because he has come down a chimney but because he carries a lantern that burns souls; the soot is the residue of what it has consumed. He does not speak during the procession. He does not need to speak. The children understand what the silence means.

**Lampblack Residue:** The soot-black face — soot from chimney-work, not caricature; the specific darkness of one who descends and ascends on behalf of another. The bundle of birch rods. The sack. The silence.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** In this Pane his name is *Schmutzlein* — the diminutive suffix, which in German makes things sound smaller and therefore more dangerous, the way a very small knife is more dangerous than a very large one. He has no eyes you can see. This is not a metaphor.

**Art-direction note:** A pure black figure in the silver light emanating from Sintereclaus's lantern; birch rods bound with dark cord in his right hand; an empty sack in his left; face obscured by shadow with two faint ember-points where eyes might be; the canal-boat visible behind him.

**Sensitivity note:** The sooty-face depiction is explicitly the Swiss *Schmutzli* tradition — chimney-soot, not racial caricature. The name, the framing, and all art direction must be unambiguous about this. See CLUSTER-K §7 and CLUSTER-J §7.

**Flavor text:** *"The coal is not punishment. The coal is the memory of what you chose. Carry it all year. Decide differently."*

*Voice note: Archetype A4 (The Curse) — a binding stated as already in force; the mechanism is named; the consequence is the coal itself.*

### MOMENT 04 — Ruprecht der Nachgänger
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, Knecht Ruprecht is not a companion of St. Nicholas — he is the fragment of the Hunt that lingers after the main host has passed. He arrives where the Hunt has been. He is the Hunt's wake, its residue, its last trailing edge. His rod is not punishment for bad behavior: it is the last whip-crack of the Hunt's passage, the wind after the horses, the sound that arrives when the horses are already gone. Children who hear him are children who heard the Hunt and survived it; he comes to see if the survival was earned.

**Lampblack Residue:** The staff or rod. The ragged cloak with soot on it — the chimney-soot of the first tradition, preserved here as Hunt-residue. The question he always asks: *Can you say your prayers?* The question is not about religion. The question is about whether you know how to address the dead correctly.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** His name in this Pane: *Ruprecht der Nachgänger* — Ruprecht the After-Goer, the one who follows where the Hunt has already been. He comes second. That is worse. The Hunt arrives and passes; Ruprecht arrives and stays until he has finished his accounting.

**Art-direction note:** A figure seen from behind, receding down a snow-lane at night; the deep impression of horse-hooves in the snow ahead of him, already beginning to fill with new snow; his rod leaving a single line in the snow as he drags it; the lane ending in fog; no source of the hoofprints visible.

**Flavor text:** *"The Hunt left an hour ago. I am what they left behind to see if anyone is still watching."*

*Voice note: Archetype A10 (The Last Word) — the figure speaks in his own voice; the statement is complete and unembellished; the horror is in what the watching means.*

### MOMENT 05 — Ghaidabhak (The Goat-That-Brings-Back)
**Pane:** Pre-Vedic  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** In the Pre-Vedic Pane the Yule Goat is not a Scandinavian straw ornament and not merely Thor's chariot-beast — he is a vestige of the great goat-and-cattle traditions of the Indo-European steppe, the animal whose slaughter and resurrection is not a Norse peculiarity but an ancient theology. Tanngrisnir and Tanngnjóstr, yes — but in this Pane the goat also carries the sacred fire through the dark months, a Mithraic agni-rite survival: the warmth of the sun carried on the back of an animal that does not know it is carrying it. He is shaggy and immense and he smells of the deep cold. The straw ornaments are memorials to what he was.

**Lampblack Residue:** The horns that carry light or fire. The straw wound around the neck — the ornament that is both decoration and documentation of a much older thing. The death-and-resurrection cycle embedded in the same body.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** In the Pre-Vedic Pane, the goat's name comes from the Proto-Indo-European root *\*ghaid-* (goat/kid), rendered in the game as *Ghaidabhak* — the goat-that-brings-back; the compound names both what he is and what he does.

**Art-direction note:** A massive shaggy goat in deep snow at night; a lit torch somehow lodged in or wound with his horns, burning steadily despite the weather; his coloring ancient amber-and-ochre; both Norse geometric patterns and Vedic angular designs worked into the straw ornament bound at his neck; his breath visible in the cold air, enormous.

**Flavor text:** *"The goat does not know it is carrying fire. That is what makes it holy."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — the Pre-Vedic cattle-priest speaking from the tradition; the theological precision is in the unknowing, which is the condition of the sacred vessel.*

### MOMENT 06 — Studen Djed (Grandfather Cold)
**Pane:** Old Ones Persist  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** In the Old Ones Persist Pane, Father Winter is not a Slavic folk figure with Soviet-era trappings — he is older than Slavic Christianity, older than the Slavic people, older than the word *moroz* (frost) that later epithets gave him. He is the personification of cold itself as a primordial force: something that preceded the gods, that was here before Perun raised his hammer, that will be here after. He does not arrive. He approaches. The cold comes before him, and the cold is not his power — the cold is his body, moving.

**Lampblack Residue:** The white beard. The staff. The cold that moves faster than he does — you feel it before you see him, and you see him before you understand what you are seeing.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** His name in this Pane is *Studen Djed* — Grandfather Cold — rooted in Proto-Slavic *\*studenь* (cold/well/source). The *Moroz* (frost) epithet came later, during the Christianization period; in the Old Ones Persist Pane, he predates it and does not answer to it. He is not the frost. He is what makes the frost.

**Art-direction note:** An enormous figure in ice-blue robes seen at vast distance across a frozen steppe at dusk; the horizon itself appears to curve slightly under his approach; no Snegurochka beside him; purely elemental and purely alone; a faint distortion in the air ahead of him where the cold precedes him.

**Flavor text:** *"He was here before the gods named him. He will be here after the gods forget his name. He does not mind."*

*Voice note: Archetype A2 (Prophecy) — stated in past tense for a future fact; the future is not in doubt; the card is the moment the prophecy is confirmed.*

### MOMENT 07 — Krampen
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, Krampus is not a companion of St. Nicholas — he is a Hunt-beast who broke free of the procession. The chains are the Hunt's chains: the ones used to drag the reluctant dead along behind the riders. He pulled himself loose. He wanders now, the Hunt-smell still on him, finding the bad children not to punish them but because their fear-smell is the closest thing in the settled world to the scent of the Hunt itself, and he is following a scent he cannot put down. He is not cruel. He is lost.

**Lampblack Residue:** The chains, still trailing. The birch-rod he carries because the Hunt-host carried them. The horns that mark him as something that was once bound. The enormous tongue — the Hunt-hound's tongue, still tasting the air for what it was chasing.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** In this Pane, the name is *Krampen* — the cramping thing, the clutching thing, from the same root as "cramp," the involuntary seizing of a muscle. He grabs without meaning to. The chains drag behind him and catch on things and he does not notice until something has been pulled over.

**Art-direction note:** A massive horned figure in motion through a snowfield at night, chains trailing across the snow behind him; his posture is not threatening but confused — head up, scenting the air, looking around as if a sound has stopped and he is trying to locate where it came from; a single child's woolen boot in his basket, left there accidentally.

**Flavor text:** *"He doesn't want the children. He wants the Hunt. They are the closest thing to it that still runs."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — an observer who has understood something about Krampen that Krampen has not understood about himself; the pity in this is the card's spine.*

### MOMENT 08 — Pelznichol (The Fur-Nicholas)
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** In the Pennsylvania Dutch communities of the Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane, Belsnickel arrived before any other winter figure and will outlast all of them. He is German-American, fur-clad from the old country — actual pelts, not a bishop's vestments — carrying switches in one hand and candy in the other. He does not come to reward the good: he comes to test everyone. He tests children. He tests adults. He is not jolly and does not pretend to be. He comes to the door and waits until it is opened, and the opening of the door means you have agreed to the test.

**Lampblack Residue:** The fur coat — the un-ecclesiastical form of the Nicholas figure, the version before Christianization finished its work. The two hands: one with candy, one with switches; the choice has not been made yet when he arrives, and it will not be made until he sees you.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** In this Pane, *Pelznichol* — the full historical name-form: *Pelz* (fur) + *Nichol* (Nicholas). The fur-Nicholas, the wild form of the bishop, the one that remembers what the bishop was before the mitre. The name carries the Wodan substrate more clearly than the Swiss or Dutch forms.

**Art-direction note:** A gaunt figure in rough animal-pelts at the door of a Pennsylvania farmhouse in deep winter; a lantern held up at face-level; Pennsylvania Dutch decorative elements on the door (a hex-sign star, dried-herb bundles); his face weather-burned and entirely assessing; both hands visible, one holding switches bound with cord, one holding a twist of hard candy; the candy hand is behind his back.

**Flavor text:** *"He doesn't leave gifts. He leaves the memory of the test. You carry that all year too."*

*Voice note: Archetype A4 (The Curse) inflected as a folk-observation — the binding is not spoken by him but about him; the mechanism is the memory, which is the gift he actually gives.*

### MOMENT 09 — La Befanina
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** The Italian witch of Epiphany. She sweeps the floor before she goes — sweeping out the old year's residue, the mess of twelve months, everything that should not cross into the new one. She leaves gifts in stockings on January 5, coal for bad children, but she does not leave a note. She smells of woodsmoke and fresh bread. In the Lattice-Standard Pane she is older than the Christian feast she has been attached to — she is the earth-crone who attends the death-and-rebirth of the year at its actual hinge point, the Epiphany threshold, the moment the Twelve Days finally close.

**Lampblack Residue:** The broom — both transport and ceremonial sweeper of the old year; the same broom used for both purposes. The soot on her face from the chimney-descent. The stocking. January, not December.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Befanina* in the oldest Italian dialect forms — a diminutive that appears to understate her. She is not a diminutive figure. The diminutive is what you call something when you are trying to make it sound less serious than it is. She has been called Befanina for centuries and has found it more amusing every year.

**Art-direction note:** An old woman on a worn broomstick against a clear January night sky; the three Magi's star visible behind her, left, receding; she is flying in the opposite direction from where it points; her broom leaves a faint streak of soot across the starfield; below her, an Italian hill town with lit windows.

**Flavor text:** *"The Magi followed the star. She followed the street. She got there first."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — a second-rank observer who has noticed the thing that the official account missed; the observation is the card's claim.*

### MOMENT 10 — Tomtegubbe
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** The farm-spirit who lives under the floorboards. Ancient beyond the age of the farm. He is the spirit of the first man who broke this specific ground — he died and he stayed, because the farm was not finished and he could not leave unfinished work. He protects the farm because the farm is him: the fields are his muscles, the animals are his breath, the roof is his promise. In the Lattice-Standard Pane, the Tomte is not a small gnome in a red hat distributing gifts. He is an ancestor who chose not to depart. If you feed him porridge at Christmas, you are feeding the dead. They are grateful for the porridge.

**Lampblack Residue:** The red cap — its color is not a costume but the color of living blood, which he no longer has; he wears it to remember. The porridge-bowl on Christmas Eve. The farmhouse foundation, which is also his skeleton.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Tomtegubbe* — the old man of the homestead, the full form rarely used because it emphasizes age in a way that makes people uncomfortable. He is older than the family. He is older than the house. The house is the youngest thing about him.

**Art-direction note:** A small grey-bearded figure seated at a Swedish farmhouse table on a Christmas Eve, looking down at a bowl of porridge with one pat of butter on it — the butter is correct; he has checked; his posture is one of relief; through the wide-plank floorboards beneath him, the faint visible outline of old bones in the earth.

**Flavor text:** *"The porridge isn't for him. The porridge is so he remembers that you remember him. The difference is important."*

*Voice note: Archetype A5 (Riddle/Apple-Test) — a distinction offered where the right answer costs the chooser a comfortable misunderstanding; the card is the moment you understand why the distinction matters.*

### MOMENT 11 — Maryferch Llwyd
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** The horse skull on the pole. The ribbons and the bells. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, the Mari Lwyd is the skull of a Hunt-horse — the horse that died under its rider during the passage of the Hunt and was left at the boundary between the Hunt's world and the living's. The boundary-people found it in the morning and wrapped it in ribbons because a thing that terrible needed something human placed over it. The ribbons did not make it bearable. It is not bearable. It knocks on your door now and challenges you to a battle of verse. If you lose, you let it in. You should not let it in. But you should not lose, either.

**Lampblack Residue:** The horse skull. The ribbons, which are the same colors as the mourning-wreaths on the house's door. The verse-battle — *pwnco* — which is a ritual negotiation between the living and the dead at a threshold that the dead are pressing against. January, the space after Christmas, the Twelve Days closing.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Maryferch Llwyd* in this Pane — "Mary daughter of the Gray" — giving her a proper Welsh name with lineage and patronymic, where in the standard tradition she is merely a noun (Gray Mare). She has lineage. That is worse. A noun cannot be owed anything. A person with a father can be owed everything.

**Art-direction note:** A Welsh stone farmhouse on a dark January night; a figure carrying a white-sheeted pole topped with a decorated horse skull, ribbons streaming in the wind, glass eyes catching the torchlight; the skull's jaw hinged open; the ribbons are blue-and-black, matching mourning-wreaths on the door-lintel; inside, through a gap in the shutters, the light of people scrambling for an answer.

**Flavor text:** *"The verse-battle is not a game. It is a negotiation with the dead. Win, and you keep the night."*

*Voice note: Archetype A4 (The Curse) — a binding in force; the mechanism (the verse-battle) is named precisely; the consequence of loss is implied without being stated.*

### MOMENT 12 — The Hoodening Contract
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** The East Kent equivalent of the Mari Lwyd. The Hooden Horse: a snap-jaw horse-skull on a pole, carried through villages at midwinter, accompanied by a rider in tattered Hunt-colors and a jester whose job is to keep the proceedings from becoming entirely unbearable. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, the rider is the dead Hunt-master returning to claim what the village owes; the jester is the only one who can be funny about it; and the snap of the horse's jaw is not for show. The Hoodening is not a folk custom in this Pane. It is the annual discharge of an old contract.

**Lampblack Residue:** The snapping jaw — the death-year's teeth, closing on what it takes. The jester who walks between the skull and the door; humor is the only fence that holds. The horse's teeth, which are real.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** In this Pane, "the Hoodening" is not a tradition but a civic obligation — the village has a contract with the dead, and the Hooden Horse procession is the annual evidence that the contract is being honored. The word "hooden" carries in this Pane both the "wooden" etymology and the "Wodan" etymology, and everyone in Kent knows both and does not choose between them.

**Art-direction note:** An East Kent village at midwinter dusk; a snap-jaw horse skull on a pole with a rider in parchment-pale Hunt-colors behind it and a jester ahead of it, playing a small squeaky instrument with extraordinary concentration; every window in the street has a lit candle in it; the doors are all closed; everyone is watching.

**Flavor text:** *"The jester is there so that the horse doesn't take the whole night. Humor is the only fence against what the horse wants."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — someone watching from behind a closed shutter who has understood the jester's function in the cosmological mechanics; the line is affectionate and afraid simultaneously.*

### MOMENT 13 — Le Père Fouet
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** The French "Father Whipper" — the butcher who killed three children, was caught by St. Nicholas, condemned to eternal service as the punishing companion of the saint, carrying forever the implements of his crime. In the Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane, the penance is not finished and will not be finished. The switch he carries is not a birch rod: it is his own guilt, externalized and bundled. He follows the bishop's canal-boat at a long rope's distance. He does not speak to the bishop. He does not speak to the children. He shows them what he carries and lets them understand.

**Lampblack Residue:** The dark cloak. The bundle of switches — which are also the permanent debt he carries; the crime is not behind him, it is in his hands. The rope tied to the prow of the bishop's boat.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Le Père Fouet* in older French — "Father Whip" without the affectionate *-ard* suffix. The suffix implies a familiarity with the thing named, a domestication of it. Something as old and unredeemed as this should not be familiar. The *-ard* came later; in this Pane, it has not arrived.

**Art-direction note:** A stooped black-cloaked figure at the far end of a long rope tied to the stern of Sintereclaus's canal-boat; the canal black and still; the bishop's lantern visible ahead at the prow, far away; the figure's bundle of switches dragging in the dark water, leaving a wake; his face not visible; his posture neither threatening nor humble — simply weighted.

**Flavor text:** *"He is not punishing the children. He is punishing himself, and the children are watching."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — the canal-side observer who has understood the theology of the penance more clearly than the penance's subject has; the watching children are also the card's subject.*

### MOMENT 14 — Hulda (Lady of the Well)
**Pane:** Old Ones Persist  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** In the Old Ones Persist Pane, Frau Holle is not a fairy-tale figure supervising spinning-girls. She is a goddess of the Germanic lands who predates Christianity by millennia and predates the Brothers Grimm by considerably more. She shakes her featherbed to make snow. She receives the dead — specifically the dead women, the dead mothers, the dead spinners, the souls of children who died unbaptized. Her realm is reached through a well. When it snows, the dead are dreaming beneath the ground in her care, and she is making them comfortable, and the snow is a side effect of their breath.

**Lampblack Residue:** The featherbed — the snow is not a power, it is a by-product of domestic labor; she is not making snow, she is making beds. The well as portal. The spinning connection. The dual form: beautiful in one aspect, terrible in the other; the same face at different seasons.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Hulda* in the older name-form — from a root meaning "hidden" rather than "gracious." The *Holda* / *Holle* name-variants carry a grace that is real but secondary; the first name carries what she hides, which is more important than what she shows.

**Art-direction note:** A vast woman lying in a cloud-formation, shaking a featherbed; the snow falling below her is clearly breath-mist from many small sleeping figures visible in the cloud-substance around her; below the snowfall, a German winter landscape with a single farmhouse window lit; everything white except that one window.

**Flavor text:** *"She is not making it snow. She is making the dead comfortable. The snow is a side effect."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — a correction offered by someone who knows more than the standard account; the distinction changes the moral weight of the snow entirely.*

### MOMENT 15 — Berchta of Two Faces
**Pane:** Sinterklaas-Reigns  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** The Alpine terror-crone. She arrives on January 5. She inspects your spinning. She inspects your house, your stores, your distaff, your larder. She checks whether the work of the long dark months has been done correctly, whether the domestic contract has been honored, whether the winter was survived through actual effort or by waiting it out. If you have not finished your spinning by Twelfth Night, she opens your belly and replaces your organs with straw and stones, and she sews you back up. In the Sinterklaas-Reigns Pane, this is not metaphorical. The night is called *Berchtennacht* and the village knows what it means.

**Lampblack Residue:** The knife that is also the inspector's tool. The straw, which is the evidence of idleness. January 5 — the final night of the Twelve Days, the last chance to have done the work.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Berchta* in the Bavarian high-register form — from *\*beraht* (bright/shining); the most precisely ironic of her name-forms, since she is the darkest figure of the season. She carries the name Bright. She arrives in darkness. The name is the one she was given before anyone had met her.

**Art-direction note:** An Alpine farmhouse interior on January 5; a woman standing before an unfinished distaff with one beautiful face (the Schönpercht aspect) and one ancient-terrible face (the Schiachpercht aspect) — both visible simultaneously, as if the card cannot decide which one to show; her hands are too large for her sleeves; her apron has old stains; the firelight is adequate; the distaff thread is not finished.

**Flavor text:** *"She is not cruel. She is accountable. There is a difference. You should learn it before January 5."*

*Voice note: Archetype A5 (Riddle/Apple-Test) — a distinction stated without decoration; the cost of learning it after January 5 is implied and does not need to be stated.*

### MOMENT 16 — Snezhdeva (Snow-Daughter)
**Pane:** Old Ones Persist  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** In the Old Ones Persist Pane, Snegurochka is not Ded Moroz's granddaughter — she is his daughter, which is the older relationship, the one that carries more weight. She is made of snow. She loves a mortal boy in spring. In spring she melts. This is not a tragedy in the Old Ones Persist Pane — it is the correct function of the world. She knows this. She understands what she is and what that means. She melts willingly and happily, because the alternative is winter not ending, and she would not keep winter from ending even if she could.

**Lampblack Residue:** The blue and white robes — the colors of ice, worn like a decision rather than a costume. The melting in spring. The happiness of it, which is the thing the tragedy-reading misses.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Snezhdeva* — a constructed Slavic name meaning "Snow-daughter" (*snezh-* = snow, *-deva* = daughter/maiden), closer to the older folk root than the *Snegurochka* diminutive that Ostrovsky crystallized. The diminutive makes her small and dear; the older form makes her elemental.

**Art-direction note:** A girl made of pale blue-white ice standing in the first spring sunshine with her face turned up to it; her edges are softening and beginning to run; she is holding a single spring flower, also made of ice, also beginning to melt; she is smiling; the happiness is real.

**Flavor text:** *"She doesn't regret the spring. She is the spring. You cannot regret being what you are."*

*Voice note: Archetype A2 (Prophecy) — the future of her melting stated as an accomplished truth; the card is the moment the prophecy is confirmed; the happiness is the turn that makes it Tier I rather than a tragedy card.*

### MOMENT 17 — The Kallikantzariki (The Small Sawyers)
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard  
**Tier:** II (Echo)  
**Rarity:** Uncommon

**Figure's nature:** The Greek Christmas goblins who emerge from underground during the Twelve Days, December 25 to January 6. They have been sawing at the roots of the World Tree for the entire year. By Christmas they have nearly cut through — but then the Twelve Days arrive and they must surface, and while they are above ground causing mischief, the roots heal. When Epiphany comes and they must return underground, the tree is whole and they must start again. Every year they almost destroy the world. Every year they are tricked back underground just in time. They do not know this yet. They will not know this.

**Lampblack Residue:** The scraping, sawing sound from underground during December — the Twelve Days are the gap in which the sawing stops. The tiny saws. The emerging from limestone caves and well-mouths.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Kallikantzariki* — the diminutive plural form in modern Greek, which makes them sound less dangerous. They are not less dangerous. They are only small and confused. The diminutive is what you use for things you have learned to live with, not for things that have become safe.

**Art-direction note:** An Aegean village at Christmas; the cobblestone square lit with candles and Christmas lights; through a crack in the limestone paving, two small dark figures visible below, still holding their tiny crosscut saws — they are looking up at the Christmas lights through the crack with expressions of absolute confusion; one of them is pointing at a string of colored bulbs and conferring with the other about what it is.

**Flavor text:** *"They don't understand Christmas. They understand sawing. They are very good at sawing."*

*Voice note: Archetype A9 (The Wisecrack-That-Lands) — the humor is real; the cold thought arrives one second later when you remember what they are sawing and why they are good at it.*

### MOMENT 18 — Baboushenka (The One Who Searches)
**Pane:** Lattice-Standard  
**Tier:** I (Surface)  
**Rarity:** Rare

**Figure's nature:** The Russian old woman who did not follow the Magi. She was too busy cleaning her house. She has been searching ever since, leaving gifts at every house where a child sleeps in case this one is the child she missed. In the Lattice-Standard Pane she has been searching for long enough that she no longer quite knows what she is looking for — the image has blurred with the searching. She does not stop. She has learned that the searching is the point: each gift left on a doorstep is not a failure (it was not the right child) but a success (a child received a gift tonight).

**Lampblack Residue:** The shawl. The sack of small gifts. The doorstep in the dark, the wrapped package left without knocking, the moving on to the next house without looking back. The searching, which is also the giving.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Baboushenka* — the affectionate Russian diminutive of *baboushka* (grandmother), used for grandmothers you know and love personally, not for figures from legends. It makes her specific and near rather than distant and archetypal. She is your grandmother, searching in the snow, which is more important and more moving than a legend searching in the snow.

**Art-direction note:** An old Russian woman in dark winter clothes leaving a small wrapped package on a doorstep in heavy snowfall; every door visible on the narrow street has a small package on its step, already snow-dusted; she is halfway to the next door; she is not looking back at any of them; the street goes on beyond the frame.

**Flavor text:** *"She knows it isn't the right child. She leaves something anyway. That is the point."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — an observer who has followed her long enough to understand the theology of the searching; the last sentence is the card's spine and its deepest claim.*

### MOMENT 19 — Iólnir (The Yule-Figure in Disguise)
**Pane:** Wild Hunt Eternal  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** *Jólnir* is one of Odin's *heiti* — his winter identity, the name he holds when he rides in disguise through the dark of the year. He is the origin of every gift-giver in Europe before the Christian saint erased him: the old man in the broad-brimmed hat and gray cloak who arrives at farmhouse doors during the Twelve Nights and asks for hospitality. If you feed him, the year turns. If you do not, you have refused the king of the dead at his most civil. In the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane, the Hunt rides by night and Iólnir walks by day; he is both the hunter and the hospitality-tester, and the test is always whether you know which one you are hosting.

**Lampblack Residue:** The one eye, or the eye covered. The wide-brimmed hat. The gray cloak. The disguise — he does not tell them who he is, because if he had to tell them, it would not count. The distant sound of the Hunt's horn, audible just past the edge of the frame.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** *Iólnir* in the runic orthography with the Germanic *Io-* prefix form — slightly older than the Old Norse *Jólnir*, carrying the Proto-Germanic substrate closer to the surface. Art direction note: do NOT use runic typography as decorative framing in isolation — if runes appear they must appear as part of a broader tapestry of sacred scripts, per CLUSTER-B §7.

**Art-direction note:** A tall old man at the door of a Viking-age longhouse in deep midwinter; a pilgrim's broad-brimmed hat casting shadow over his face; a gray cloak, staff in hand; one eye is in shadow or turned away; the farmstead behind him snow-bound; the Hunt's distant horn-sound must be suggested in the visual — perhaps as distant shapes in the sky just at the horizon, barely visible; his breath in the cold air is enormous, a horse's breath-cloud.

**Flavor text:** *"He does not tell them who he is. If he had to tell them, it would not count."*

*Voice note: Archetype A11 (Anonymous Witness) — the card's narrator has understood the rule of the hospitality test; the line states the theology of divine disguise in thirteen words; the card is the moment someone either understands this or does not.*

### MOMENT 20 — The Long Night Itself
**Pane:** All Panes (cross-Pane card)  
**Tier:** III (Deep)  
**Rarity:** Legendary

**Figure's nature:** A cross-Pane card — the only Moment in this Set that exists in all five Panes simultaneously. The Long Night is not a figure. It is the condition. In every Pane, at the dark of the year, the same thing is true: the sun has withdrawn; the boundaries between the living and the dead are thin; something will come to the door; and whether you call it Father Christmas or Ded Moroz or the Wild Hunt or Perchta, what you are doing when you leave the porridge out and open the door and answer the verse-battle and finish the spinning is the same thing. You are making it through. The Long Night has always been the same. What changes is who comes to the door.

**Lampblack Residue:** The darkness. The waiting. The door — always *the* door, never *a* door. The warmth inside, which is specific and particular. The darkness outside, which is general and has shapes in it.

**Pratchett-grade tweak:** This card has no name beyond the Set title. It has a symbol: an open door with warmth inside and darkness outside, and a figure at the threshold that is not yet defined — the figure is the collector's, assembled from the other nineteen cards.

**Art-direction note:** An illuminated door in absolute darkness; the warmth from inside falls through the open door as specific golden light, falling on a specific threshold-stone; the darkness outside is not uniform — it has the barely-visible shapes of every figure in this Set, suggested rather than depicted, layered and translucent; the figure at the threshold is a silhouette only, facing inward, undefinable as to Pane; the darkness is present and waiting. This card must not depict any single cultural tradition's winter figure; the darkness contains all of them.

**Sensitivity note:** Cross-Pane abstract card. No single tradition's iconography should dominate. The darkness contains multitudes. Art director must approve final comp against this criterion before print.

**Flavor text:** *"The Long Night is the same in every world. What changes is who comes to the door."*

*Voice note: Archetype A10 (The Last Word) for a Set — the final statement, complete and unembellished, that retrospectively frames the other nineteen cards. The attribution is the Set itself.*

### Cross-Set Connections

**Iólnir (M19)** connects to the Wild Hunt Set (planned): he is both the gift-giver and the Hunt-leader; both cards show the same figure; the Lampblack Residue (the hat, the one covered eye, the disguise) links them across Sets. Collector who holds both reads them as a chord: the disguise is the Hunt.

**Studen Djed (M06)** connects to the Old Ones Persist Set (planned): Father Winter in this Set is a winter figure; in the Old Ones Persist Set he will appear as a cosmological actor in the Perun-conflict narrative. The figure's nature does not change; the frame around him does.

**Ghaidabhak (M05)** connects to the Pre-Vedic Pane's cattle-cult thread (planned): the death-and-resurrection of the sacrificial goat links to the Mithraic Yalda-night narrative (the unconquered sun born at the solstice) and to the broader Indo-European sacrificial theology. The goat carries the fire; the fire is the sun; the sun returns.

**Maryferch Llwyd (M11) and The Hoodening Contract (M12)** connect to Gwyn ap Nudd (planned Wild Hunt Set): the horse-skull processions are fragments of the Hunt's passage; a Wild Hunt Set card showing the Hunt in full should carry a Lampblack echo of the horse-skull's ribbons.

**Tomtegubbe (M10)** connects to the Household Spirit Set (planned): the Tomte, Domovoi, Brownie, and Nisse share the same Lampblack residue (the ancestor in the foundation, the porridge, the farm as the ancestor's body). The Long Night Set shows the Tomte at Christmas specifically; the Household Spirit Set will show him at other moments of the calendar.

### Cultural Sensitivity Flags

**Zwarte Piet / Blackface Imagery:** EXCLUDED from this Set in full. The dark-companion archetype is accessed through Schmutzlein (Swiss sooty-chimney tradition, no racial connotation), Le Père Fouet (condemned butcher), and Ruprecht der Nachgänger (Hunt-remnant). All art direction for these cards must be reviewed against the CLUSTER-K §7 and CLUSTER-J §7 sensitivity gates before commissioning.

**Sinterklaas figures:** Rooted in pre-modern Dutch, Swiss, and German tradition. No connection to the modern Dutch Sinterklaas controversy is made or implied. Schmutzlein's sooty appearance is chimney-soot, not caricature — this must be unambiguous in all art direction briefs and must be re-confirmed at art review stage.

**Norse / Germanic imagery and white nationalism:** Per CLUSTER-B §7: Iólnir (M19) must appear in a set alongside figures from multiple other traditions; the Set architecture satisfies this. Art direction for M19 must not use runic typography as decorative framing in isolation. The Lampblack residues linking Iólnir to the gift-giver tradition (the hat, the disguise, the hospitality test) should be the visual language, not runic symbols.

**M20 (Cross-Pane):** Must not depict any single cultural tradition's winter figure. Art director must confirm this at comp-approval stage.

**Living traditions:** No Hopi Soyal, no Kachina imagery, no other living indigenous ceremonial content appears in this Set. The Set is limited to European folk-survival traditions and their cosmological-variant Pane re-axiomings.

**Holly King / Oak King formulation:** Not used in this Set. Where seasonal-combat archetype is relevant (Father Christmas, Studen Djed), it is accessed through the attested folk figures, not through the Graves formulation.

### Research Flags for Canon Council

1. **The Kallikantzaroi (M17)** are assigned to the Lattice-Standard Pane on the basis of CLUSTER-K's recommendation. A Wild Hunt Eternal Pane framing is possible (the Kallikantzaroi as the subterranean complement to the aerial Hunt) but was not used here to avoid over-saturating the Wild Hunt Eternal Pane. Canon Council may wish to reconsider.

2. **La Befana / Strenia alternate placement** (noted in CLUSTER-K §8): if M09 is re-assigned to Pre-Vedic, the card's flavor text and Pratchett tweak would need to be revised to foreground the Strenia-origin reading rather than the Italian folk-Epiphany reading. The current Lattice-Standard framing is the richer folk card; the Pre-Vedic framing would be the more mythologically aggressive card. Both are valid.

3. **Père Fouettard vs. Père Fouet placement:** CLUSTER-K §8 recommends Wild Hunt Eternal Pane as primary for this figure. This Set places him in Sinterklaas-Reigns because the canal-boat composition (M02) creates a stronger visual-thematic link. If a Wild Hunt Set is produced, Père Fouettard should appear there as a separate card — the two readings (condemned butcher in eternal service vs. Hunt-hound at the saint's heel) are genuinely distinct and both merit cards.

4. **The Cailleach Bheur** (CLUSTER-J, CLUSTER-B) does not appear in this Set. She is the most important Celtic winter-conflict figure and the adversarial cosmological power against which all gift-bringers are measured (per CLUSTER-K §3). Her absence is noted. She belongs in the Old Ones Persist Pane and should appear in either a supplemental card for this Set or as a priority card in the planned Old Ones Persist Set.
