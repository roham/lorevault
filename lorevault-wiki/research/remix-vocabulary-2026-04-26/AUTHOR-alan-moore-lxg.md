# Remix-Vocabulary Dossier: Alan Moore (League of Extraordinary Gentlemen)
## The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen — All Volumes and Spin-offs

**Prepared for:** LoreVault Multi-Pantheon Pane Architecture  
**Date:** 2026-04-26  
**Scope:** Works analyzed — *The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen* Vol. 1 (1999–2000), Vol. 2 (2002–2003), *The Black Dossier* (2007), *Century: 1910*, *1969*, *2009* (2009–2012), the Nemo Trilogy (2013–2015), *The Tempest* (2018–2019); with supporting reference to Jess Nevins' annotation companions *Heroes & Monsters* (2003) and *A Blazing World* (2004), and *Impossible Territories* (2011).

---

## 1. Author Overview

Alan Moore was born in 1953 in Northampton, England, into a working-class family. Self-educated, a practicing occultist — he dates his decision to become a ceremonial magician with peculiar precision to a drunken announcement on his fortieth birthday — his theory of fiction and his theory of magic are the same theory. "The membrane which exists between reality and fiction is a semi-permeable one," he has stated, "and it is by this leakage from the imaginary that our whole human world has come into being." Treating fictional characters as real entities in a shared world is not a game; for Moore it is a metaphysical position.

*Swamp Thing* (1984–1987), *Watchmen* (1986–1987), and *V for Vendetta* established him as the dominant formalist in comics — the writer who could fold literary structure, research density, and political argument into sequential panels. By the late 1990s, having fallen out catastrophically with DC over character rights, he launched America's Best Comics with an explicit intention to create work that was genuinely progressive without being merely transgressive.

*The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen* debuted in 1999 as his most ambitious project. Moore's research process is documented via the Jess Nevins annotation project: a librarian and Victorian literature specialist, Nevins posted exhaustive page-by-page annotations online, later expanding them into companion volumes with Moore's blessing. Moore acknowledged that Nevins' research informed the construction of the *New Traveller's Almanac*. For Vol. 1 alone, Nevins devoted over 5,000 words to the first twelve pages. Moore's own account confirms the governing rule: around the second issue he decided that "any character that's mentioned by name had got to be a real character from fiction" — and retrofitted the universe backward from that axiom. He identified Victorian literary fiction as "the actual point of generation of the superhero genre." The Hulk is Jekyll/Hyde. Sue Storm is Wells' Invisible Man. The X-Men are Moreau's beast-people. Moore wanted the originals, not the shadows.

## 2. Catalog of Remix-Relevant Works

**The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen, Vol. 1 (1999–2000)** — Victorian England, 1898: British Intelligence assembles Mina Murray (Dracula's survivor), Allan Quatermain (Haggard's Kenya hunter, now an opium ruin), Captain Nemo (Verne's anti-colonial submariner), Hawley Griffin (Wells' Invisible Man), and Dr. Jekyll/Hyde against the crime syndicate of the Yellow Peril archetype implied to be Fu Manchu, staging the theft of a Cavorite substance and threatening London.

**The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen, Vol. 2 (2002–2003)** — 1898 continues: the Martian tripod invasion from Wells' *War of the Worlds* arrives; Nemo grows increasingly horrified by British military desperation; Hyde becomes the League's most destructive weapon; the climax literalizes the monster as the only entity capable of genuine sacrifice.

**The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen: The Black Dossier (2007)** — Set in 1958 in a post-*1984* Ingsoc England that has recently collapsed; Mina and Allan steal the League's secret history — a dossier composed entirely of fake literary documents — and flee toward the Blazing World; the book's form is the content.

**Century: 1910 (2009)** — Captain Nemo dies, his daughter Janni refuses his legacy, is raped in London's docklands, and is reborn as a pirate captain; simultaneously an occultist named Oliver Haddo (Maugham's Aleister Crowley surrogate) begins a ritual to birth an Antichrist; the chapter is structured around Brecht/Weill's *Threepenny Opera*.

**Century: 1969 (2010)** — The Haddo cult murders a rock musician; Mina battles Haddo's transferred spirit on the astral plane and is institutionalized; the 1960s counterculture is processed through the filter of fictional Swinging London; the Antichrist ritual comes closer.

**Century: 2009 (2012)** — The Antichrist, revealed as a Harry Potter analogue manipulated since childhood by Haddo-as-Voldemort, destroys the fictional school and triggers Armageddon; a figure unmistakably Mary Poppins descends, turns him into a chalk drawing, and washes him away in the rain.

**Nemo: Heart of Ice (2013)** — 1925: Janni Nemo leads an expedition to Antarctica drawn from Lovecraft's *At the Mountains of Madness*, encountering Elder Things and the ruins of the Miskatonic expedition.

**Nemo: The Roses of Berlin (1941) (2014)** — Janni and her husband are captured in a Berlin ruled by characters from Fritz Lang's *Metropolis*, Chaplin's *The Great Dictator*, and Nazi science-fiction pulp.

**Nemo: River of Ghosts (1975) (2015)** — An 80-year-old Janni makes a final journey into the Amazon, fighting the legacy of Ayesha and confronting the toll of a life lived at the intersection of fictional violence and real grief.

**The Tempest (2018–2019)** — Moore and O'Neill's declared final work; Prospero returns; the series turns metafictional, interrogating the nature of fictional characters and the act of storytelling itself; each issue is formatted as a different era of British comics history.

## 3. Source-Mining Technique

Moore's method is systematic archaeological excavation through the strata of Anglophone (and to a lesser extent French, German, and Russian) popular fiction from roughly 1660 to 1960, with a weighting toward the 1880–1920 seam he identified as the origin layer of modern popular genre.

**The governing rule:** From the second issue onward, every named character in LXG is a character from existing fiction. This is not just a surface policy. Moore extended it into crowd scenes. Background figures in Victorian London street panels are visually designed as ancestors of *EastEnders* cast members — a joke so embedded that only a viewer who had watched the soap opera for decades would catch it. A dominatrix operating a school in Vol. 1 is Rosa Coote, drawn from Victorian erotic literature. Inspector Dick Donovan, who appears briefly, is from a specific strand of Victorian detective fiction entirely extinct in popular memory.

**The ratio of famous to obscure:** Vol. 1 operates at roughly 60/40 — famous characters anchoring the plot, the background populated with deep cuts. By the *Black Dossier* and *Century*, the ratio inverts: the famous characters become the background. The ratio shift marks the point where Moore's pleasure in the research begins to outpace his investment in story.

**The Dictionary of Imaginary Places as source tool:** Alberto Manguel and Gianni Guadalupi's *The Dictionary of Imaginary Places* (1980, expanded 1987), which catalogues over 1,200 fictional locations from literature worldwide, was a key resource for the *New Traveller's Almanac* — the 46-page prose supplement appended to Vol. 2's collected edition, covering the British Isles, continental Europe, the Americas, Africa, Asia, and the polar regions, each section dense with locations from literary sources, including works never translated into English. The Almanac is a paracartography of pure fiction rendered as if it were a Victorian traveler's handbook.

**Kevin O'Neill's art and the density problem:** O'Neill brought a specific visual grammar: dense, cross-hatched pen work in a style descended partly from 2000 AD, partly from Victorian wood-engraving. His panels reward multiple readings. A single exterior shot of a London street contains visual identifiers — a hat shape, a walking posture, a shop sign — that identify specific fictional characters to readers who know the period. This is the visual equivalent of Moore's naming rule: even background texture is coded. The limitation is that the density requires the reader to bring significant prior knowledge. The average reader gets a compelling adventure story; the specialist gets a second, richer, parallel narrative running beneath it.

## 4. Anchoring Move

Moore's solution to the instability of a story set in a shared universe where every fictional world coexists is to anchor the narrative in specific, unglamorous Victorian physical texture. The first six pages of Vol. 1 are not action: they are the smell of the opium den in Cairo where Mina Murray finds a wrecked Allan Quatermain, the legal formality of Campion Bond's commission, the specific ugliness of Victorian bureaucratic power. The effect is that the fantastical becomes plausible not through explanation but through grounding in material reality.

**The Mina/Quatermain dynamic** anchors the first two volumes. Quatermain is Haggard's character as written: an opium addict, a racist with an awkward conscience, genuinely brave and genuinely broken, attracted to Mina because she competently manages everything he cannot. Mina is established in the first issue as the actual operational leader — she recruits, negotiates, plans, manages Nemo and Hyde. The men defer to her not because Moore writes them as enlightened but because she is visibly the most capable person in every room. She keeps the vampire scar hidden under a red silk scarf: she carries her history without advertising it, and the damage has not reduced her.

**Hyde's physicality** is the other primary anchor. O'Neill draws him as a massive, deformed, animalistic figure whose anatomy is wrong in ways that communicate the wrongness of what he is. The scene in Vol. 2 where Hyde detects the invisible Griffin by smell — infrared and olfactory senses intact where Griffin's invisibility cannot reach them — and then kills him uses the full physicality of O'Neill's draftsmanship. Moore structures it as a triple move: it resolves the Griffin threat, reveals Hyde as defender-through-violence, and implicates the reader in the logic of the monster's protection. Hyde becomes sympathetic not by being cleaned up but by being deployed as the blunt instrument the morally compromised Empire actually requires.

**The Empire setting as context, not backdrop.** The heroes are, from the first page, conscripts of the colonial machine. Moore does not resolve this tension; the series' moral ambiguity is structural, not incidental.

## 5. Subversion Technique

Moore's primary method is not to replace the source character with a new version but to foreground the subtext that previous adaptations systematically suppressed. He restores what the popular imagination edited out.

**Hyde as sympathetic monster.** Stevenson's Hyde is a vehicle for Victorian anxieties about the repressed self, typically rendered as straightforward evil in adaptation. Moore's Hyde knows exactly what he is: "He knows he's a monster," Moore stated in interviews, and that self-awareness is what makes him honorable in the LXG framework. In the climactic scene of Vol. 2, Hyde fights the Martian tripods on London Bridge with his skin burning off from their heat-ray, topples one of the machines, and begins eating the alien inside. When the remaining tripods combine their heat-rays to kill him, he does not run. Before the battle he asks Mina for a kiss — "which she graciously agrees to" — and then, in his own words as rendered by Moore, ends up looking heroic when all he wanted was to slaughter something. The joke is the honesty: Hyde's heroism is entirely incidental to his appetite. He is the most truthful character in a story full of characters performing heroic identities.

**Nemo as anti-colonialist.** Verne's Nemo is frequently read as a romantic individualist; Moore restores his original Indian identity (Prince Dakkar of Bundelkund), his post-Sepoy Rebellion fury, and his ideological refusal to align with British values even while cooperating operationally. His line — "If I work with the British, it is because I no longer feel even Indian. The sea, now, is my only nation" — is the series' most articulate statement of its moral ambiguity. The Nemo succession in *Century: 1910* extends this through Janni: arriving in London as Jenny Diver (the Brecht/Weill character from "Pirate Jenny"), brutalized by the docklands, she accepts the Nautilus not as inheritance but as an instrument of revenge. The ship is painted black. Nemo's skull is nailed to the forecastle.

**Mina as actual competent leader.** Stoker's Mina is intelligent and courageous but functions within a framework that positions her as the object of Dracula's assault and the motive for the men's pursuit. Moore retains her intelligence and courage and strips the framework. In Vol. 1, she gives orders. In Vol. 2, she absorbs the horror of Griffin's assault and continues functioning — not because Moore resolves her trauma but because her operational focus overrides it. The scarf at her throat is the only sustained reference to her victimization; she does not invite the reader's pity. By *Century* she is immortal, has outlived multiple Leagues and multiple Allies, and her exhaustion is the series' primary emotional register.

**The Invisible Man as unambiguous villain.** Griffin's villainy in Wells is diffuse. Moore makes it explicit and early — Griffin is assaulting girls at a school when we first encounter him — and his eventual betrayal to the Martians is entirely consistent. The Invisible Man, who should be the clever morally ambiguous figure, becomes the most despicable League member; his death by Hyde carries no grief.

## 6. Cascade Logic

LXG operates on a specific propagative rule system: establish the premise at the widest possible scope, then follow the implications forward without exception. The system is:

**Step 1:** All Victorian fictional characters coexist in a shared reality. This is stated via the governing rule (every named character must be a real literary character) but never announced within the fiction itself — it is a structural fact, not a plot point.

**Step 2:** If all Victorian fictional characters coexist, the criminal underworld of 1898 London cannot be a generic crime syndicate — it must be a specific competition between existing fictional criminals. The "Dr. M" running British Intelligence's counter-criminal network in Vol. 1 is Moriarty. His antagonist, never named but architecturally implied, is Fu Manchu (still under copyright; Moore signals the identification through context alone, never committing to it in text).

**Step 3:** The Martian invasion of 1898 is not hypothetical but documented. The League's response flows from the specific cast's specific natures: Hyde fights a tripod because he is the only member whose physical capacity exceeds normal human parameters. Plot outcomes are generated by character logic, not by narrative convenience.

**Step 4:** The cascade extends forward through the century. *Century*'s three chapters (1910, 1969, 2009) require that the full library of each decade's fiction coexist in that decade. By 1969, every British spy novel, every piece of kitchen-sink drama, every psychedelic counterculture pulp is simultaneously real. The MI5 operative surveilling the League is identified through spy-fiction visual coding without being named.

**Step 5:** By 2009, the most culturally dominant fictional universe of the early twenty-first century — Harry Potter — must be incorporated as documented fact. Haddo has been inhabiting host bodies for a century, working toward the Antichrist's birth, and has spent decades manipulating a specific child: staging his magical school adventures, creating a theatrical nemesis (Tom, whose middle name is "a marvel," whose last name is "a conundrum" — Tom Marvolo Riddle), manufacturing the heroic mythology the child needs to believe. The entire Potter narrative was Haddo's preparation ritual. The chosen one is the Antichrist. Mary Poppins — positioned as something approximating the divine — turns him into a chalk drawing and washes him away in the rain.

The cascade logic is Moore's most transferable structural technique. It requires only one rule — all fiction in the relevant period is real and simultaneous — and then forces the remixer to follow every implication forward without making exceptions for convenience.

## 7. Deep-Cut Handling

The deep cuts operate at three legibility levels: plot-relevant identifications a specialist can make but a casual reader misses; background/crowd-scene references requiring Nevins' companions or independent knowledge; and structural references built into the book's physical format.

**The Gulliver succession.** The *Black Dossier*'s fake historical documents establish a League lineage stretching back to Elizabethan England. Among listed predecessors: an eighteenth-century League led by Lemuel Gulliver (Swift, 1726) until his death in 1799, and subsequent Gulliver descendants operating in a world where Lilliput, Brobdingnag, and Laputa are treated as documented geography. The cascade logic applied retroactively: if all Victorian fiction is real, then all the fiction that preceded it is equally real. Swift's satire becomes a travel narrative.

**The Prospero founding and the 007 etymology.** The *Black Dossier*'s fake Shakespearean folio positions Prospero (*The Tempest*) as the League's Elizabethan founder, operating in the role historically associated with John Dee. Nevins' annotations identify the payoff: Dee signed his correspondence to Elizabeth with "007." The code number on Campion Bond's lighter in the Dossier descends from Dee/Prospero directly. James Bond — presented through a surrogate named "Jimmy" — is the spiritual inheritor of a line of occult state agents running from the Elizabethan era forward. The Bond parody, with Jimmy as an explicitly brutal, sexually predatory thug, is the Dossier's most sustained satirical target.

**The Blazing World 3D section.** Margaret Cavendish's *The Blazing World* (1666) — one of the earliest works in English that could be called proto-science fiction — is literalized by Moore as the extradimensional realm where the most durable fictional characters abide. The climax of the *Black Dossier* is printed in 3D, requiring glasses physically included with the book. This is not a gimmick: it formally enacts the premise that the Blazing World occupies a different perceptual register from the rest of the narrative. Crucially, characters within the Blazing World are also depicted wearing 3D glasses — they see their own world with the same special attention the reader is being forced to adopt.

**The fictional geography of Britain.** The *New Traveller's Almanac* lists specific literary locations as real geography: Alderley Edge (Alan Garner), Diana's Grove (Stoker's *Lair of the White Worm*), a retirement cottage near Fulworth Cove occupied by "an elderly beekeeper" (Sherlock Holmes, post-"Adventure of the Lion's Mane"), Greyfriars School in Kent, the location later known as the Ministry of Love. Sources span entirely different fictional universes, different authors, different decades — listed adjacently without irony, as a Victorian travel handbook lists counties.

**The Orlando section and Virginia Woolf.** The *Black Dossier* includes a 25-page "Life of Orlando" drawn in the style of a 1950s British children's comic, covering Orlando (from Woolf's 1928 *Orlando: A Biography*) from birth in thirteenth-century Thebes through World War II. Woolf's source is itself a queer love letter to Vita Sackville-West, a queer text disguised as biographical comedy. Moore carries that biographical multiplicity forward through *Century* and beyond; the Woolf connection is the kind of deep cut that, once made, enriches every subsequent appearance of the character.

**The "Bert" figure.** In *Century: 2009*, a chimney sweep who travels between fictional worlds and leads Mina to Mary Poppins is identified through costume and pavement chalk art as Bert from Disney's *Mary Poppins* (1964). The identification is never stated. This is the deepest-cut technique in *Century*: purely visual coding of a corporate-owned character Moore could not name, positioning him within the cascade logic without ever committing to the identification in text.

## 8. What LoreVault Should Learn — Five Transferable Moves

**Move 1: The Public-Domain-as-Shared-Reality axiom.**
Moore's foundational technique is not to assemble public-domain characters for narrative convenience but to assert that they constitute a single shared ontological reality. The assertion is the move. Once you state — as structural fact, not conceit — that all characters from a defined corpus coexist, every subsequent decision follows from that logic rather than from arbitrary creative preference. For LoreVault's Pane model: all public-domain pantheons have always coexisted; their isolation is an artifact of mortal parochialism, not cosmic fact. Place them in a shared geography where their incompatibilities generate productive friction. The cascade logic does the rest.

**Move 2: The governing rule that forces research.**
Moore's rule — every named character must come from existing fiction — is a constraint that forces research and eliminates lazy invention. Every background figure carries a source, a history, implications imported from elsewhere. For LoreVault: every named entity in a Pane must have a documented mythological or literary source. The depth comes for free if the research is done; the constraint is the method.

**Move 3: Encyclopedic density as two-tier readership.**
LXG works as adventure story and as annotatable encyclopedia simultaneously, without conflict, because the layers are sequential: you read the surface to follow the plot; you read the deep cuts to get the full reward. For LoreVault: build content so the surface experience is complete without specialist knowledge, while the deep-cut layer pays off disproportionately for attentive collectors. The specialist who identifies an obscure Vedic minor deity in a Pane's background iconography has a qualitatively different experience from the casual collector — and that difference generates community and collective annotation of the kind Nevins built around LXG.

**Move 4: Subversion through foregrounding suppressed subtext.**
Moore's most repeatable technique: identify what the popular version of a character edits out, then restore it as the primary trait. Nemo is anti-colonial; adaptations made him romantic. Hyde is genuinely monstrous; adaptations domesticated him. Mina is competent; adaptations made her a victim. The source texts contained the subversive readings — Moore merely centered them. For LoreVault: when building from mythological figures flattened by dominant reinterpretation, locate the original, contradictory, unsanitized version. Ares as defender of women and cities. Loki as the necessary condition for cosmological dynamism. The depth is already there.

**Move 5: Cascade logic as architecture.**
One rule, followed without exception. Moore's: all Victorian fiction is simultaneous reality. Every decision that holds to the rule generates authentic-feeling world-density because the density is logical, not decorative. For LoreVault: if all pantheons exist simultaneously in a shared cosmological geography, then conflicts between them are implied by the rule and need only be discovered, not manufactured. The cascade logic means the world writes itself from the axiom forward.

**Move 6: Format as argument; annotator as co-creator.**
The *Black Dossier*'s physical form argues before its content does: 3D glasses, Tijuana bible, Jeeves pastiche, Shakespearean folio — each in its appropriate period format because the container communicates first. For LoreVault: the visual grammar and framing devices of a Pane are not packaging — they are the first layer of world-building. And: Nevins' annotations actively informed subsequent LXG volumes. Sufficient deep-cut density invites community annotation, which is participatory co-creation. Build for it.

## 9. What LoreVault Should Not Do — The Failure Modes

**Failure Mode 1: Annotations become the product.**
The *Black Dossier* sits at the threshold of this failure. Its fake documents — Tijuana bible, folio play, Jeeves story, Beat prose, Orlando comic strip — are brilliant in isolation, but no single reader has the background to appreciate all of them equally. The result is intermittent illumination surrounded by opacity, with the actual plot (Mina and Allan's flight to the Blazing World, which is genuinely urgent) subordinated to the document parade. For LoreVault: encyclopedic depth must serve a navigable experiential core. If the deep-cut layer is the only layer, you have a reference work, not a world.

**Failure Mode 2: References become more important than characters.**
By *Century: 2009*, critics noted that characters as emotional presences had receded behind the reference work. Moore's identification of Potter as a manufactured Antichrist is a genuinely interesting idea; the execution is snide rather than rigorous. Compared to the Bond treatment in the Dossier — where Jimmy's brutality is built into the character's tissue through specific behavioral and visual detail — the Potter treatment is a jab aimed at something Moore dislikes, not a substantive analysis of what Potter *is*. One critic stated it exactly: the work needed to apply to Potter the same imagination and craft it applied elsewhere. It did not. For LoreVault: the operative question is always what does this source reveal that was not visible before — not merely can this source be connected to others. Connection is infrastructure; revelation is the product.

**Failure Mode 3: Pattern becomes policy (the misogyny problem).**
LXG contains three scenes of sexual violence against women — the school assault (Vol. 1), Mina's assault by Griffin (Vol. 2), Janni's rape (Century: 1910) — each with defensible individual narrative functions. Griffin's assault clarifies alignment and sets up Hyde's protective act. Janni's assault is structurally parallel to Pirate Jenny and motivates her transformation. But the frequency of the pattern — each major female character's arc catalyzed by sexual violence — reproduces rather than interrogates a formula. The Galley-Wag character (drawn from the deeply racist golliwog caricature tradition) is a related failure: Moore's intention to redeem it does not override what the visual code communicates cold. For LoreVault: one dark-content decision is a choice; three is a policy. Build pattern-awareness into your content review process.

## 10. Exemplar Passage

The best single scene in *The League of Extraordinary Gentlemen* is the Hyde death sequence in Vol. 2 — pages 183 to 188 of the collected edition, drawn by Kevin O'Neill and written by Moore.

The setup: Griffin has betrayed the League to the Martians, delivered military plans, and assaulted Mina. Hyde has killed Griffin — locating him through infrared vision and smell, since invisibility does not affect heat or scent. The Martian tripods are burning central London. No effective counter exists.

Hyde asks Mina for a kiss. She gives him one — formally, without passion, but without hesitation. He dances out onto London Bridge.

O'Neill's panels operate at three simultaneous registers. One: Hyde running at a tripod, his massive body covering the distance in a way a normal human could not — a monster's charge, ugly and wrong. Two: his skin begins to burn under the heat-ray, O'Neill drawing the physical cost at full realism — skin peeling, the enormous body continuing as it is destroyed. Three: Hyde reaches the tripod, climbs it while burning, topples it, eats the alien inside. When the remaining tripods converge their heat-rays to kill him, he does not run.

Moore's dialogue is the finest in the entire series. Hyde does not die heroically, for humanity or for love. His last words are a version of: *I ended up looking really heroic, when all I wanted to do was slaughter something.*

This works because every element was built across the preceding hundred and eighty pages. Hyde's attachment to Mina is the attachment of a monster to the single person who treats him as a fixed identity rather than a threat. His violence has been consistently monstrous, never sanitized. His self-knowledge is established across multiple scenes where he articulates his nature with more precision than the supposedly rational League members manage about themselves. When he says his heroism was accidental, the reader understands it is simultaneously the truest and the most heroic thing anyone in the series says. Moore did not cheat Hyde into nobility; he let Hyde's consistent nature produce nobility as a by-product of the character being fully himself.

Jess Nevins, in *Heroes & Monsters*, identifies this as the scene where Moore and O'Neill most fully realize the series' core argument: that the monsters Victorian culture created to contain its anxieties are often more honest, more capable, and more genuinely heroic than the gentlemen who assembled them.
