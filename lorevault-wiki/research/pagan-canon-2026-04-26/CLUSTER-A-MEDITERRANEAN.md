## 1. Pantheon Enumeration

### Greek Olympians (Canonical Twelve)

**Zeus** — king of the gods, sky-father, lord of thunder and lightning, upholder of divine law (themis), shape-shifter in countless myths. Son of Cronus and Rhea. Attributes: thunderbolt, eagle, oak tree.

**Hera** — queen of the gods, goddess of marriage and legitimate birth, protector of women in childbirth. Known for jealous persecution of Zeus's lovers and illegitimate offspring. Attributes: peacock, pomegranate, diadem.

**Poseidon** — lord of the sea, earthquakes ("Earth-Shaker"), and horses. Rival of Athena for Athens. Wielder of the trident. Inhabits a palace beneath the waves; father of the Cyclops Polyphemus.

**Demeter** — goddess of grain, agriculture, and the harvest. Mother of Persephone. Her grief at Persephone's abduction causes winter. Attributes: wheat sheaf, torch, poppy.

**Athena** — goddess of wisdom, strategic warfare, crafts, and the city. Born fully armored from Zeus's forehead after he swallowed her pregnant mother Metis. Patron of Athens. Attributes: owl, olive tree, aegis, spear.

**Apollo** — god of the sun (in later synthesis), prophecy, music, poetry, plague, and healing. Twin of Artemis. The Delphic Oracle speaks in his name. Attributes: lyre, silver bow, laurel wreath, raven.

**Artemis** — goddess of the hunt, the moon (in later synthesis), and wild places. Twin of Apollo. Protector of young girls and childbirth. Eternally virginal. Attributes: silver bow, stag, crescent moon.

**Ares** — god of war — specifically the savage, bloodthirsty aspect of battle. Lover of Aphrodite. Unpopular among the Olympians. Attributes: spear, shield, vulture, dog.

**Aphrodite** — goddess of love, desire, beauty, and procreation. Born from seafoam around the severed genitals of Ouranos (Hesiod) or daughter of Zeus and Dione (Homer). Wife of Hephaestus, lover of Ares. Attributes: dove, sparrow, myrtle, rose.

**Hephaestus** — divine smith, god of fire, metalworking, and craftsmanship. Lame, cast from Olympus (by Hera in one tradition, by Zeus in another). Forges the armor of Achilles, the chains of Prometheus, the automata of the gods. Attributes: hammer, anvil, tongs.

**Hermes** — messenger of the gods, guide of souls to the underworld (psychopomp), god of travel, commerce, thieves, and language. Wears winged sandals (talaria) and carries the caduceus. Attributes: caduceus, petasos hat, rooster.

**Dionysus** — god of wine, ecstasy, theater, madness, and vegetation. Twice-born: first from Semele, then from Zeus's thigh after Semele's death. Dismembered by the Titans and reconstituted. Associated with leopards, ivy, and the thyrsus staff.

**Hestia** — goddess of the hearth, home, and the state. First-born and last-swallowed child of Cronus. Later ceded her Olympian seat to Dionysus to keep the peace. Every sacrificial meal begins and ends with her.

### Extended Greek Pantheon

**Persephone** — queen of the underworld, daughter of Demeter. Abducted by Hades. Her annual return from the underworld corresponds to spring; her descent corresponds to winter. Also known as Kore ("Maiden").

**Hades** — lord of the dead and the underworld realm named after him. Third brother (with Zeus and Poseidon) in the division of the world. Rarely leaves his realm. Wears a helmet of invisibility. Attributes: bident, Cerberus, Cypress.

**Hecate** — goddess of magic, crossroads, the night, ghosts, and necromancy. Often depicted as three-formed (Triformis). Companion to Persephone. Leads a procession of ghosts and howling dogs. Attributes: torches, key, dagger, serpents.

**Nike** — goddess of victory, winged companion of Zeus. Personification of triumph in war and athletics.

**Tyche** — goddess of fortune, chance, and fate of cities. Often depicted with a rudder (directing the course of life) and a cornucopia. Roman equivalent: Fortuna.

**Nemesis** — goddess of retribution and righteous indignation — punisher of hubris and undeserved fortune. Daughter of Nyx or Oceanus (sources vary). Carries a measuring rod and scales.

**Iris** — goddess of the rainbow; another messenger of the gods alongside Hermes, serving particularly the Olympian court.

**Eris** — goddess of strife and discord. Her golden apple inscribed "to the fairest" (kallisti) triggers the Trojan War.

**Hypnos** — god of sleep. Son of Nyx, twin of Thanatos. Lives in a cave in the underworld where the river Lethe flows.

**Thanatos** — god of death (peaceful death, as distinct from violent death under Ares). Son of Nyx, twin of Hypnos.

**Pan** — god of the wild, shepherds, flocks, and nature. Part-goat, part-man. His sudden appearance causes irrational "panic." Associated with Arcadia.

**Proteus** — the "Old Man of the Sea," a sea-god with prophetic gifts and the ability to change shape at will. Must be held fast to compel prophecy.

**Morpheus** — god of dreams, specifically those appearing in human form. Son of Hypnos.

**Helios** — god of the sun itself; drives the solar chariot across the sky each day. Distinguished from Apollo (who absorbed solar functions later).

**Selene** — goddess of the moon, drives the lunar chariot. Distinguished from Artemis (who absorbed lunar functions later). Lover of the sleeping shepherd Endymion.

**Eos** — goddess of the dawn. Rose-fingered, saffron-robed. Mother of the winds; abducts the Trojan prince Tithonus, for whom she requests immortality (but forgets to request eternal youth).

**Nyx** — goddess of night. One of the primordial Protogenoi. Mother of an immense brood: Hypnos, Thanatos, Eris, Nemesis, Moirai, Keres, Moros, the Hesperides, and others.

**Erebus** — primordial god of deep darkness and shadow; husband of Nyx.

**Pontus** — primordial sea, one of the earliest beings, born of Gaia alone. Father of Nereus, Thaumas, Phorcys, Ceto, and Eurybia.

**Gaia** — the Earth herself; mother of all. First to emerge from Chaos in Hesiod's account.

**Ouranos** — primordial sky, son and then husband of Gaia. Castrated by Cronus; from the severed genitals cast into the sea arose Aphrodite.

**Cronus** — king of the Titans; overthrew Ouranos and was in turn overthrown by his son Zeus. Swallowed each of his children at birth until Rhea substituted a stone for Zeus. Later identified (questionably) with the Roman Saturn.

**Rhea** — Titan mother-goddess, wife of Cronus. Saves Zeus by substituting a swaddled stone. Later venerated as Cybele-like Magna Mater.

### The Titans (Full Enumeration)

*First generation (twelve children of Ouranos and Gaia):*
**Oceanus** (river encircling the world), **Tethys** (his consort, mother of rivers/Oceanids), **Hyperion** (Titan of light), **Theia** (Titan of sight/heavenly light), **Coeus** (Titan of intelligence), **Phoebe** (Titan of prophecy, oracle at Delphi before Apollo), **Cronus** (described above), **Rhea** (described above), **Mnemosyne** (Titan of memory, mother of the nine Muses), **Themis** (Titan of divine law and order, mother of Horai and Moirai), **Crius** (Titan of the heavenly constellations), **Iapetus** (Titan of mortal lifespan; father of Prometheus, Epimetheus, Atlas, Menoetius).

*Second-generation notable Titans:*
**Prometheus** (forethought; steals fire for humanity; chained to a rock by Zeus), **Epimetheus** (afterthought; accepts Pandora), **Atlas** (condemned to hold up the sky), **Menoetius** (hubris and violence; cast into Tartarus by Zeus), **Helios**, **Selene**, **Eos** (children of Hyperion and Theia), **Leto** (mother of Apollo and Artemis), **Asteria** (goddess of falling stars), **Perses**, **Pallas**, **Astraeus** (father of the winds with Eos).

### The Moirai (Fates)
**Clotho** — "the Spinner," spins the thread of life. **Lachesis** — "the Allotter," measures the thread. **Atropos** — "the Inflexible," cuts the thread. Daughters of Zeus and Themis (Hesiod's Theogony). Even Zeus cannot override their decrees entirely.

### The Erinyes / Furies
**Alecto** (unceasing anger), **Megaera** (jealousy/grudge), **Tisiphone** (vengeance for murder). Born from the blood of Ouranos when he was castrated. They pursue oath-breakers, murderers, and those who violate the bonds of family. Called Eumenides ("Kindly Ones") euphemistically.

### The Muses (Daughters of Zeus and Mnemosyne)
**Calliope** (epic poetry), **Clio** (history), **Erato** (love poetry), **Euterpe** (music/lyric poetry), **Melpomene** (tragedy), **Polyhymnia** (sacred hymns), **Terpsichore** (dance), **Thalia** (comedy), **Urania** (astronomy).

### Major Heroes
**Heracles (Hercules)** — greatest of the Greek heroes; son of Zeus and Alcmena; performed the Twelve Labors; achieved apotheosis and became a god after death.
**Achilles** — greatest warrior of the Trojan War; son of Peleus and the Nereid Thetis; nearly invulnerable except at his heel; killed by Paris's arrow guided by Apollo.
**Odysseus (Ulysses)** — king of Ithaca; hero of the Odyssey; renowned for cunning and resourcefulness.
**Perseus** — slayer of Medusa; used the head as a weapon; rescued Andromeda; founder of Mycenae.
**Theseus** — king of Athens; slew the Minotaur in the Labyrinth; struggled with the Amazons; a culture-hero of Athenian civic identity.
**Jason** — leader of the Argonauts; quested for the Golden Fleece; aided by the witch Medea.
**Orpheus** — legendary musician of the Thracians; descended to the underworld to recover Eurydice; his music charmed all living and dead things.

### Roman Pantheon

**Jupiter** (Zeus) — **Juno** (Hera) — **Mars** (Ares, though more dignified in Roman cult; father of Romulus) — **Venus** (Aphrodite) — **Mercury** (Hermes) — **Saturn** (Cronus; also associated with the Golden Age) — **Pluto/Dis Pater** (Hades) — **Neptune** (Poseidon) — **Vulcan** (Hephaestus) — **Ceres** (Demeter) — **Diana** (Artemis) — **Minerva** (Athena; also patron of crafts and guilds) — **Bacchus** (Dionysus) — **Proserpina** (Persephone).

**Janus** — uniquely Roman; god of beginnings, doorways, transitions, and time. Two-faced (bifrons), looking simultaneously forward and back. No Greek equivalent. The month January is named for him.

**Terminus** — god of boundaries and border-markers; refused to yield his place even to Jupiter.

**Quirinus** — deified Romulus; a war-god distinct from Mars, associated with civic order.

**Lares** — household protective spirits, tied to the home and its hearth. Kept in a lararium (household shrine). Also Lares Compitales (protecting crossroads).

**Penates** — gods of the pantry and storehouse; protected the food supply of the household and of the Roman state.

**Fortuna** — goddess of luck and fate (equivalent of Tyche). Widely venerated throughout the Roman world.

**Victoria** — Roman Nike; goddess of military victory.

### Etruscan Pantheon

**Tinia** — sky-father and king of the Etruscan gods; equivalent to Zeus/Jupiter. Commands three thunderbolts: the first is advisory, the second requires consultation with the superior gods, the third is annihilating.

**Uni** — queen of the Etruscan gods, equivalent of Hera/Juno. Name may have contributed to "Juno."

**Menrva** — goddess of wisdom and crafts; equivalent of Athena/Minerva. Represents the triad of Tinia-Uni-Menrva, worshipped at Volsinii and the Capitoline at Rome.

**Charun** — Etruscan demon of death; a grim psychopomp who escorts the dead. Distinguished from the Greek Charon — Charun has a hammer and is monstrous rather than boatman-like.

**Vanth** — female winged death-demon; companion of Charun. Present at the moment of death; not purely malevolent but acts as a guide.

**Voltumna** — supreme deity of the Etruscan confederacy; nature-god, possibly earth or vegetation based; the sanctuary at Volsinii (Fanum Voltumnae) was the political and religious center of the twelve Etruscan city-states.

**Fufluns** — god of wine and vegetation; Etruscan Dionysus/Bacchus.

**Laran** — god of war; Etruscan Ares/Mars.

**Turms** — messenger of the gods; Etruscan Hermes/Mercury.

**Nethuns** — god of water; Etruscan Neptune.

**Usil** — god of the sun; Etruscan Helios/Sol.

### Phoenician / Carthaginian Pantheon

**El** — supreme father-god of the Canaanite/Phoenician world; "Father of the Gods," creator and patriarch. Lives at the sources of the rivers, at the cosmic mountain. Compassionate but distant.

**Baal Hadad** — storm-god, warrior, and lord of rain and fertility. Son of Dagan, or son of El depending on source. The preeminent active deity in the Ugaritic Baal Cycle. Battles Yam (sea) and Mot (death) for cosmic supremacy. Widely worshipped across Phoenician colonies.

**Asherah** — consort of El; mother-goddess, "Lady of the Sea." Intimately connected to sacred poles (asherim) in Canaanite worship. Related to (though distinct from) Astarte.

**Astarte** — goddess of love, sexuality, and war; Phoenician cognate of Mesopotamian Ishtar. Called "Queen of Heaven." Worshipped with high intensity at Sidon, Byblos, and Carthage. Syncretized with Aphrodite and Juno Caelestis by the Romans.

**Tanit** — supreme goddess of Carthage; "Face of Baal." Her distinctive symbol (a triangle surmounted by a horizontal bar and circle) appears across the Carthaginian world. Consort of Baal Hammon at Carthage. The tophet (ritual precinct) at Carthage was dedicated to Tanit and Baal Hammon.

**Baal Hammon** — chief god of Carthage; "Lord of the Incense Altar" or "Lord of the Amanus (mountain)." A sky-and-sun god; recipient of votive offerings at the Carthaginian tophet. Often depicted enthroned with a ram's horns.

**Mot** — god of death and the underworld; dry, dusty, lethal. Baal's great enemy; swallows Baal whole. Killed in turn by Baal's sister Anat.

**Yam** — god of the sea and rivers; chaos-force opposed to Baal's order. Called "Prince Sea" and "Judge River." Defeated by Baal with weapons crafted by Kothar-wa-Khasis.

**Melqart** — city-god of Tyre; "King of the City." A dying-and-rising deity, celebrated annually with an egersis (awakening) ritual. Syncretized heavily with Heracles/Hercules; the Pillars of Hercules may originate as the pillars of Melqart's temple at Cadiz (Gadir).

**Eshmun** — god of healing; Phoenician Asclepius. Worshipped especially at Sidon. A dying-and-rising deity; his mythos involves a mortal youth who attracts the love of a goddess, dies to escape her, and is resurrected.

**Kothar-wa-Khasis** — divine craftsman and smith; "Skilled and Clever." Builds Baal's palace; forges the weapons Yagrush and Aymur ("Chaser" and "Driver") with which Baal defeats Yam. Phoenician cognate of Hephaestus/Vulcan and the Egyptian Ptah.

**Adonis** — Phoenician/Syrian youth-god; beloved of Astarte/Aphrodite, killed by a boar (sent by Ares/the jealous gods), mourned annually in the Adonia festival. His name derives from the Semitic adon ("lord").

**Anat** — fierce war-goddess and huntress; sister of Baal; avenges Baal by slaughtering Mot; not as prominent in Western Phoenician/Carthaginian religion as in Ugaritic sources but present.

## 2. Cosmogony

### Greek Cosmogony (Hesiod's Theogony, c. 700 BCE)

At the very beginning there was **Chaos** — not "disorder" in the modern sense but a primordial yawning void, an opening or gap. Out of Chaos emerged:

1. **Gaia** (Earth) — broad, solid, sustaining
2. **Tartarus** — the deep pit beneath the earth, as far below Hades as heaven is above earth
3. **Eros** (primordial) — the force of attraction and procreation; distinct from the later Eros son of Aphrodite
4. **Erebus** (Darkness) — deep shadow, the air of the underworld
5. **Nyx** (Night)

From Erebus and Nyx mating: **Aether** (bright upper air) and **Hemera** (Day).

Nyx alone produces a vast brood: **Moros** (doom), **Keres** (death-spirits), **Thanatos** (death), **Hypnos** (sleep), the **Oneiroi** (dreams), **Momus** (blame), **Oizys** (misery), the **Hesperides**, the **Moirai** (Fates), the **Keres**, **Nemesis**, **Apate** (deceit), **Philotes** (friendship/sex), **Geras** (old age), **Eris** (strife).

Gaia alone produces **Ouranos** (Sky), **Ourea** (Mountains), and **Pontus** (Sea).

**The Titans' Generation:** Gaia and Ouranos mate to produce the twelve Titans (listed in §1). Ouranos hates his children and forces them back into Gaia's body as each is born. Gaia, in pain, persuades young **Cronus** to castrate Ouranos with an adamantine sickle. When Ouranos's severed genitals fall into the sea:
- From the sea-foam and blood arose **Aphrodite** (and the Erinyes / Furies from the blood that fell on land, along with the Giants and Meliae — ash-tree nymphs).

**The Olympians' Generation:** Cronus now rules but swallows each child born to him by Rhea, fearing a prophecy that his son will overthrow him. The children swallowed: Hestia, Demeter, Hera, Hades, Poseidon. Rhea, at Gaia's advice, wraps a stone in swaddling clothes and gives it to Cronus; Zeus is hidden in Crete (on Mt. Ida or in a cave at Dicte). Raised by nymphs and the goat Amaltheia. Upon reaching maturity, Zeus forces Cronus to disgorge his siblings (the stone is last, and is set up at Delphi as the omphalos).

### The Titanomachy

Zeus frees the Hecatonchires (Hundred-Handed ones: Cottus, Briareos, Gyges) and the Elder Cyclopes from Tartarus, where Ouranos had imprisoned them. The Cyclopes forge Zeus's thunderbolts, Poseidon's trident, and Hades's helmet of invisibility. The ten-year war between the Olympians (aided by the Hecatonchires) and the Titans rages until Zeus and his allies prevail. The defeated Titans are cast into Tartarus, guarded by the Hecatonchires. Notable Titans allied with Zeus: Prometheus, Themis, Oceanus, Styx.

**Alternative outcome (What-If hinge):** Had the Titans retained power, the universe would be governed by the older, more primal order — time and fate as absolute rulers (Cronus = Chronos in late syncretism), with no special solicitude for human civilization, no oracles, no divine champions for heroes. The cosmos would be colder, more cyclical, more impersonal.

### Roman Variant (Ovid's Metamorphoses, c. 8 CE)

Ovid opens with a proto-cosmogony: one "God or better Nature" separates the warring elements — earth, sea, air, fire — from the primordial mass (rudis indigestaque moles). The Four Ages follow: Golden (Saturn's reign, no law, no toil, eternal spring), Silver (Jupiter's reign, seasons introduced, agriculture begins), Bronze (war begins), Iron (crime, greed, sacrilege — the present age). Jupiter destroys humanity with a flood; only Deucalion and Pyrrha survive and repopulate the earth by casting stones (the "bones of the Great Mother").

### Etruscan Cosmological Frame

The Etruscans received their sacred knowledge through **haruspicy** — reading the livers of sacrificial animals. The foundational myth holds that the god **Tages** (a divine child who emerged from a plowed field) taught the art to the haruspex Tarchon. Etruscan sacred texts (Libri Tagetici, Libri Fulgurales, Libri Rituales) describe the cosmos as divided into sixteen regions of the sky, each assigned to a deity, from which omens could be read. The Etruscan cosmos has a defined lifespan — the Etruscan people's history was divided into saecula (ages of uncertain length), and augurs tracked the total number of saecula allotted to Etruscan civilization.

### Phoenician Cosmogony (Philo of Byblos, transmitting Sanchuniathon, c. 1st-2nd century CE / possibly Bronze Age source)

In the beginning was **Mot** — primordial mud or dark watery chaos, ambiguous matter. From Mot arose the cosmic egg, and from it light and the heavens. Wind and desire (Pothos) breathe across the chaos. A pair of first beings, **Kolpia** (wind) and **Baau** (Night) produce mortal humans. Their descendants discover fire, learn to build, and begin civilization. **El** (identified by Philo with Kronos) murders his father **Ouranos** (Epigeios/Autochthon in Philo's version) and castrates him — a notable structural parallel with Hesiod. El's children include Baal, Dagan, Mot, Yam, and many others. Philo's account shows the Phoenician pantheon as a family lineage with inheritance of power through violence, echoing both Hesiod and the Ugaritic texts.

## 3. Pantheon-Conflict Events

### The Titanomachy (Full Detail)
Location: The Olympians hold Mount Olympus; the Titans hold Mount Othrys. Duration: ten years. The Olympians gain decisive advantage by freeing the Elder Cyclopes (who forge divine weapons) and the Hecatonchires (who hurl mountains as projectiles). Zeus reveals himself as the son of Rhea to the Titans, some of whom — notably Oceanus, Prometheus, Themis, and Styx — side with Zeus. The Hecatonchires' ability to hurl three hundred boulders simultaneously ends the war. Outcome: Titans imprisoned in Tartarus beneath the earth, guarded eternally. Atlas receives special punishment: he must hold up the sky alone.

### The Gigantomachy
After the Titanomachy, the Giants (born from Gaia's blood when Ouranos was castrated, or alternatively from Tartarus and Gaia in response to the Titans' imprisonment) rise against the Olympians. The prophecy states the Olympians cannot win without a mortal champion — Heracles fulfills this role. Key battles: Enceladus vs. Athena (she buries him under Sicily, creating Mt. Etna); Polybotes vs. Poseidon (buried under Nisyros); Porphyrion vs. Zeus and Heracles; Alcyoneus (in some versions immortal in his native land of Pallene, so Heracles must drag him across the border before killing him).

### The Typhonomachy
The most terrifying of the challenges to Zeus. **Typhon** (Typhoeus) — child of Gaia and Tartarus, the last and greatest monster born against the Olympians — has a hundred serpent-heads, voices of every animal, and immense power. In one tradition, Typhon defeats Zeus, strips out his sinews, and hides them in a bear-skin; Hermes and Pan recover them and restore Zeus. Zeus ultimately defeats Typhon by striking him repeatedly with thunderbolts and burying him under Mount Etna (or in Tartarus). Typhon fathers the Lernaean Hydra, the Nemean Lion, the Sphinx, Cerberus, the Chimera, and the Eagle that gnaws Prometheus's liver — making him the progenitor of most of the monsters in Greek mythology.

### The Trojan War
Triggered by Eris's golden apple ("to the fairest"), the Judgment of Paris, and Paris's abduction of Helen. The war lasts ten years. Key divine factions: Hera and Athena favor the Greeks; Aphrodite, Apollo, and Ares favor Troy. Zeus attempts nominal neutrality but is swayed. Achilles's wrath, the death of Patroclus, Hector's death, the stratagem of the Trojan Horse, and the sack of Troy form the arc. The gods intervene directly and repeatedly in battle — deflecting spears, rescuing heroes, wounding (and being wounded) by mortals. Ultimately a story about the limits of divine patronage.

### The Seven Against Thebes
Oedipus's sons Eteocles and Polynices agree to share the throne of Thebes by rotating annually. Eteocles refuses to yield. Polynices raises an army of seven champions (including Adrastus, Capaneus, Amphiaraus, Tydeus, Hippomedon, Parthenopaeus, and Polynices himself) to take Thebes by force. All seven attackers are defeated; only Adrastus survives. The brothers kill each other in single combat. Their sons, the Epigoni, subsequently sack Thebes in revenge. This cycle feeds directly into the tragedies of Sophocles (Antigone) and Aeschylus (Seven Against Thebes).

### Zeus vs. Prometheus
Prometheus steals fire from the gods' hearth (or from the forge of Hephaestus) and gives it to humanity. Zeus punishes him by chaining him to a rock in the Caucasus, where an eagle (some say a vulture) eats his liver daily; it regenerates overnight. Heracles eventually frees him. Additionally, Zeus creates Pandora — the first woman — as punishment for humanity, sending her with a jar (pithos, misnamed "box") containing all evils; only Hope remains inside.

### The Olympian Succession Crisis
Several traditions hint at a perpetual threat to Zeus's rule. The Moirai had decreed that the son of the sea-goddess **Thetis** would surpass his father — leading Zeus to ensure Thetis married a mortal (Peleus), producing the merely-human-but-extraordinary Achilles. Additionally, the Titan **Prometheus** holds the secret of which child of Zeus will overthrow him; this knowledge gives Prometheus leverage in negotiations. The threat of succession — the same pattern that destroyed Ouranos and Cronus — is the structuring anxiety of the Olympian order.

### The Baal Cycle (Ugaritic, c. 1400-1200 BCE)
**Baal vs. Yam:** Yam (Sea/River) demands Baal as his slave; the divine assembly, led by El, seems to acquiesce. Baal refuses. Kothar-wa-Khasis forges two magic clubs — Yagrush ("Chaser") and Aymur ("Driver") — for Baal. In the climactic battle, Yagrush strikes Yam's shoulders and Aymur strikes his forehead; Yam collapses. Baal is declared king.

**Baal vs. Mot:** Mot, god of death, demands Baal descend to him. Baal does, and dies — the land dries up, crops fail, the world mourns. Baal's sister **Anat** finds Mot, attacks him with a sickle, winnows, grinds, scatters, and buries him (mirroring agricultural processing). Baal revives. Eventually a direct combat between Baal and Mot ends in a draw; El proclaims Baal's permanent kingship. This cycle encodes the ancient Near Eastern seasonal drama — rain returning to the parched summer earth.

## 4. Forgotten / Adjacent Figures

### The Protogenoi (First-Born)
The deepest layer: **Chaos** (void), **Gaia** (Earth), **Tartarus** (Abyss), **Eros** (Desire), **Erebus** (Darkness), **Nyx** (Night), **Aether** (Bright Air), **Hemera** (Day), **Phanes** (in Orphic tradition — the first radiant being, hatched from the cosmic egg).

### Nyx's Full Brood (Hesiod)
Moros (doom), Keres (death spirits), Thanatos, Hypnos, Oneiroi (dreams), Momus (blame), Oizys (woe), Hesperides (guardians of the golden apples), Moirai (Fates), Keres (death spirits), Nemesis, Apate (deception), Philotes (friendship), Geras (old age), Eris (strife). This catalog demonstrates that Greek theology treated abstract experiences — sleep, death, blame, old age — as genuinely divine forces.

### The Anemoi (Wind Gods)
Sons of Eos (Dawn) and Astraeus (a second-generation Titan): **Boreas** (North Wind), **Notus** (South Wind), **Zephyrus** (West Wind), **Eurus** (East Wind). Also Aeolus — not a wind-god himself but the keeper of the winds (a mortal king elevated to divine wind-warden). Additional minor Anemoi exist in fuller lists.

### The Nereids (Sea Nymphs)
Fifty daughters of Nereus ("Old Man of the Sea") and Doris, an Oceanid. Most famous: **Thetis** (mother of Achilles), **Amphitrite** (wife of Poseidon), **Galatea** (beloved of the Cyclops Polyphemus), **Psamathe**, **Panopea**. The Nereids mourn Achilles's death and carry his armor.

### Nymphs, Dryads, and Others
**Nymphs** — female spirits of nature: Naiads (freshwater), Oreads (mountains), Dryads/Hamadryads (trees), Nereids (sea), Oceanids (ocean streams). **Satyrs and Sileni** — wild male spirits of the woodland, associated with Dionysus. **Centaurs** — half-human, half-horse; mostly savage, but Chiron is wise and tutors heroes.

### Figures Killed in the Cradle or Almost-Never-Were
**The Infant Heracles** strangling Hera's serpents — establishing divine status from birth. **Zagreus** — in Orphic tradition, the first Dionysus, son of Zeus and Persephone; dismembered and eaten by the Titans at Hera's instigation; only his heart survives, from which the second Dionysus is born. This myth provides the Orphic theology of human origins: humanity is born from the ash of the destroyed Titans who had consumed Zagreus, giving us both a Titanic (sinful) nature and a divine (Dionysian) spark.

## 5. Cyclical Figures and Ritual Scenes

### Dying-and-Rising Gods

**Dionysus** undergoes multiple deaths and resurrections. In the Orphic account, the infant Dionysus (Zagreus) is lured by the Titans with toys and mirrors, dismembered, boiled in a cauldron, and eaten. Athena saves his heart, which Zeus swallows, then reconceives with Semele. The Dionysian mysteries encoded this narrative: initiates underwent symbolic death and rebirth mirroring the god's own dismemberment and reconstitution. The theatrical festival of Dionysia at Athens reenacted this death/rebirth logic in the tragic form — the drama as ritual resurrection.

**Adonis** is the Phoenician-Greek fusion figure par excellence. A beautiful youth (born from the incestuous union of Myrrha and her father Cinyras), he is shared between Aphrodite (who loves him in life) and Persephone (who loves him in death). Zeus or Calliope mediates: Adonis spends part of the year above ground (with Aphrodite, spring/summer) and part below (with Persephone, autumn/winter). Killed by a boar (sent by Ares, or Artemis, or Hephaestus depending on the tradition). Women mourned Adonis annually in the Adonia — a uniquely female-led festival involving roof-garden plantings of fast-sprouting seeds that quickly died ("gardens of Adonis"), wailing, and lamentation.

**Persephone's Descent** constitutes the deepest Greek cycle. Abducted by Hades while gathering flowers (narcissus in particular, a flower of death), Persephone eats pomegranate seeds in the underworld (six or three, depending on source), binding her to return below for that portion of the year. Demeter's withdrawal of her agricultural gifts causes winter — a world experiencing the goddess's grief. The Eleusinian Mysteries (see below) took this myth as their central revelation.

### The Mysteries of Eleusis
The most prestigious mystery cult of the ancient world, lasting from the Mycenaean period to 396 CE when Alaric sacked the sanctuary. Initiates undertook a two-day procession from Athens to Eleusis, ritual fasting, and then the secret telesterion ceremony — the contents never fully disclosed in antiquity. Likely involved: a dramatic reenactment of Persephone's descent and return, a sacred drink (kykeon — grain and water with pennyroyal), and some kind of vision or revelation. The cult promised initiates a better fate in the afterlife.

### The Wild Hunt Equivalents

**Hecate's Night Procession** — Hecate moves through the world at night with a retinue of ghosts (the restless dead, those who died violently or without burial) and howling dogs. Her arrival is presaged by the howling of dogs. She appears at crossroads, where offerings were left for her (hecate's suppers — deipna). Offerings included dogs, black lambs, honey cakes, and fish. She is invoked by witches (as in Medea's rites in Euripides) and is a patron of magic.

**The Lemures (Roman)** — shades of the dead who had not received proper burial or who died violently. Unlike the Lares (friendly household ancestors), Lemures are dangerous. At the Lemuria festival (May 9, 11, 13), the pater familias rose at midnight, made the mano fica gesture, washed his hands, tossed black beans over his shoulder nine times saying "with these I redeem myself and mine," and banged bronze pots to drive off the lemures.

### The Saturnalia (Roman, December 17-23)
Festival of Saturn during the winter solstice period. Social order temporarily inverted: masters feasted their slaves; slaves could speak freely; gambling was permitted. Gift-giving was common. The festival reenacted the Golden Age of Saturn's reign when all were equal. An early locus for the spirit of midwinter festivity later absorbed by Christmas traditions.

### The Lupercalia (Roman, February 15)
Archaic fertility and purification festival at the Lupercal cave on the Palatine Hill — the cave where the wolf (lupa) had nursed Romulus and Remus. Priests called Luperci sacrificed goats and a dog; strips of goatskin (februa) were cut; young men ran naked through the streets striking women with the strips to promote fertility and easy childbirth.

### The Bacchanalia (Roman-Greek, formally suppressed 186 BCE)
Nocturnal initiation festivals in honor of Bacchus/Dionysus, spreading throughout Italy in the 2nd century BCE. Included wine, sexual rites, and ecstatic practices; the Roman Senate feared them as a subversive secret society and suppressed them brutally (the senatus consultum de Bacchanalibus). The episode illustrates how Dionysian religion was politically threatening to Roman order even as it was theologically absorbed.

### The Parentalia (Roman, February 13-21)
Public festival honoring deceased ancestors. Temples were closed, no marriages permitted. Families visited tombs, brought offerings of wine, bread, violets, and incense. On February 22 the Caristia (Cara Cognatio) followed — a reconciliation feast among the living family.

### Etruscan Augury Rituals
Haruspicy — reading the liver of a sacrificed animal — was the primary form. The Piacenza Liver (a bronze model liver found in northern Italy) maps the liver's lobes onto the sixteen celestial regions. Fulgurites (lightning-reading) interpreted where and how lightning struck relative to compass points — each type of strike presaged different events. The disciplina etrusca was a complete sacred science: augury, haruspicy, and the rituals of city-founding. Roman religion borrowed extensively from Etruscan augural science; Roman magistrates routinely consulted haruspices of Etruscan training.

## 6. Public-Domain Status

### Status Summary
All figures and narratives described in this dossier derive from ancient sources that are unambiguously in the public domain. The mythological archetypes, characters, and storylines are the common inheritance of humanity.

### Primary Ancient Sources (All Public Domain)

**Greek:**
- **Hesiod**, *Theogony* and *Works and Days* (c. 700 BCE) — foundational cosmogony and genealogy
- **Homer**, *Iliad* and *Odyssey* (c. 750-700 BCE) — epic war narrative, the Olympians in action
- **Homeric Hymns** (c. 7th-5th century BCE) — hymns to individual deities
- **Pindar**, Odes (c. 522-443 BCE) — heroic myths, divine genealogies
- **Aeschylus**, *Oresteia*, *Prometheus Bound* (5th century BCE) — Erinyes, Titans

- **Apollodorus (pseudo-)**, *Bibliotheca* (c. 1st-2nd century CE) — systematic myth compilation
- **Pausanias**, *Description of Greece* (c. 143-176 CE) — religious sites and local traditions
- **Nonnus**, *Dionysiaca* (c. 5th century CE) — Dionysian mythology at length

**Roman:**
- **Ovid**, *Metamorphoses* and *Fasti* (c. 8 CE) — comprehensive mythological compendium
- **Virgil**, *Aeneid* (c. 29-19 BCE) — Roman foundation mythology

**Phoenician/Canaanite:**
- **Ugaritic tablets** (Ras Shamra, c. 1400-1200 BCE) — Baal Cycle, El texts (now in museum collections; thoroughly published and public)
- **Philo of Byblos** (c. 64-141 CE), preserved in Eusebius's *Praeparatio Evangelica*
- **Sanchuniathon** (Phoenician priest, date disputed, transmitted through Philo)

## 7. Cultural-Sensitivity Gates

### Greek and Roman Mythology
**Sensitivity level: LOW** for the mythological content itself. Greek and Roman mythology is foundational academic and literary canon, taught globally, and its figures are widely used in culture, commerce, and art.

**Exception — Living Traditions:** Hellenismos (also Hellenism) is a reconstructed ancient Greek religious practice with living practitioners in Greece and globally, recognized as an official religion in Greece since 2017. Practitioners worship the Olympian gods as living deities. Do not mock, trivialize, or reduce their practices to "fantasy." The tradition is small but sincere and should be treated with the same respect given to any minority religion.

Similarly, Nova Roma and Roman reconstructionist communities maintain living Roman polytheist practice. Engage respectfully with the religious dimension of these traditions.

**Ritual specifics:** Some ancient Greek rituals (Thesmophoria — women-only rites; some underworld/chthonic rites) retain cultural weight for modern pagans. Do not treat sacred ritual space as horror-game scenery without awareness of the living community.

### Etruscan Material
**Sensitivity level: LOW-MODERATE.** Etruscan culture is extinct as a living tradition but is part of Italian cultural heritage. Italian-heritage communities may feel pride of ownership over Etruscan content. The Etruscans are sometimes characterized as "mysterious" in ways that can tip into othering — prefer accurate characterization (a sophisticated, literate civilization with profound influence on Rome) over mystification.

Death iconography (Charun, Vanth) is rich but should not be handled in a way that reduces Etruscan funerary culture to mere grotesque — it reflects a nuanced relationship with death and the afterlife.

### Phoenician/Carthaginian Material
**Sensitivity level: MODERATE.** Several vectors of care apply:

**North African and Levantine heritage:** Carthaginian culture is part of the heritage of Tunisia, Libya, and the broader Maghreb, and Phoenician culture is central to Lebanese, Cypriot, and broader Levantine identity. Modern Lebanese cultural discourse explicitly reclaims Phoenician ancestry. Treat the cultures as sophisticated civilizations, not merely as Rome's defeated enemy or as exotic backdrop.

**Abrahamic adjacency:** El, Baal, Asherah, and Astarte appear directly in the Hebrew Bible (often as objects of condemnation by the prophets). The Baal against whom Elijah contends is Baal Hadad or Baal Melqart. Treating these figures in a card game will inevitably intersect with living religious heritage for Jewish, Christian, and Muslim players who encounter these names as the "false gods" of their scriptures. Handle this adjacency with care:
- Do not frame Phoenician deities as demonic or inherently evil (they were worshipped by sincere believers across centuries)
- Avoid visual/naming choices that will read as anti-Semitic dog-whistles (Baal in particular has been weaponized in certain conspiratorial rhetorics)
- Tanit/Baal Hammon: the ancient accusation of child sacrifice at the Carthaginian tophet is historically contested — modern archaeology debates whether the urns contain sacrifice victims or natural infant deaths. Do not treat this as confirmed fact in card flavor text.

**The Adonis figure** is particularly cross-cultural (Greek-Phoenician-Syrian) and is also referenced in the prophets (Ezekiel 8:14 — women weeping for Tammuz, the Mesopotamian cognate). Treat with sensitivity to the multiple communities who have stakes in this figure.

## 8. Cross-Pantheon Archetype Mapping

This section maps major mythological figures to universal archetypes for LoreVault card design, enabling creation of archetype-consistent card families across pantheons.

### Sky-Father / King of the Gods
- Greek: **Zeus** (thunderbolt, eagle, oak; guarantor of law and hospitality)
- Roman: **Jupiter** (the capitoline king; his temple is the symbolic heart of Roman power)
- Etruscan: **Tinia** (three thunderbolts; the graduated power of divine wrath)
- Phoenician: **El** (patriarch, compassionate but distant; the divine council answers to him)
- Phoenician active variant: **Baal Hadad** (warrior-king, storm-lord; more combative than El)
- Carthaginian: **Baal Hammon** (enthroned sky-lord; ram-horned)

**Card design note:** These figures share: sky dominion, council leadership, thunderbolt weapon, paternal authority. Differentiated by temperament (El = compassionate, Zeus = capricious, Tinia = graduated).

### Sea-Father / Chaos of Waters
- Greek: **Poseidon** (trident, horse, earthquake; dangerous and unpredictable)
- Roman: **Neptune**
- Phoenician: **Yam** (chaos-sea; adversarial rather than protective; "Prince Sea, Judge River")
- Etruscan: **Nethuns**

**Differentiation:** Poseidon/Neptune = powerful but subject to Olympian authority; Yam = pure chaos-force, not integrated into the divine family. This distinction opens an interesting design space.

### Death-Keeper / Lord of the Underworld
- Greek: **Hades** (invisible helmet; bident; grim but just; does not kill, merely receives)
- Roman: **Pluto / Dis Pater** (the wealth beneath the earth; chtonic richness)
- Etruscan: **Charun** (hammer-wielding, monstrous; death as violence rather than administration)
- Phoenician: **Mot** (death as devouring; swallows Baal whole; dry and agricultural-metaphor)

**Differentiation:** Hades = bureaucrat of death; Charun = executioner; Mot = hunger and consumption. Three distinct death-card archetypes.

### Terror-and-Love Goddess
- Greek: **Aphrodite** (desire, beauty, devastating power to undo heroes)
- Roman: **Venus** (adds the dimension of Rome's divine ancestry through Aeneas)
- Etruscan: **Uni** (overlaps here as queenly power, though more regal than seductive)
- Phoenician: **Astarte** (explicit war + love duality; Queen of Heaven; armed and dangerous)
- Carthaginian: **Tanit** (supreme at Carthage; her symbol is among the most recognizable Carthaginian icons)

**Card design note:** Astarte as armed-love goddess is more directly card-playable than Aphrodite's softer ancient depictions. Tanit as city-protector adds a civic-power dimension.

### Wisdom-Warrior / Patron of Civilization
- Greek: **Athena** (tactical war, not bloodlust; patron of crafts, city, law)
- Roman: **Minerva** (craftsmen's guilds, teachers, doctors — more domestic than Athena)
- Etruscan: **Menrva** (member of the supreme triad; strong synthesis figure)

### Divine Smith / Maker
- Greek: **Hephaestus** (lame, volcanic, resentful; makes divine weapons and automata)
- Roman: **Vulcan** (more dangerous than Hephaestus; volcano-god; destructive fire)
- Phoenician: **Kothar-wa-Khasis** (the "Clever" one; makes the weapons that defeat chaos; cosmic architect)

**Card design note:** Kothar's paired divine clubs (Yagrush and Aymur) are distinctive weapons that distinguish Phoenician smith mythology from the Greek/Roman version.

### Trickster-Messenger / Boundary-Crosser
- Greek: **Hermes** (crosses all boundaries — living/dead, divine/mortal, city/wilderness; psychopomp; lord of thieves and trade)
- Roman: **Mercury** (commerce and communication emphasized over psychopomp function)
- Etruscan: **Turms** (Etruscan Hermes; depicted with caduceus)

### Dying-and-Rising Vegetation God
- Greek: **Dionysus** (dismemberment and reconstitution; wine as blood; tragedy and ecstasy)
- Phoenician-Greek: **Adonis** (killed by boar, mourned, cyclically resurrected; beloved of Aphrodite and Persephone)
- Phoenician: **Melqart** (annual egersis ritual; syncretized with Heracles; civilizational god)
- Phoenician: **Eshmun** (healing and resurrection; Sidon's god; the divine physician who overcame death)
- Baal Cycle: **Baal** (dies to Mot, is resurrected; structurally a seasonal dying-and-rising figure)

**Card design note:** This archetype is the richest for cyclical card mechanics — damage-and-revive, exile-and-return, sacrifice-and-reward design patterns.

### Threshold Guardian / Liminal Deity
- Roman: **Janus** (two-faced, sees past and future simultaneously; every door; every beginning; patron of the month of transitions)
- Greek: **Hecate** (crossroads, magic, the space between living and dead; three-formed)
- Greek underworld: **Charon** (ferryman; the crossing of the Styx as threshold)

**Differentiation:** Janus = threshold of time and space; Hecate = threshold of the magical/mundane and life/death; Charon = threshold of mortality itself.

### Wild Hunter / Mistress of Beasts (Potnia Theron)
- Greek: **Artemis** (hunt, moon, wild places; protects animals while killing them; demands ritual purity)
- Roman: **Diana** (adds a strong cult-of-the-woods dimension at Nemi; Diana Nemorensis)
- Greek: **Pan** (the wild itself, panic, nature's indifferent power; male counterpart)

### Fate / Time / Inevitable Necessity
- Greek: **Moirai** (Clotho/Lachesis/Atropos — the three together; unstoppable even by Zeus)
- Roman: **Parcae** (equivalent Fates; more austere in Roman conception)
- Greek: **Cronus** (as Kronos = Time; the consuming nature of time)
- Greek: **Nemesis** (the correction force; the universe's self-righting mechanism against hubris)
- Greek: **Tyche/Fortuna** (the capricious force of luck, unattached to justice)

### War (Berserker/Savage)
- Greek: **Ares** (war as slaughter; hated by his own father; necessary but abhorrent)
- Phoenician: **Anat** (the most violent of the Phoenician deities; wades through blood; necklace of severed heads)
- Etruscan: **Laran** (Etruscan war-god; depicted as a young warrior)
- Roman: **Mars** (rehabilitated in Roman tradition as the dignified father of Romulus; protective as much as destructive)

### Healing / Medicine
- Greek: **Asclepius** (son of Apollo; raised by Chiron; killed by Zeus for resurrecting the dead; snakes as healing instruments)
- Phoenician: **Eshmun** (as noted above)

**Design note:** Asclepius and Eshmun form a natural card pair — both healing gods associated with death-defiance.

### Divine Fire / Light-Bringer
- Greek: **Prometheus** (stolen fire; civilization's patron; eternal punishment; the rebel god who loves humanity more than the gods do)
- Greek: **Helios** (solar chariot; all-seeing; witnesses oaths)
- Greek: **Hephaestus** (fire as craft)

**Prometheus** stands alone as the archetype of the divine rebel and the cost of civilizational gifts — uniquely LoreVault-playable as a card that grants power at enormous cost.
