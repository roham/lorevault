## 1. Pantheon Enumeration

### Vietnamese Folk / Daoist-Adjacent Tradition (Tín Ngưỡng Dân Gian)

Vietnamese religion is a syncretic, layered tradition drawing on animist substrate, Mahayana Buddhism, Daoist administrative cosmology, Confucian ethics, and the indigenous Đạo Mẫu (Mother Goddess Way). It is not a codified pantheon but a living devotional ecosystem.

**Ngọc Hoàng (Jade Emperor / Ngọc Hoàng Thượng Đế):** The supreme celestial administrator, equivalent to the Chinese Yù Huáng but localized through Vietnamese liturgical vernacular. He presides over a heavenly bureaucracy that mirrors the imperial court. He is not a creator god but a cosmic magistrate — the moral governor of heaven and earth. His court issues decrees that bind humans, spirits, and nature-gods alike.

**Táo Quân — The Three Kitchen Gods:** A triad of household deities (two husbands and one wife, reflecting a folk narrative of remarriage) who dwell on the kitchen hearth and record the moral conduct of the household throughout the year. On the 23rd day of the twelfth lunar month they ascend to heaven on carp-transformed into dragons to report to Ngọc Hoàng. Vietnamese families release live carp into rivers on this day, and new kitchen-god images are installed for the New Year. This annual reporting cycle is one of the most widely observed rituals in the Vietnamese world.

**Tứ Bất Tử — The Four Immortals:** These four figures are uniquely Vietnamese and represent the highest tier of the Vietnamese sacred canon — they cannot die and thus stand outside the celestial bureaucracy. They are:

- **Tản Viên Sơn Thánh (Lord of Mount Tản):** Master of mountains, rain, and floods; received the magic staff from the Long King; defeated Thủy Tinh (the flood-spirit) annually. He is the guardian of the land itself and the oldest of the Four Immortals in terms of mythological stratum.
- **Thánh Gióng (The Iron-Armored Child):** A miraculous child who does not speak until age three, then immediately asks for iron armor, an iron horse, and an iron staff to drive out foreign invaders (the Yin/Shang army). He grows to giant size, defeats the enemy, and ascends to heaven without dying. He embodies the Vietnamese ideal of national defense and selfless service.
- **Chử Đồng Tử:** A man of extreme poverty who, through a series of magical encounters (including marrying a princess and receiving a sacred staff and conical hat from a Daoist hermit), ascends to heaven with his wife. He is the patron of merchants, couples, and the poor; his story contains strong shamanic overtones.
- **Liễu Hạnh:** A celestial princess who, after breaking a jade cup in heaven, is exiled to earth twice — living as a poet, a tavern-keeper, and a wandering woman of great magical power. She is the only female immortal and is central to the Đạo Mẫu system. She became one of the four primary Mother Goddesses and is revered with particular intensity in northern Vietnam.

**Tứ Phủ — The Four Realms / Mother Goddesses (Đạo Mẫu):** This is arguably the most distinctive and ancient stratum of Vietnamese religious practice. The four realms are Heaven (Mẫu Đệ Nhất — Liễu Hạnh), Water (Mẫu Đệ Nhị — Lady of Water), Mountains and Forests (Mẫu Đệ Tam — Lady of Mountains), and Earth (Mẫu Đệ Tứ — Lady of Earth). The Tứ Phủ system underpins the lên đồng spirit-mediumship ritual, in which mediums are possessed by the goddesses and their courts (the Tứ Phủ court includes 36 manifestations across the four realms). Đạo Mẫu predates Sinicization and survived both Chinese administrative religion and later Christianization as a folk practice.

**Lạc Long Quân (Dragon-Lord of Lạc) and Âu Cơ:** The founding ancestral couple of the Vietnamese people. Lạc Long Quân is a dragon-king, son of the Dragon Lord of the Eastern Sea; Âu Cơ is a fairy of the mountains, descended from the spirit of the high peaks. Their union produced a sac of one hundred eggs, from which one hundred sons hatched — the ancestor-heroes of the hundred Vietnamese clans. The couple separated by nature: the dragon returned to the sea with fifty sons (the coastal and maritime peoples); Âu Cơ ascended to the mountains with fifty sons (the highland peoples and the ancestor of the Hùng Kings). This myth encodes Vietnam's ecological and ethnic geography — the tension and partnership between highland and coastal life.

### Thai / Lao Tradition

Thai religious culture is built on a Theravada Buddhist foundation overlaid on a pre-Buddhist animist layer and with significant Brahmanical Hindu borrowings that arrived via the Khmer cultural sphere. The result is a tradition where Buddhist monks and Brahmin court priests coexist with spirit-mediums and animist specialists.

**Phra Phrom (พระพรหม):** The Thai adaptation of Brahma as a four-faced deity of supreme blessing and protection. He is not understood in his Hindu cosmological role (creator of worlds) but as a powerful intercessor deity. The Erawan Shrine in Bangkok, housing a famous Phra Phrom image, became one of the most active devotional sites in Southeast Asia — a living example of how Brahmanical figures operate within a Buddhist devotional economy.

**Phra Narai (พระนารายณ์):** Vishnu adapted into the Thai royal-protective tradition. The king in Thai court ideology was understood as an avatar of Phra Narai; the coronation rites and royal regalia (including the Garuda as royal symbol) are drawn from this identification. The Garuda appears on Thai government seals.

**Phi — The Spirit World:** The phi are a vast and ancient category of spirits that predate Buddhism in the Thai world. They include: phi pob (malevolent spirits that possess and cause illness), phi krasue (a severed glowing head with trailing entrails, one of Southeast Asia's most widespread supernatural figures), phi phrai (spirits of women who died in childbirth, used in love magic), phi fa (sky spirits), phi nam (water spirits), and dozens of localized varieties. The phi tradition represents the uncodified animist substrate that Buddhism never fully replaced. Specialist practitioners — the mor phi or spirit doctors — mediate between the living and the phi world.

**Nang Kwak (นางกวัก):** The smiling female deity of wealth and good fortune, depicted with one hand raised in beckoning and the other holding a money bag. She is found in almost every Thai shop and business. Her origins are debated — she may derive from Lakshmi or from older Mon-Khmer prosperity-spirit traditions.

**Mae Thorani (แม่ธรณี):** The Earth Goddess, who in the Thai Buddhist narrative wrings water from her hair to flood and defeat Mara's army at the moment of the Buddha's enlightenment. In this role she is the most prominent female divine figure in Thai Buddhist iconography. She predates Buddhism as a Southeast Asian earth-spirit and was assimilated into the Buddhist narrative as guardian of the earth's witness to the Buddha's merit.

**The Naga (นาค):** Serpent-kings who dwell in underground rivers and seas. In Thai Buddhism, the naga is the protector of water sources and the guardian of temple boundaries. The famous stairway balustrades of Thai temples are flanked by multi-headed naga. The naga is also the sponsor of ordination — a young man entering the monkhood is called a naga before his head is shaved.

### Khmer Tradition (Cambodia)

**Devaraja — The God-King Theology:** The Khmer kings of Angkor (9th–15th centuries CE) governed through a theological-political system in which the king was identified as the earthly manifestation of a Hindu deity (Shiva in the early period, Vishnu in later reigns, Lokeshvara/Avalokiteshvara in the Buddhist period of Jayavarman VII). The state temple — Angkor Wat for Suryavarman II, identified with Vishnu; the Bayon for Jayavarman VII — was the earthly axis mundi, the body of the god-king extended in stone. The consecration of the linga (the Shiva-phallus) in the central tower of the state temple was the ritual act that constituted Khmer sovereignty.

**The Churning of the Sea of Milk (Samudra Manthan):** Depicted on the southern gallery of Angkor Wat in one of the largest narrative bas-reliefs in the world (nearly 50 meters long), this myth shows the gods (devas) and demons (asuras) cooperating to churn the cosmic ocean using Mount Meru as a spindle and the serpent Vasuki as a rope. From the churning emerge: the apsaras (celestial dancers), the divine physician Dhanvantari, the elephant Airavata, the horse Ucchaishravas, the goddess Lakshmi, and finally the amrita (nectar of immortality). The Khmer artistic treatment of this myth emphasizes the apsara (heavenly dancers) as central figures of divine beauty and cosmological order.

**The Naga as Cosmic Axis:** At Angkor, the naga serves multiple functions: as the rainbow bridge between the human world and the divine (the temple causeways are flanked by naga balustrades), as the world-serpent who supports the cosmic mountain, and as the primordial water-being from whom the Khmer people claim partial descent (the founding myth of Funan mentions a Brahmin marrying a naga princess).

### Indonesian / Balinese Tradition

Balinese Hinduism (Agama Hindu Dharma) is a living synthesis of Shivaite Hinduism, Buddhism, and indigenous Austronesian animism. It is practiced by roughly 3.9 million Balinese today and is one of the world's few surviving Hindu traditions in Southeast Asia. It is NOT a historical curiosity — it is an active religious system requiring care and respect.

**Sang Hyang Widhi Wasa:** The supreme being of Balinese theology — a monotheistic summit above the Hindu pantheon. The concept was formalized and emphasized in the 20th century partly in response to Indonesian national law requiring citizens to profess one of six recognized religions (monotheism was a bureaucratic requirement). However, the underlying theological concept of a supreme unity (the Brahman of Advaita Vedanta filtered through Balinese understanding) is older than the modern formulation.

**Batara Guru (= Shiva):** The supreme deity in the Indonesian Shivaite tradition, identified with the Indic Shiva but carrying local epithet and mythology. He is the teacher of all knowledge and the lord of the cosmos. In Balinese theology, Shiva-Batara Guru is typically invoked as the highest active divine principle below the supreme unity of Sang Hyang Widhi Wasa.

**Dewi Sri:** The rice goddess and the most intimately important deity of daily Balinese life. Her myth describes how she was killed by the gods (some versions say through jealousy or misadventure), and from her body grew the rice plant. She thus embodies the grain-mother archetype: the deity who dies and is reborn as the crop that sustains life. Her rituals permeate the agricultural calendar; the construction of a new rice barn, the first planting, the harvest — all involve offerings to Dewi Sri. She is paired with her consort Wisnu (Vishnu), and the rice-barn architecture itself represents their union. She is also connected to snakes — the rice-field snake is her guardian form.

**The Dewata Nawa Sanga — Nine Directional Gods:** A cosmological system of nine major deities assigned to the eight compass directions and the center, each with associated color, mount, and weapon. The center is occupied by Shiva (white, the navel of the cosmos); the other directions are held by Vishnu (black, north), Iswara (white-yellow, east), Mahadewa (yellow, west), Brahma (red, south), Sambhu (green, northeast), Sangkara (blue-green, northwest), Mahesora (pink, southeast), and Rudra (orange, southwest). The Dewata Nawa Sanga underpins Balinese temple orientation, calendar ritual, and mandala design.

**Barong:** The lion-spirit and leader of the hosts of good in the cosmos. He is most typically depicted as the Barong Ket — a magical lion whose ornate mask and shaggy body are animated by two dancers. Barong is a protector deity — a benevolent, cosmic guardian. His battles with Rangda are never finished; neither can permanently defeat the other. This is not a failure of good over evil — it is the Balinese theological position that cosmic balance requires the coexistence of opposing forces.

**Rangda:** The witch-queen and lord of the leyak demons. Leyak are malevolent spirits capable of shape-shifting who threaten the living. Rangda's image — with bulging eyes, fangs, pendulous breasts, long clawing hands, and a lolling flame-tongue — is one of the most immediately striking images in world religious art. She derives partly from the Hindu Durga/Kali complex and partly from indigenous Balinese traditions of the leak witch. In ritual drama, the kris-dance (Calonarang) enacts the Barong-Rangda combat; the entranced dancers attempt to stab themselves with kris (ritual daggers) but are supernaturally protected by Barong's blessing.

**Garuda:** The great eagle-king who is Vishnu's mount. In Balinese religion he is both a cosmic guardian and a national symbol — he appears as Garuda Wisnu Kencana, depicted in one of Bali's most significant modern sculptures (completed 2018), straddling the hill above Jimbaran. He is also the symbol of Indonesian national aviation and the state emblem.

**Bhoma:** The fierce face carved above temple gates (kori agung), derived from the Indic Kirtimukha ("face of glory") — a demonic face whose function is apotropaic, consuming impurity before it can enter the sacred space. In Bali he is understood as a son of Shiva and Prithvi (earth) and is both terrifying and protective.

### Polynesian Tradition (Pan-Polynesian with Maori Specifics)

Polynesian mythology spans the largest geographical area of any single cultural tradition — from Hawaii in the north to New Zealand in the southwest to Rapa Nui (Easter Island) in the east. The core mythological figures appear across this entire triangle with regional variation.

**Maui:** The supreme trickster-demigod of the Polynesian world. He appears in Hawaiian (Māui), Maori (Māui-tikitiki-a-Taranga), Samoan, Tongan, and Tahitian traditions. His exploits include: fishing up islands from the sea floor with a magical fishhook (in Maori tradition, the North Island of New Zealand IS the fish of Māui — Te Ika-a-Māui); lassoing the sun with braided flax rope to slow it down so his people would have enough daylight for cooking and work; obtaining fire for humanity from his ancestor Mahuika (goddess of fire) by trickery; and his final, fatal attempt to gain immortality by entering the sleeping body of Hine-nui-te-pō (the goddess of death) through her vagina while she sleeps, to reach her heart and invert the power of death. The fantail bird laughs and wakes the goddess; she crushes Māui between her thighs of obsidian, and he dies — and thus death entered the world for all humans. His character combines the heroic and the buffoonish, the creative and the destructive. He is associated with the demigod class — he has divine parents but is fully mortal in his vulnerability.

**Tāne (Maori / Pan-Polynesian):** God of forests, birds, and in the Maori tradition, the progenitor of humans. Tāne created the first human woman by shaping her from the soil of the earth (the name Hineahuone — "woman formed from earth") and then fathered upon her the first fully human being, Hinetītama. When Hinetītama later discovered that her father was also her husband, she fled in shame to the underworld, becoming Hine-nui-te-pō, the goddess of death — the same figure who would later kill Māui. Tāne is also the deity who separated Rangi and Papa, pushing the sky father upward with his upraised feet (or in some versions with trees) to let light into the world. In Hawaiian tradition his equivalent is Kāne, one of the four great gods of Hawaii (with Ku, Lono, and Kanaloa).

**Tangaroa:** God of the sea, fish, and marine creatures. He is the great opponent of Tāne (sea vs. forest) — when fish swim under logs and birds perch in trees, each is claiming territory for their patron deity. In Samoan tradition, Tagaloa (Tangaroa) is the supreme creator deity.

**Papatūānuku and Ranginui:** The Earth Mother and Sky Father — the primal couple of Maori cosmology. They lie locked in embrace from the beginning of time, pressing their children between them in darkness. Their children — Tāne, Tangaroa, Tūmatauenga, Rongo, Haumia-tiketike, and others — decide to push them apart to make space for the world of light. The weeping of Rangi for Papa produces the morning dew and the rain. The volcanic heat of earth is Papa's longing for her partner.

**Pele (Hawaiian):** The volcano goddess, creator of the Hawaiian Islands through her volcanic fires. She is the great-granddaughter of the supreme being and arrived in Hawaii by canoe from a distant homeland (Kahiki), driven out by her sister Nāmakaokahaʻi, goddess of the sea, after Pele seduced Nāmakaokahaʻi's husband. Pele traveled down the island chain creating volcanoes — each island represents a stage of her journey — until Nāmakaokahaʻi caught and dismembered her on Maui. But Pele's spirit settled on Hawai'i Island (the Big Island), where Kīlauea volcano is her home. She is alive as a cultural and religious force among Native Hawaiians today. She is also a shapeshifter who tests the hospitality of humans by appearing as an old woman or a beautiful young woman.

**Io (Maori):** A supreme being in some Maori tradition, presented as existing before Rangi and Papa, before Te Po, as a pure and self-existing spiritual power. His existence is debated by scholars — some argue Io was a pre-contact theological concept; others argue he was developed after contact with Christianity and monotheistic thought. The question of Io's antiquity is unresolved. His name was restricted knowledge in some traditions, known only to tohunga (ritual specialists).

**Rongo, Tu, Haumia-tiketike, Rūaumoko:** The remaining major children of Rangi and Papa. Rongo is the god of cultivated food crops (especially the kumara / sweet potato) and peace; his domain is the protected space of the cultivation ground. Tūmatauenga (Tu) is the god of war and humans — humans as a species are his creation or his charge. Haumia-tiketike governs wild, uncultivated food — the food of the forest floor. Rūaumoko is the god of earthquakes and the unborn — he was still in the womb of Papa when she and Rangi were separated; his movements in the earth cause earthquakes.

### Aboriginal Australian Tradition — HIGH CULTURAL SENSITIVITY GATE

**NOTICE TO LOREVAULT RESEARCHERS AND DESIGNERS:** The following section follows the protocols established in Section 7. Only categories and publicly acknowledged framing concepts are described. Sacred specifics, ceremony details, restricted knowledge, and community-specific traditions are NOT enumerated here and must NOT be sourced from secondary literature for game use.

**The Dreaming / Dreamtime:** The most foundational concept in Aboriginal Australian religious cosmology. The term "Dreamtime" (a translation of the Arrernte word altjira/alcheringa) is imprecise and has been largely superseded by "the Dreaming" in scholarly and community usage. The Dreaming is NOT a past creation event in the Western sense; it is not "how the world was made long ago." It is the ongoing, ever-present dimension in which ancestral beings continue to inhabit and constitute the landscape, the law, the relationship networks, and the moral fabric of existence. Past, present, and potential future intersect in the Dreaming. This is a fundamentally different ontology from the cosmogonies of Section 2.

**Country:** In Aboriginal Australian understanding, land is not territory in the Western legal sense but a living, spiritually active entity with which specific people have custodial, kinship, and spiritual relationships. Different nations and language groups are custodians of specific Country. The knowledge about Country — its sacred sites, its ancestral pathways, its stories — belongs to those custodians. This knowledge is not freely transferable or available for external cultural use.

**The Rainbow Serpent:** A widely distributed but NOT unified figure. Many different Aboriginal language groups across the continent have traditions involving a great serpent or water-snake being of immense creative and destructive power. These figures differ significantly between nations in name, role, gender, and association. It is incorrect to speak of "the Rainbow Serpent" as a single deity. LoreVault must not represent any specific community's Rainbow Serpent tradition without that community's consent.

**The Wandjina:** Cloud-spirit beings of the Kimberley region (Western Australia), associated with the Mowanjum and neighboring peoples. Their images — large-eyed, mouthless figures with halos — appear in rock art. These images are among the most restricted sacred objects in Australian Indigenous religious life. The Wandjina are living spiritual beings whose images remain the property of the communities that hold custodianship. Reproduction or commercial use of Wandjina imagery is not merely culturally insensitive — it is a direct violation of the rights of the Kimberley Aboriginal communities.

**The Seven Sisters (Pleiades Complex):** One of the most widely distributed story complexes in Aboriginal Australia, connecting different nations' traditions to the Pleiades star cluster. The specifics of each group's Seven Sisters story belong to those groups. The category and its astronomical connection are publicly acknowledged.

## 2. Cosmogony

### Polynesian Maori: From Void to World

The Maori cosmogony is among the most philosophically intricate origin narratives in world mythology. It proceeds not from a divine act of creation but from an ontological unfolding — being emerging from non-being through a sequence of increasing definition.

The sequence begins with **Te Kore** — the void, the nothing, the not-yet. But Te Kore is not a simple emptiness. The tradition lists multiple stages of Te Kore: Te Kore-tē-whiwhia (the void without possession), Te Kore-i-ai (the void of not-being), Te Kore-tuatahi, Te Kore-tuarua (first void, second void), and further progressions. This is not a linear absence but a taxonomy of nothingness — each stage a slightly more differentiated mode of not-yet-being.

From Te Kore emerges **Te Po** — darkness, also multiple-staged. Te Po-nui (the great dark), Te Po-roa (the long dark), Te Po-uriuri (the deep dark), Te Po-kerekere (the intense dark). In these stages of darkness, potential gathers.

From the darkness emerge first principles: Te Atea (space/light), then the primal couple — **Ranginui** (Sky Father) and **Papatūānuku** (Earth Mother). They lie locked in embrace, their children compressed between them in a world without light. The children eventually debate whether to push their parents apart or to kill them. Tāne's argument prevails: push them apart, let in the light. Tāne places his shoulders against his mother and his feet against his father and straightens upward — or in another version, he plants the first trees whose growth pushes the sky away. Light floods into the world. Rangi weeps for Papa; his tears are rain and dew.

This cosmogony is structurally significant: it does not require a creator deity. Being unfolds from non-being through its own internal logic. The gods are themselves products of the cosmogonic process, not its agents.

### Balinese: The Cosmic Egg and the Mountain Axis

The Balinese cosmogony, drawing on Indic Shaiva cosmology but shaped by Austronesian geography, begins with the cosmic egg (Brahma-anda) — the totality of potential being in undifferentiated form. From it emerge the male and female principles, identified with Siwa (Shiva) and Saraswati (or Uma/Parvati). The world is constituted through their generative interaction.

Central to Balinese cosmological geography is the **cosmic mountain** — Meru in the Sanskrit sources, but experienced locally as **Gunung Agung**, the great volcano of eastern Bali. Gunung Agung is the world-axis (axis mundi), the point where heaven, earth, and the underworld intersect. The cardinal orientation of Balinese spatial organization — kaja (toward the mountain / upward / sacred) vs. kelod (toward the sea / downward / impure) — is derived from this cosmological geography. All temples face kaja. The correct sleeping direction is kaja. The body's orientation in sacred space is always relative to the mountain.

The underworld is governed by the serpent Antaboga, who coils below the earth and whose movements cause the foundations of the world to shift.

### Aboriginal Australian: The Ongoing Constitution of the World

The Dreaming is NOT a cosmogony in the Western sense, and it must not be mapped onto Western creation-myth frameworks. The ancestral beings of the Dreaming did not create the world from outside it; they moved across a pre-existing landscape, singing, speaking, and acting — and in doing so, they shaped the features of the land (mountains, rivers, rock formations) and established the laws (marriage rules, ceremony obligations, kinship structures, food taboos) that continue to govern life.

The world is not a finished product; it must be continually renewed through ceremony. When ceremonies are not performed, the vitality of Country diminishes. The Dreaming is thus not a past event but an ongoing responsibility. This is perhaps the most profound cosmological distinction between Aboriginal Australian ontology and the cosmogonies of any other tradition in this dossier.

## 3. Pantheon-Conflict Events

### Maori: Māui's Death and the Entry of Death into the World

The death of Māui is one of the most mythologically significant deaths in any tradition because it is not presented as tragedy but as consequence — the inevitable result of hubris applied to the most fundamental limit. Māui has done the impossible before: he slowed the sun, he fished up an island, he stole fire. He believes he can defeat death by entering the sleeping body of Hine-nui-te-pō and extracting her heart. She is depicted as beautiful yet terrifying — her eyes flash like greenstone, her body is the threshold between the living world and the dead. Māui enters her body as she sleeps. The fantail bird, which has been following Māui and his companions through his adventures, cannot contain its laughter at the absurd sight. The bird's call wakes Hine-nui-te-pō. She contracts her body of obsidian and greenstone, crushing Māui to death.

The myth operates on several levels: it explains the origin of death (before this attempt, humans might have achieved immortality), it establishes the fantail as the bird associated with death omens in Maori culture (its presence near a house is a warning of death), and it presents Hine-nui-te-pō not as a villain but as the natural order — she was not malicious, only awakened. Māui's failure is not vilified; he is still the greatest of demigods. But some things cannot be defeated.

### Maori: Tāne vs. Tangaroa — The Cosmic Opposition of Forest and Sea

The enmity between Tāne (forests) and Tangaroa (sea) is not a single battle event but a structural cosmic opposition embedded in the natural world. Tangaroa's son Punga fathered both the fish (Ikatere) and the reptiles (Tū-te-wehiwehi). When Tāne and Tangaroa quarreled, the fish retreated to the sea under Tangaroa's protection, and the reptiles retreated to the forest under Tāne's. This is why: fish live in the sea and reptiles on land; when a log falls into the sea, that is Tāne's domain encroaching on Tangaroa's; when waves wash over the shore, Tangaroa reclaims territory. Human fishermen are taking Tangaroa's children with Tāne's help (wood canoes, flax nets) — they stand in the middle of an ancient cosmic conflict every time they fish.

### Balinese: The Eternal Battle of Barong and Rangda

Unlike most mythological conflicts, the battle between Barong and Rangda is designed never to end. This is theologically intentional. The Calonarang ritual drama — which can last through an entire night — enacts their combat repeatedly. Rangda summons her leyak forces; Barong rallies his followers. At the climax, the human followers of Barong, falling into trance, attempt to stab themselves with kris daggers to demonstrate their utter surrender of self to the conflict. They cannot be harmed because Barong's magical protection covers them (if a performer is genuinely wounded, it is understood as a failure of ritual preparation or an attack by Rangda's power that has been inadequately blocked). The battle is never resolved. A healer-priest (the pemangku or pedanda) must always bring the ritual to a safe close by restoring balance — not by defeating either side but by separating them. The cosmos requires both forces.

### Hawaiian: Pele and Nāmakaokahaʻi — Geology as Myth

The sequence of Hawaiian Islands from northwest to southeast corresponds precisely to their geological ages: the older, more eroded islands in the northwest, the youngest (and most volcanically active) in the southeast. The mythological narrative maps this exactly: Pele fled from Nāmakaokahaʻi from island to island, lighting volcanoes that her sister extinguished with the sea. At Maui she was caught and torn apart. But her spirit re-coalesced on Hawai'i Island, where Kīlauea — the youngest, most active volcano on the chain — remains unextinguished. The myth is a pre-scientific understanding of volcanic island chain formation — and given the Hawaiian hot-spot geology, it is structurally correct. Pele is still there, and the island chain is still growing to the southeast.

## 4. Forgotten / Adjacent Figures

**The Menehune (Hawaiian):** The little people of Hawaiian tradition — small, immensely strong, prodigiously skilled craftsmen and builders who work only at night and disappear before dawn. They are credited with building the Alekoko Fishpond (Menehune Fishpond) on Kaua'i and other large-scale pre-contact engineering works in a single night. Their origin is debated: some scholars argue they represent a folk memory of an earlier wave of Polynesian settlers (the so-called Mu or earlier peoples); others argue they are a mythological category analogous to the fairy-craftsmen of other traditions. In some accounts they are shy and peaceful; in others they are mischievous. They predate the arrival of the current Hawaiian peoples in the mythological timeline.

**The Patupaiarehe (Maori):** Fairy-folk of the Maori tradition — fair-skinned, red-haired beings who live in mist, on mountaintops, and in other liminal, between-places. They are associated with flute music (the koauau and nguru instruments) and with moonlight; they cannot tolerate sunlight, cooked food, or the smell of red ochre. Some scholars suggest they represent a folk memory of pre-Polynesian inhabitants of New Zealand (Melanesian or other Austronesian peoples who may have arrived earlier); others interpret them as purely mythological. Their music is beautiful and dangerously seductive — a man drawn to follow it may not return. They intermarried occasionally with humans; the descendants of such unions might have uncanny musical gifts.

**Te Kore — The Multiple Voids:** As noted in the cosmogony section, Te Kore is not merely "the void" but a philosophical taxonomy of non-being. In the Maori intellectual tradition, the careful enumeration of these stages of Te Kore represents a proto-philosophical investigation into the nature of potential — the distinction between absolute nothing and the nothing that contains the potential for something. This is an underexplored category in comparative mythology; it parallels in interesting ways the Kabbalistic concept of Ain (nothing), Ain Soph (limitless), and Ain Soph Aur (limitless light).

**Indonesian Pre-Hindu Animist Layer — The Hyang:** Beneath the Hindu overlay in Javanese and Balinese religion lies an older stratum of ancestral spirit veneration. The Hyang (sometimes Sang Hyang) are ancestral spiritual forces or exalted beings who were present before the Hindu deities arrived with Indianization. In Bali, the Sang Hyang trance rituals (including Sang Hyang Dedari, in which prepubescent girls are possessed by celestial nymphs) represent this pre-Hindu substratum. The Hyang are not demons or lesser spirits — they are the original sacred beings of the land, accommodated within rather than displaced by the later Hindu system.

**Vietnamese Pre-Sinicized Layer — Đạo Mẫu:** The Mother Goddess tradition is likely the oldest surviving stratum of Vietnamese religion, predating the Chinese administrative Daoist influence. Its core — the veneration of female cosmic powers as organizers of the four realms of existence — may reflect Austronesian or pre-Austronesian religious patterns. The tradition survived two millennia of Chinese cultural dominance, survived French colonial Christian missionization, survived Communist suppression (it was condemned as "feudal superstition" in the Democratic Republic of Vietnam and again in unified Vietnam). It was formally recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2016. Its survival across these pressures suggests a depth of cultural embedding that mere "superstition" cannot account for.

## 5. Cyclical Figures + Ritual Scenes

**Polynesian Tattooing as Cosmological Inscription:** In pre-contact Polynesian culture — particularly in Maori (tā moko), Samoan (pe'a / malu), Marquesan (which produced some of the most complex tattooing in the ancient world), and Hawaiian traditions — tattooing was not primarily decorative. The body was understood as a cosmological text. Genealogy, rank, spiritual power (mana), and relationship to the divine were inscribed on the body. In Maori tā moko, the facial tattoo encoded the wearer's complete ancestral and social identity — it was essentially a readable genealogical document and a visible sign of one's spiritual relationship to atua (gods) and tupuna (ancestors). The act of tattooing was itself a ritual performance requiring karakia (prayers) and involving the controlled release of tapu (sacred power/restriction). Tattooing the face was literally inscribing the cosmos — one's place in the cosmic hierarchy — on the most visible and personally potent part of the body.

**Hawaiian Hula as Cosmic Narrative:** Hula is not a form of entertainment in its original religious context. It is a ritual mode of embodied knowledge transmission through which the cosmogonic narratives, the genealogies of gods and chiefs, the natural and supernatural history of the islands, and the protocols of sacred relationship are maintained and transmitted. Different forms of hula correspond to different sacred contexts. The chants (mele) that accompany hula are the primary carrier of Hawaiian mythological and historical knowledge in the oral tradition. The hālau hula (hula school) was and remains a sacred institution. The figure of Laka, goddess of hula, presides over the altar in any hula space. The suppression of hula by American Christian missionaries in the 19th century was understood by Hawaiians as an attack on their entire theological and historical knowledge system — which is precisely what it was.

**Balinese Nyepi — The Day of Silence:** The Balinese New Year (Saka calendar, occurring in March/April) is celebrated through an act of total cessation. On Nyepi, no fire is lit, no work is performed, no lights are turned on, no one travels — the entire island goes dark and silent. The purpose is cosmological misdirection: the demonic forces (bhutas and kalas) who have been stirred up and drawn to Bali during the preceding ogoh-ogoh procession are to be confused into thinking the island is uninhabited and thus leave it alone. The night before Nyepi, enormous effigies (ogoh-ogoh) — grotesque, elaborate demons — are paraded through the streets by torch and gamelan, their purpose being to attract all the malevolent supernatural forces and concentrate them. Then silence. An island of 4.3 million people goes dark for 24 hours. This is one of the most remarkable collective ritual performances in the contemporary world.

**Vietnamese Tết — The Kitchen Gods' Annual Report:** The Vietnamese New Year ritual complex (Tết Nguyên Đán) is organized around the departure and return of the Táo Quân. On the 23rd day of the 12th lunar month, Vietnamese families burn paper offerings (a paper carp to carry the kitchen gods to heaven, paper clothes, paper money) and release live carp into water — the vehicle for the gods' celestial journey. The kitchen gods travel to Ngọc Hoàng's court and report on the moral conduct of the household for the preceding year. The family then prepares for New Year: cleaning the house (clearing away the old year's spiritual residue), preparing ancestral offerings, and welcoming the gods of fortune. The New Year's Eve and first three days are governed by a complex protocol of auspicious and inauspicious activities, designed to inscribe good fortune at the cosmological reset point of the new year. The first visitor to cross the threshold in the new year sets the household's fortune for the year.

**Aboriginal Australian Ceremony — Category Only:** Ceremonies exist across the hundreds of distinct Aboriginal Australian nations that renew the relationship between the living and the Dreaming, reinscribe the sacred pathways across Country, initiate young people into deeper levels of sacred knowledge, and maintain the vitality of the land. The specific nature, structure, participants, timing, and content of these ceremonies are restricted sacred knowledge and are not described here or to be sourced from secondary literature for LoreVault purposes.

## 6. Public-Domain Status

### Polynesian Mythology

The oral traditions of Polynesian peoples are ancient, with origins in the Lapita cultural complex (c. 1600–500 BCE) and their transmission across the Pacific in successive waves of Polynesian expansion. However, the problem of public-domain status is complex:

**Scholarly Collections:** The major 19th–20th century collections — Sir George Grey's *Nga Mahi a Nga Tupuna* (1854), Elsdon Best's extensive work on Maori religion and mythology (especially *Maori Religion and Mythology*, 1924), Sir Te Rangi Hiroa's (Peter Buck) *Vikings of the Sunrise* (1938) and *The Coming of the Maori* (1949), Martha Beckwith's *Hawaiian Mythology* (1940) — are either in the public domain or approaching it. The oral traditions they recorded are, in their ancient form, public domain in the conventional copyright sense. However, see the cultural-sovereignty note below.

**Disney's Moana (2016):** Copyright on all character designs, specific story elements created for the film, and the visual representation of Maui's tattoos (particularly the animated tattoos), Tamatoa's design, Te Fiti/Te Kā's design, and the Hook of Māui as depicted. The underlying mythology — Māui fishing up islands, Māui and the sun, the Polynesian concept of the Heart of Te Fiti does not exist in traditional mythology — is mixed. LoreVault must avoid visual similarity to Disney character designs and must NOT use story elements invented by Disney writers. Note: The film triggered significant protests from Hawaiian, Samoan, and other Pacific Islander communities over cultural appropriation concerns. The character design for Māui (extremely large, tattooed, with a magic fishhook) drew specific criticism.

**Public-Domain / Open Status:** The mythological narratives themselves as documented in pre-1925 ethnographic literature are public domain. Specific card designs should draw on academic sources rather than popular adaptations.

### Balinese / Indonesian Texts

**Palm-Leaf Manuscripts (Lontar):** The lontar manuscript tradition of Bali preserves an enormous body of literature — kakawin (Sanskrit-metre poetry adapted into Old Javanese), parwa (prose adaptations of the Mahabharata and Ramayana), and original Balinese compositions. The Agastya Parwa, the Adiparwa, the Uttarakanda — these are ancient texts, pre-1700, and are public domain in the conventional sense. Modern scholarly translations (e.g., by C. Hooykaas, I.G.N. Bagus) may carry modern copyright.

**Living Tradition:** As a living religious tradition, specific liturgical texts and ritual manuals used by pedanda priests may have community-ownership considerations beyond conventional copyright.

### Vietnamese Mythology

Vietnamese mythology is largely oral and folk in transmission. Written sources — including the 13th-century *Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái* (Extraordinary Tales from South of the Passes) and the 15th-century *Việt Điện U Linh* (The Spiritual World of the Việt Realm) — are ancient and fully public domain in copyright terms. Modern retellings and scholarly analyses are copyright-protected.

### Aboriginal Australian

**CRITICAL:** Aboriginal Australian Dreaming traditions are NOT in the public domain in any meaningful sense. They are:

1. Living cultural property of specific communities, transmitted under cultural law.
2. Subject to Indigenous Intellectual Property (IIP) frameworks, which have been increasingly recognized in Australian law.
3. Governed by the AIATSIS (Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies) Code of Ethics, which requires free, prior, and informed consent from relevant communities.
4. In many cases, restricted sacred knowledge — forbidden to those outside specific gender, age, or initiation categories even within the communities themselves.

The fact that some Dreaming stories appear in published children's books or tourist materials does NOT make them available for commercial game use. Do NOT use.

## 7. Cultural-Sensitivity Gates

### Aboriginal Australian — MAXIMUM GATE: EXCLUDED

**This is an absolute exclusion, not a cautionary advisory.**

The following specific rules apply:

1. **No Aboriginal Australian sacred figures** may be used in LoreVault cards, artwork, naming conventions, or flavor text.
2. **The Dreaming** may not be used as a game mechanic, zone name, card type, or thematic frame.
3. **The Rainbow Serpent** — any specific community's tradition — may not be used. If LoreVault wishes to develop a "cosmic serpent" archetype, this must be clearly derived from other traditions (Egyptian Apophis, Norse Nidhogg, Mesopotamian Tiamat) with no visual or textual reference to Aboriginal Australian traditions.
4. **The Wandjina** images and associated spiritual beings are controlled sacred property of the Mowanjum and neighboring Kimberley communities. Any reproduction of Wandjina-style imagery is a direct violation of those communities' rights. This is not merely an ethical consideration — it is an active area of Indigenous rights advocacy and the communities have pursued formal complaints against unauthorized commercial use.
5. **The Seven Sisters** and other sky-story complexes are restricted in their specifics; the category may be noted only.
6. **RECOMMENDATION:** LoreVault should commission formal consultation with AIATSIS and with relevant community-controlled organizations (e.g., local Land Councils, community cultural organizations) before any further consideration of Aboriginal Australian content. Community partnership — not mere acknowledgment — is the minimum standard for ethical engagement.

**Relevant frameworks:** AIATSIS Code of Ethics for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Research (2020); UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007, endorsed by Australia 2009); Australian Copyright Act 1968 (which lacks specific Indigenous Cultural Expression protections, a recognized gap); and the emerging framework of Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP) protocols.

### Hawaiian — HIGH GATE: PROCEED WITH CONSULTATION

Hawaiian cultural sovereignty is an active political and legal issue. The Kingdom of Hawaii was illegally overthrown by U.S.-backed forces in 1893; Hawaii's annexation and statehood occurred under contested sovereignty conditions that Native Hawaiian sovereignty movements continue to contest. In this context, the commercial use of Hawaiian religious and cultural figures carries political weight.

Specific concerns:
- **Pele:** Is a living spiritual reality for many Native Hawaiians. Her use in commercial products has been contested; the Pele Defense Fund has argued against uses of her name and image in commercial contexts.
- **Māui:** As noted above, the Disney Moana controversy demonstrates the scale of community concern. Any LoreVault representation of Māui should be demonstrably derived from ethnographic rather than popular sources, and should involve consultation with Hawaiian and broader Pacific Islander cultural organizations.
- **Hula, mele, and the hālau tradition:** Are not available for use as game mechanics or flavor elements without specific partnership.
- **The Hawaiian language (ʻŌlelo Hawaiʻi):** Use with care; the Hawaiian language revitalization movement is politically significant, and incorrect or trivializing use is noticed and opposed.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Engage with the Office of Hawaiian Affairs (OHA) or consult with Hawaiian cultural practitioners before finalizing any Hawaiian content.

### Maori — HIGH GATE: CONSULT RELEVANT IWI

Maori cultural taonga (treasures) are protected under New Zealand's Waitangi Treaty frameworks and the Māori Affairs Act. "Taonga" includes intangible cultural property — stories, imagery, techniques, and protocols that belong to specific iwi (tribes) and hapū (sub-tribes).

Specific considerations:
- **Tā moko (facial tattoo):** Deeply sacred. Each design is specific to an individual's genealogy. Generic "Maori tattoo" designs on LoreVault cards would be deeply offensive. Any tattooed figures should use generalized Polynesian patterns or partner with a Maori artist.
- **The haka:** A ceremonial performance with specific protocols of use; not available as generic "warrior display."
- **Specific whakapapa (genealogical narratives):** The detailed genealogies connecting Maori figures belong to specific iwi; pan-Maori generalization should be used with care.
- **Kaitiakitanga (guardianship):** The Maori concept that communities hold custodial rather than ownership relationships with sacred knowledge — they guard it for future generations. This framework means consent cannot be given by individuals but requires community process.

**RECOMMENDATION:** Engage with Te Papa Tongarewa (Museum of New Zealand) or Te Taura Whiri i te Reo Māori (the Māori Language Commission) for referral to appropriate iwi consultation.

### Balinese Hindu — ACTIVE TRADITION: RESPECT AND ACCURACY

Balinese Hinduism is a living faith with approximately 3.9 million practitioners. Cards depicting Barong, Rangda, Dewi Sri, or other Balinese figures must:
- Be demonstrably accurate to the tradition (not fused with generic "Asian fantasy" elements).
- Not depict the figures in degrading, sexualized, or satirical contexts.
- Not reproduce specific sacred mask designs (especially the Barong Ket face or the Rangda mask) in ways that could be confused with sacred objects.
- Note: The Balinese themselves have a robust tradition of depicting their sacred figures in art, carving, painting, and performance — this is culturally internal practice, not an argument for external appropriation.

### Thai / Khmer — ACTIVE TRADITIONS: BUDDHIST SENSITIVITY

The phi traditions, the Brahmanical court religion of Thailand, and the royal symbolism (Garuda, Phra Narai) are all active living elements of Thai culture. Representing Thai royal sacred symbols in game contexts requires care. The Thai lèse-majesté framework also means that any content conflating the royal institution with supernatural or demonic figures is genuinely dangerous in the Thai market context.

## 8. Cross-Pantheon Archetype Mapping

### Earth-Sky Separation

**Rangi and Papatūānuku (Maori)** = **Geb and Nut (Egyptian)** = **Ouranos and Gaia (Greek)** = **Dyaus Pita and Prithvi Mata (Vedic)** = **Nut and Geb inverted (Egyptian — sky goddess, earth god).**

The cosmic couple locked in primordial embrace, their children pressed between them, separated by a deliberate act that lets in light — this is an extraordinarily widespread pattern. In each case, the separation is both necessary (creating the space for life) and painful (an original loss that is mourned forever). In the Maori case the rain is Rangi's tears; in the Vedic case the morning dew has similar resonance. The separating agent varies: in Maori it is Tāne (a child of the couple, using upward force); in Greek tradition it is Ouranos' castration by Kronos; in Egyptian tradition it is Shu (air) lifting Nut.

**LoreVault Archetype:** PRIMORDIAL SEPARATION. Card concept: the moment of cosmic sundering that creates the possibility of all subsequent existence.

### Trickster-Demigod

**Māui (Polynesian)** = **Hermes (Greek)** = **Loki (Norse)** = **Anansi (West African/Caribbean)** = **Sun Wukong (Chinese)** = **Coyote (various Native American)** = **Eshu/Elegba (Yoruba).**

The trickster-demigod is one of the most universal mythological characters. Shared features: transgression of cosmic boundaries, use of cleverness over force, simultaneous creation and destruction, dual nature (beneficial and dangerous), often associated with communication, crossroads, and liminality. Māui specifically parallels Prometheus (theft of fire for humanity, punishment following cosmic transgression) and Hermes (the guide who operates across boundaries). His fishing-up of islands parallels the creative acts of tricksters who shape the physical world through unexpected means.

**LoreVault Archetype:** THE TRICKSTER-SHAPER. What distinguishes Māui from pure tricksters: his acts are cosmogonic (he physically reshapes the world), not merely social (he doesn't just trick characters but rearranges geography and time).

### Volcano / Sacred Fire

**Pele (Hawaiian)** = **Hephaestus (Greek, forge-fire)** = **Agni (Vedic)** = **Sekhmet (Egyptian, fire-rage)** = **Vulcan (Roman)** = **Pele's partial parallel to the Promethean fire-bringer.**

Pele is notably distinct from the forge-fire deities: she is not a craftsman deity but a force of raw geological creation. Her fire makes land — it is generative, not merely useful or destructive. She is perhaps better mapped to the Vedic notion of tapas (the creative heat of asceticism that produces the world) or to the concept of the demiurge as active creative force.

**LoreVault Archetype:** PRIMAL FIRE / GEOLOGICAL CREATION. Note the gendered distinction: most forge-fire deities are male (Hephaestus, Vulcan, Goibhniu); Pele's female volcanic fire suggests a distinct archetype of earth-womb creation through heat.

### Grain-Mother / Death-and-Rebirth Deity

**Dewi Sri (Balinese)** = **Demeter (Greek)** = **Persephone (Greek, grain cycle)** = **Osiris (Egyptian, grain-king)** = **Inanna's descent (Sumerian)** = **the Corn Mother (various Mesoamerican and Native American traditions).**

Dewi Sri specifically parallels Osiris more than Demeter: she is killed, and from her dead body the grain grows. This is the dying-deity-as-grain archetype: the deity's death IS the harvest, not merely the mythological explanation for it. In psychological terms this archetype encodes the fundamental paradox of agriculture: life depends on death; the seed must "die" in the earth to produce new growth.

**LoreVault Archetype:** THE GRAIN SACRIFICE. Dewi Sri's version is particularly pure: no rescue, no descent-and-return, just death and abundant aftermath.

### Death-by-Entry / Body-as-Death-Gate

**Māui's death in the body of Hine-nui-te-pō** = **Enkidu's death after lying with Shamhat (death enters through sexual/feminine encounter)** = **Jonah in the whale (descent into the body of death, survival inverted)** = **Inanna in the underworld (descent through successive gates into death's domain)** = **Orpheus descending to Hades** = **The motif of the hero entering the world-serpent.**

This archetype — the hero attempting to enter death's body and being destroyed or transformed — maps the category of impossible heroism: the act that crosses the one uncrossable boundary. Māui's specific version is distinguished by its humor (the laughing bird) and its finality — there is no rescue, no return. This places it closer to Enkidu's fate than to Inanna's (who returns) or Jonah's (who is disgorged).

**LoreVault Archetype:** THE LAST THRESHOLD. The attempt to reverse the irreversible.

### Cosmic Serpent

**The Rainbow Serpent (Aboriginal Australian — NOTE ONLY: category applicable, specifics excluded)** = **Apophis (Egyptian)** = **Nidhogg (Norse)** = **Tiamat (Mesopotamian)** = **Vritra (Vedic)** = **the Naga of Southeast Asia** = **the Leviathan (Hebrew)** = **Quetzalcoatl in serpent aspect (Mesoamerican).**

The cosmic serpent is among the most globally distributed mythological archetypes: a vast serpentine being associated with primordial water, chaos, creation, and destruction. In different traditions it is defeated and from its body the world is made (Tiamat), it is an ongoing threat to cosmic order (Apophis, Nidhogg), or it is the guardian of the cosmic waters (Naga). The Southeast Asian Naga occupies a particularly nuanced position — it is both dangerous and protective, both chaos-threatening and temple-guarding. For LoreVault: the cosmic serpent archetype is available from multiple non-restricted traditions (Egyptian, Norse, Mesopotamian, Vedic, Southeast Asian Hindu); the Aboriginal Australian tradition must not be drawn upon.

**LoreVault Archetype:** THE WORLD-SERPENT. Note: can be developed without any reference to Aboriginal Australian traditions by drawing on the rich Egyptian/Norse/Mesopotamian/Vedic versions.
