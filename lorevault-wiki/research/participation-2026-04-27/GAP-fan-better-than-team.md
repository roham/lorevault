# GAP: When Fan Contraband Is Better Than the Core Team's

**Gap statement:** V1 designs canon promotion as a rare exception — at most 1 fan-promoted Pane per Series, 5-15 Canon Marks per cycle by Series 5, with the Hub as the actual destination for 99% of contributions. This treats the fan contribution as something the Council carefully selects from. It does not design for the inverted scenario: a fan contributor whose work is consistently, demonstrably better than the staff lore team's output, whose Pane proposal is the most beloved Pane of Series 6, whose narrative voice becomes the project's defining voice. V1 also does not address the now-unavoidable adjacent question: what happens when a fan submission is suspected to be AI-generated, when an AI-assisted submission is the best entry of a cycle, or when the very category of "human-authored" becomes contested.

## Why this matters

Three reasons:

1. **Meritocratic discomfort is the test of editorial integrity.** Every fan-content platform that has lasted has faced this scenario. SCP-173, the wiki's flagship article, was a fan submission — and the legal entanglement around its authorship (Shimoda's photograph, the eventual replacement of the canonical image) is a cautionary tale about not designing for the fan-better-than-team case in advance. Homestuck's most beloved character arcs were collaborative with fans whose contributions were not credited cleanly. RWBY's strongest villain reveal was a fan suggestion. The architecture must answer: when this happens at LoreVault, what is the right response, and is the institutional posture pre-committed?

2. **The Council has a structural conservatism bias.** A 5-of-7 supermajority requirement, a "1 fan-promoted Pane per Series, ever" cap, and 4 staff seats out of 7 produces a Council that defaults to under-promoting. If the steady-state fan contribution quality is genuinely high (which V1 hopes for and the contraband framing is designed to produce), the Council will systematically reject canon-eligible work because the volume cap binds before the quality bar binds. This is not capture; it is constraint mismatch. V1 hopes the Council bottleneck never matters; the meritocratic-uncomfortable scenario is exactly the case where it does.

3. **AI-assisted submissions are now the base case, not an edge case.** As of 2026, any fan-canon platform that does not have an explicit policy on AI-assisted submissions has an implicit one — usually "we'll know it when we see it" — and that policy is failing at every comparable platform. AO3's ongoing AI policy debate, Wikipedia's draft policies on LLM-edited content, the SCP wiki's 2024-2025 contentious vote on AI-generated articles, all demonstrate that the absence of a clear position becomes an editorial crisis. V1 is silent on this and cannot remain so.

## Analysis

The "fan better than team" scenario decomposes into four cases, each requiring different responses:

### Case 1: The breakout single contributor

A specific fan contributor — call her A — produces 8 Hub submissions in S4-S5, of which 6 are unanimously hailed as the best work of the cycle by the community, with cross-reference counts dwarfing staff submissions. The Council promotes 4 of her entries to canon. By S6 she is the most-cited contributor in the entire archive. Should she be hired? Promoted to the Council? Given a Pane to author? V1 does not say.

The right answer is some combination of all three, executed in an order that does not consume her or the platform. The wrong answer (Homestuck pattern) is to absorb her labor into the project without proportional power, attribution, or compensation. The other wrong answer (RWBY Fan Forge pattern) is to gatekeep her with corporate process and watch her leave for a competitor.

### Case 2: The fan-Pane that eclipses staff Panes

A full-Pane proposal, accepted into the canon-eligible queue in S5 with 12-month review, emerges as the strongest Pane of S7 by every measurable metric — collector demand for the cards is highest, the lorebook chapters are most-read, the secondary-market floor on the Pane's cards is highest. The contributor receiving 1-3% royalties on the affected cards (V1 §2 Tier 4) does well financially. But the staff team's other two S7 Panes are demonstrably less successful by the same metrics. What does this do to staff morale, to staff Pane priority in S8, to the implicit contract between staff and fan contributors?

V1 does not address this. The architecture's implicit answer is "the staff are paid a salary and are professionals, they will absorb the meritocratic outcome gracefully." Empirically this is not how creative organizations behave under sustained fan-outperformance pressure.

### Case 3: The AI-assisted submission

A submission is suspected to be partially or fully LLM-generated. The Council cannot reliably detect this (no detector is reliable as of 2026). The work is good. Promote, reject, or other? V1 has no answer.

### Case 4: The fan whose Pane is so strong it implies the rest of the canon is wrong

The hardest case. A fan-Pane is so well-conceived that it implies a retconning of three existing Panes — its contraband, properly admitted, contradicts the cosmological substrate. The cross-Pane-compatibility rubric (V1 §4) would reject it. But the rejection cost is: the platform refuses canonically-superior work in defense of canonically-prior work. SCP's "Wanderer's Library" and the Foundation's various canon schisms emerged from exactly this dynamic.

## Design recommendations

Five additions, structurally compatible with V1.

### 1. The "Resident Author" track

Create a structural promotion path for breakout contributors: the **Resident Author** designation. A contributor who has earned 3+ Canon Marks is eligible. Resident Authors:

- Receive a salary or retainer from the LoreVault Treasury, sized to compete with mid-tier published-author income (~$60-120K/yr), funded from the LORE Treasury allocation.
- Are commissioned for explicit Pane work, with full credit, full royalty under the V1 Tier-4 royalty mechanism, and the option to author a future Series Pane.
- Receive an automatic seat on the Canon Council nomination pool, with the explicit expectation that they will rotate onto the Council within 1-2 election cycles.
- Retain the option to refuse the designation. A contributor who wants to remain a fan-contributor without becoming staff-equivalent can decline. The path is offered, not imposed.

This is the [HBO writer's-room poaching pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writers%27_room) combined with the Marvel-Comics breakout-creator track (see Brian Michael Bendis's path from indie creator to Marvel architect). It explicitly designs for the case where the fan is professional-grade.

The Resident Author track resolves the Homestuck failure mode (talent absorption without compensation) and the RWBY failure mode (talent rejection through gatekeeping) by offering a third path: structural elevation with retention of attribution and economic upside.

### 2. The flexible Pane promotion cap

V1 caps fan-promoted Panes at 1 per Series. This should become a soft default with a Council-supermajority override. Specifically: the 1-per-Series cap is the bylaw default; the Council may, by the same 5-of-7 supermajority that promotes any individual Pane, raise the cap to 2 in any given Series with explicit justification published in the deliberation log. The cap is also amendable downward — a Series may have zero fan-promoted Panes if the Council judges no submission meets the bar.

The hard cap (V1's "ever") is replaced by a constitutional cap: more than 2 fan-promoted Panes per Series requires a constitutional amendment. This preserves the conservatism of V1 in normal operation while admitting the meritocratic-uncomfortable case as a structural possibility.

### 3. AI-assisted submission policy

Adopt the **disclosure-and-evaluate** policy, not the **detect-and-reject** policy.

Specifically:

- **Submission interface adds a required field**: "Did you use AI tools in producing this submission, and if so, how?" A free-text disclosure. Not optional.
- **Categories of disclosed use**: (i) no AI use, (ii) AI used for ideation/brainstorming, (iii) AI used for drafting with substantial human revision, (iv) AI used for substantial generation with light human editing, (v) primarily AI-generated.
- **Council policy on each category**: (i)-(iii) are reviewed identically. (iv) is reviewed under stricter scrutiny — Council must affirm the human creative contribution is the load-bearing element. (v) is rejected from canon promotion but admitted to the Hub with explicit (v)-tag visible to readers.
- **Undisclosed AI use, if discovered, is grounds for revocation** of canon-promoted status (using the same constitutional process used for un-promoting any canon entry). The Authorship NFT is not burned but is overlay-tagged with the violation.
- **Authorship NFTs for category (v) submissions are tagged accordingly** in metadata. A reader of the Hub knows what they are reading.

This is the [academic-publication AI-disclosure policy](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-023-00191-1) translated to fan-canon. It is enforceable, transparent, and does not depend on unreliable detection.

The disclosure framework also handles the rarer case of AI-generated submissions that are genuinely good: they are admitted to the Hub, labeled, read, and not promoted to canon. The platform produces lore in many forms but reserves canon for human authorship.

### 4. The retconning protocol

When a fan-Pane proposal would, if admitted, contradict prior canon, the Council has three options instead of two:

- **Reject** (V1 default).
- **Accept with patch** — the contributor revises the proposal so it is compatible with prior canon (V1 implicit option).
- **Accept with retcon** (new) — the Council explicitly publishes a deliberation document acknowledging that prior canon is being amended to admit the new contraband. The retcon is rare (target: zero per Series in steady state), requires 6-of-7 Council supermajority, and triggers a 60-day public comment period during which any Authorship-NFT-holder can file a formal objection that the Council must address in a written response.

The retcon path is the architecture's admission that canon is not infallible and that the fan-canon Hub can, occasionally, produce work that improves the substrate. SCP's various canonical revisions, Wikipedia's article-merge mechanism, and the [Marvel Comics retcon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retroactive_continuity) tradition are the precedent.

### 5. Staff-fan parity in the Council deliberation interface

A subtle but important change: when the Council reviews a submission, the interface displays no information about whether the submitter is staff, Resident Author, or Tier-3 first-timer (except for the proven-contributor / first-time fast-track distinction described in the hostile-actor gap). Authorship is revealed only after the deliberation vote is recorded. This is the [orchestral blind-audition pattern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blind_audition) translated to lore-Council. It defends against the Council's structural bias toward known authors and forces the work to win on its own merits.

## What this changes about V1

V1 §2 (Tier 4) now admits the Resident Author as a sub-tier above standard Tier-4. V1 §4 (Council process) admits the disclosure-and-evaluate AI policy and the retcon protocol. V1 §5 (the Contraband interface) adds the AI-disclosure field. V1 §7 (rollout) adds the Resident Author program to S5 enablement, after the first canon promotions in S4 establish that the canon-promotion path is real.

The deepest change is institutional posture. V1 reads as if the Council is the designer's primary protection against fan-canon overflow. After this gap analysis, V2 should read as if the Council is the designer's primary protection against fan-canon being *under-promoted*. The bottleneck risk runs in the direction the doctrine demanded: canon should be hard to enter but not impossible, slow but not closed.

The fan-better-than-team scenario is the architecture's success case, not its failure case. If it never occurs, the platform is producing a passive-collector market with a decorative Hub. If it occurs and the architecture handles it gracefully, LoreVault has done what no major fan-content platform before it has done: built a system where fan contraband can become canonical contraband on equal terms, with attribution and compensation that survive corporate failure.
