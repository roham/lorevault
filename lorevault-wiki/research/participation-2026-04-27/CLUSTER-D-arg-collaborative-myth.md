## 1. Ecosystem Overview

### SCP Foundation
The SCP Foundation wiki launched in earnest on the [Something Awful forums](https://www.somethingawful.com) in 2008, originally as a thread where users collaboratively wrote bureaucratic-horror "containment files" for anomalous objects. The first entry, SCP-173, was authored by an anonymous user in 2007. By 2008, the project had migrated to a dedicated Wikidot instance.

Scale as of late 2025: the English-language wiki alone hosts over 9,800 SCP articles plus thousands of supplemental tales, canon hubs, and format-of-interest entries. Sixteen official foreign-language branches exist — German, Korean, Japanese, Russian, Thai, Spanish, Polish, Italian, French, Ukrainian, Portuguese, Czech, Simplified Chinese, Traditional Chinese, and Vietnamese — each with their own governance structures. The 2025 Public Domain Contest attracted 119 entries from 34 teams representing 84 distinct participating authors in a single event, which gives a lower-bound proxy for active contributor density. Total content pages across all language branches number in the tens of thousands; community estimates for cumulative distinct English contributors consistently land above 10,000 registered accounts with meaningful edit histories.

The SCP Foundation is frequently cited as the largest single-universe collaborative fiction project in internet history.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse
[Marble Hornets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marble_Hornets) is an American found-footage web series created by Troy Wagner, Joseph DeLage, and Tim Sutton. The first video was posted to YouTube on June 20, 2009 — ten days after Eric Knudsen published the original Slender Man images in a Something Awful Photoshop thread. Marble Hornets was not a loose adaptation; it essentially created the narrative grammar that defined what "Slenderman ARG" meant.

By the time the series concluded in 2014, the channel had accumulated approximately 80 million views across 92 episodes and nearly 400,000 subscribers. This success catalyzed several independent successor series — most significantly [EverymanHYBRID](https://everymanhybrid.fandom.com/wiki/EverymanHYBRID) (launched 2010) and [TribeTwelve](https://theslenderman.fandom.com/wiki/TribeTwelve) (launched 2010) — collectively dubbed the Slenderverse. The Slenderverse at peak represented perhaps 15–20 distinct active series worldwide, each independently operated.

### 17776 / 20020 (Jon Bois)
[17776](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/17776) — formally titled *What Football Will Look Like in the Future* — is a multimedia narrative by Jon Bois hosted on SB Nation, published July 5–15, 2017. It uses embedded video, HTML canvas animation, text chat transcripts, and still imagery to tell a story about immortal humans playing endless football games in a far future. The work was emphatically single-author and completed in ten days: 25 chapters at a daily cadence, no collaborative input during production.

Reception was extraordinary: Bois reported 700,000 unique visitors and 4 million total page views within the first two weeks, with an average 11-minute engagement time — remarkably high for long-form web writing. A sequel, [20020](https://17776-an-american-football-story.fandom.com/wiki/20021), published on Secret Base in September–October 2020, generated comparable enthusiasm.

The fandom is primarily Tumblr-resident with an AO3 tail (approximately 20 fanfiction works as of mid-2025). The work has no official wiki, no official Discord, and no official fan-participation apparatus. Its fandom is entirely organic.

### The Magnus Archives
[The Magnus Archives](https://rustyquill.com/show/the-magnus-archives/) is an audio drama horror podcast produced by Rusty Quill, written by Jonathan Sims, that ran from 2016 to 2021 across 200 episodes (five seasons). The show follows an archivist at a paranormal research institute recording "statements" from witnesses of supernatural events. The sequel series, [The Magnus Protocol](https://rustyquill.com/show/the-magnus-protocol/), launched January 2024.

The Magnus Archives surpassed 100 million downloads on Spotify — one of only a small number of British podcasts to achieve this milestone — and was awarded a formal accolade by Spotify for the achievement. It is the second-most-popular podcast fandom on AO3 after Critical Role. The Magnus Archives tag on AO3 had over 11,486 works as of 2021, and has continued to grow substantially; community estimates from 2024 place the total at well over 20,000 works. A licensed tabletop RPG published by Monte Cook Games raised over $550,000 on Kickstarter — more than five times its target. The Rusty Quill Big Bang fan event, an organized fan creation challenge, runs annually and encompasses multiple Rusty Quill properties.

### The Silt Verses
[The Silt Verses](https://www.thesiltverses.com/) is a horror/fantasy serial audio drama created by Jon Ware (writing, sound design) and Muna Hussen (directing) under the Eskew Productions banner. The series follows two worshippers of an outlawed river deity traveling their god's dark waters searching for revelation. It ran for 46 episodes across three seasons from January 4, 2021 to July 22, 2024, concluding deliberately.

The Silt Verses is smaller-scale than Magnus Archives — it is niche enough to operate without a major publisher — but generated an intense, intimate fandom organized primarily on Tumblr (thesiltverses.tumblr.com), with fan wiki documentation on Fandom. The creators actively engage with their community: during the 2024 final-season Q&A (a four-hour live event), voice actors and creators fielded fan questions and engaged in improvised performance. An RPG supplement for the Silt Verses universe was produced and released to Patreon backers. The show is distributed through Rusty Quill's network.

### New Albion / Steam Powered Giraffe
[Steam Powered Giraffe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_Powered_Giraffe) is a San Diego-based theatrical steampunk musical act founded in 2008 that performs as Victorian-era humanoid robots created by a fictional inventor, Peter Walter I. Their mythology is elaborated across albums, stage performances, and an ongoing webcomic primarily authored by Isabella Bennett.

The band formalized fan participation through [The Cavalcadium](https://steampoweredgiraffe.com/index.php/lore/) — an official fan community website with forums, a donation subscription program ("The Engineer-eteer Program") providing access to behind-the-scenes content, digital discography, live art streams, and rare recordings. Fan characters have achieved semi-canonical status: "Walter Tech Carissa" originated as a fan costume that was formally acknowledged in lore, and "Matter Mistress Bunny" — a concept by former band member Bunny Bennett — entered deep community canon even without a physical stage appearance. The Walter Workers (originally "Walter Girls"), who manage merchandise and perform at shows, are incorporated into the in-universe mythology as "blue matter engineers."

[New Albion](https://paulshapera.com/dolls-of-new-albion/) is Paul Shapera's steampunk opera trilogy — *The Dolls of New Albion* (2012), *The New Albion Radio Hour*, and *The New Albion Guide to Analogue Consciousness* — a genre-spanning musical narrative about a fictional Victorian-steampunk city. Shapera's community is smaller and operates primarily via Bandcamp and a Discord server (NADS), with no formal canon-contribution mechanism.

### Mountain Goats — In League With Dragons Cluster
[In League With Dragons](https://www.pastemagazine.com/music/the-mountain-goats/the-mountain-goats-in-league-with-dragons-review) (2019) is The Mountain Goats' twentieth studio album, conceived originally as a rock opera about an aging wizard defending the seaside town of Riversend. John Darnielle describes the genre as "dragon noir." The album does not resolve into a tight linear narrative, instead offering evocative fragments — character names like "Who Shall Never Fail" and "Fortress of River's End" — that fans organize into headcanon and fan theory.

The Mountain Goats' primary fan community lives on Tumblr, where John Darnielle maintains an active personal account (johndarnielle.tumblr.com) and directly responds to fans. The Mountain Goats Wiki on Fandom documents the band's extensive internal mythology (the "Alpha Couple," recurring characters like the Master, the Beast Handlers, various linked album storylines). Darnielle's interactive posture is distinct: he treats fans as co-interpreters, not co-authors. He answers fan theories about lore without either confirming or denying them categorically, preserving interpretive openness.

In 2018, Darnielle was the subject of a significant community reckoning on Tumblr: several young fans published accounts of what they described as emotional boundary violations in parasocial correspondence. Darnielle acknowledged the accounts, issued a public apology, and the fandom navigated the incident without a permanent structural rupture, though trust in the parasocial fan-creator relationship was measurably damaged.

## 2. Participation Architecture

### SCP Foundation: Open Wiki with Staged Gatekeeping
The SCP Foundation uses what might be called **graduated open architecture**. The content is hosted on Wikidot under CC-BY-SA 3.0. Anyone can read everything with zero friction. However, to publish an article, a user must:

1. Become a site member (account registration, email verification).
2. Join the official Discord or IRC chatroom and receive community critique for their concept at the **Ideas Critique** stage before writing a full draft.
3. Post a draft to the **Drafts & Critiques forum** and receive community feedback over a period of days or weeks.
4. Publish the article, at which point the community voting system takes over.

This is not a gatekeeper model in the editorial sense — no individual approves content before posting — but it is a **community-filtration model** before publication. The Drafts forum serves as a soft pre-publication firewall. Experienced community members provide line-level critique. Staff do not pre-approve articles but do enforce the post-publication voting rules rigorously.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse: Distributed Creator Model
The Slenderverse had no shared participation architecture at all. Each series was an independent creative entity. Marble Hornets (made by THAC, the production company of Wagner, DeLage, and Sutton) was entirely closed: a small creative team produced the work, and fans consumed and theorized. EverymanHYBRID and TribeTwelve were each similarly creator-closed.

The "collaboration" in the Slenderverse was horizontal — between distinct creator teams who maintained separate series and occasionally produced crossover content. The December 2013 crossover between EMH and TribeTwelve (the "Envelope" video and the "Noah" response video) was a bilateral agreement between two production teams, not an open wiki event. Fan participation was limited to forum theorizing, Reddit speculation threads, and Twitter/Tumblr engagement with creators.

ARGs in the classical sense — where the audience solves puzzles that shape the narrative — existed at the margins of the Slenderverse (some series included email addresses and coded messages for fans to decode), but Marble Hornets itself was primarily a passive-viewer experience with thin interactivity.

### 17776 / Jon Bois: Author-Closed, Fan-Open
Participation architecture here is binary and explicit. Jon Bois makes the thing. Fans read it, write about it, theorize about it, and produce fanfiction about it on AO3. There is no official apparatus for fan input to the canon. Bois does not solicit plot suggestions or canon extensions from the audience. The fandom's participation is entirely post-hoc interpretation.

This is the cleanest version of what might be called **the author-oracle model**: the creator produces canonical artifacts at intervals; the community builds interpretive superstructure around those artifacts between releases.

### The Magnus Archives: Creator-Owned, Fan-Welcomed
Rusty Quill operates as a professional podcast production company. Jonathan Sims as head writer held sole canon authority over the Magnus Archives storyline throughout the run. Fan participation in canon was structurally zero — no listener contributed to the plot. However, Rusty Quill operated an unusual **affirmative fan-permission architecture**:

- All Rusty Quill online content is distributed under a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike (CC-BY-NC-SA) license, making non-commercial fan remixing formally legal and encouraged.
- Rusty Quill published an explicit [Fan Works Notice](https://rustyquill.com/fan-works-notice/) clarifying what fan works are permitted and inviting fans to contact them for edge-case guidance.
- The Magnus Archives Fanfiction Showcase (2023) was a live streamed event — a Kickstarter stretch goal — where creators Jonathan Sims and Alexander J Newall read fan-submitted fiction on stream. The showcase accepted AU, canon-divergence, and fix-it fics, with an age-rating cap on explicit content.

This creates a **two-tier architecture**: canon is authored by professionals and closed to fan input; fan interpretation is explicitly licensed and publicly celebrated.

### The Silt Verses: Intimate Parasocial Architecture
Jon Ware and Muna Hussen maintain direct fan communication through Tumblr, where they repost fan art, answer questions, and announce updates. The fan wiki is entirely community-built with no creator oversight. The creators produced RPG content accessible to Patreon backers, allowing fans to mechanically engage with the world's systems in a structured way. The architecture is closer to an independent creator's Patreon community than to a formal collaborative institution — small enough that individual fan questions receive individual creator responses.

### Steam Powered Giraffe: Institutional Participation Funnel
SPG's Cavalcadium model is the most formalized fan-participation architecture in this set (outside of SCP). Fans can:
- Access exclusive content via the Engineer-eteer paid subscription.
- Post in official forums and receive creator acknowledgment.
- Submit fan costume/character concepts that may be canonically absorbed (as with Walter Tech Carissa).
- Participate in the Walter Workers in-character role if they attend shows and are selected.

This is an **embodied participation model**: physical attendance at shows is a genuine site of canonical contribution.

## 3. Quality Control Mechanisms

### SCP Foundation: Upvote/Downvote with Staff Deletion Tripwires
The SCP Foundation's quality control architecture is elaborate and well-documented on the [Deletions Guide](https://scp-wiki.wikidot.com/deletions-guide):

- Every page carries a public upvote/downvote module. All registered members can vote.
- A page scoring **-10 net** becomes eligible for deletion. Three staff votes for deletion (with 24-hour grace period) triggers removal.
- A page scoring **-20 net** has its grace period suspended and can be removed immediately once three staff deletion votes are recorded.
- If the rating rises back above -10, all pending deletion votes are voided.
- Staff do not pre-approve articles, but dedicated **critique infrastructure** (Drafts forum, Ideas forum, Discord) acts as a pre-publication filter. Community norms strongly encourage using critique forums before posting.

The result is a **market-mechanism quality floor** combined with **community pre-publication guidance**. Low-quality articles that bypass the critique pipeline face rapid downvoting; the deletion system removes persistent failures automatically. Staff serve as referees of process, not as editors of content.

Significant criticism exists within the community about **voting bias**: established authors receive reflexive upvotes regardless of quality; new contributors receive reflexive skepticism. The wiki's own forum thread "Abolish the No Vote, Downvote Your Friends" (published internally) catalyzed discussion about systematic bias in the voting mechanism.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse: Creator Taste as the Only Filter
Quality control in the Slenderverse was entirely subjective: the producing team decided what went out. There were no cross-series editorial standards. This created massive quality variance across the 15–20 active series at peak: Marble Hornets and EverymanHYBRID were tightly produced; many smaller entries were amateur in the fullest sense. The community developed informal canonical tiers — some series were recognized as "major Slenderverse" and others quietly ignored — but this categorization was emergent audience consensus, not any formal governance.

### 17776: No Quality-Control Problem (Single Author)
Single-author work has no quality-control architecture because it needs none. Quality control is editorial self-discipline, and Bois's reputation speaks for itself. The fanfiction community on AO3 operates under AO3's own tagging and curation norms.

### The Magnus Archives: Professional Production Standards
Rusty Quill as a company applies professional podcast production quality control — script review, recording, professional sound design, voice direction. No audience-facing quality control mechanism exists because the audience does not contribute to canon. For fan works, no quality control exists; the Creative Commons license allows any fan work regardless of quality, and Rusty Quill has no curation layer for the fan ecosystem beyond what the AO3 community self-organizes.

### The Silt Verses: Creator Quality, Community Wiki Curation
Quality control is entirely creator-side for canon. The fan wiki is community-maintained without formal moderation beyond Fandom's generic platform rules. Quality in the fan community is regulated by social norms: creators' Tumblr reposts signal community approval; fan fiction on AO3 is organized by tags and kudos.

## 4. Reward / Motivation Structure

### SCP Foundation: Status, Attribution, Community Membership
The SCP Foundation offers contributors:
- **Attribution**: Every article carries the byline of its author(s), permanently, under CC-BY-SA. Authors are named in the page footer.
- **Status**: High-rated articles carry community prestige. Authors of SCP-049, SCP-682, SCP-939, and other iconic entries are recognized community figures.
- **Community membership**: Being an established SCP author provides access to community culture, events, and recognition in the Discord/IRC ecosystem.
- **Derivative monetization rights**: CC-BY-SA allows contributors to monetize their own SCP articles commercially (games, merchandise, adaptations) as long as derivatives are CC-BY-SA licensed — an unusual and meaningful intellectual property right for fan fiction authors.
- **No monetary payment**: The wiki pays contributors nothing. No bounty system, no split of advertising revenue. All motivation is reputational and communal.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse: Creator Income, Fan Consumption
Slenderverse creators earned income through YouTube advertising revenue, Patreon subscriptions (in later years), and merchandise. Fans received: entertainment, community participation in forum theory, and the social status of being a well-known theorizer within the community. No formal reward structure existed for fans.

### 17776: Fan Appreciation, Nothing Formal
Fans of 17776 create fanfiction on AO3 and discussion posts on Tumblr. The motivation structure is entirely intrinsic: aesthetic engagement, community participation, and the social pleasure of producing interpretive work about a beloved text. Jon Bois does not reward fan works with official recognition.

### The Magnus Archives: Fan Status, Creator Acknowledgment, Occasional Spotlight
Magnus Archives fans receive:
- **Community recognition** for high-kudos AO3 works.
- **Occasional official spotlight**: the Fanfiction Showcase directly featured fan works read by the creators on stream — a meaningful social-capital event for selected authors.
- **No monetary reward**: CC-BY-NC-SA explicitly prohibits commercial exploitation of fan works.
- **Patreon-adjacent access**: Rusty Quill's Patreon provides early access, bonus content, and creator Q&A participation. This is consumption reward, not creation reward.

### The Silt Verses: Intimacy with Creators, Community Recognition
The Silt Verses community is small enough that creator acknowledgment (a Tumblr reblog of fan art, a shoutout during the Q&A) functions as a significant social reward. The drag revue promise during the 2024 final Q&A — where voice actors committed to judging a fan-organized drag performance — is an example of social reward being formalized playfully.

### Steam Powered Giraffe: Embodied Recognition, Canon Absorption
SPG's reward structure is unique:
- **Canonical absorption** of fan characters (the Walter Tech model) is a high-status outcome that fans actively pursue.
- **Engineer-eteer access** provides material benefits (exclusive content, art streams) as a reward for financial support.
- **Physical recognition** at shows — being acknowledged on stage, having a costume incorporated into performance — is a social reward unavailable in text-only ecosystems.

## 5. Failure Modes

### SCP Foundation: Trademark Capture, Voting Bias, Bureaucratic Drift
**Trademark capture (2018–2022)**: Russian national Andrey Duksin registered the SCP Foundation name and logo as a trademark in the Russian Federation and Eurasian Customs Union, paying approximately 100,000 rubles (~$1,400 USD). He admitted to doing so to pursue commercial licensing opportunities. By summer 2018, he was using the trademark to suppress competitor merchandise sellers and petitioned VK (Russia's dominant social network) to transfer ownership of the official SCP Foundation page to him — which temporarily succeeded. The SCP community launched a legal fundraiser in 2020, raising $140,000 against a $50,000 target. The Russian Federal Antimonopoly Service ruled against Duksin in November 2021; his trademark was cancelled in October 2022. The incident demonstrated that CC-BY-SA licensing without trademark coordination creates a vulnerability where bad actors can register the brand name and leverage it against the community.

**Voting bias and canonical stratification**: Long-term community members have documented systematic upvote bias toward established authors and downvote bias toward newcomers. The highest-rated articles are disproportionately from the pre-2015 era, when the community was smaller and social networks more dense. This has contributed to a perceived two-class system — "legacy SCP" authors with permanent high-status articles and new contributors who face a legitimacy ceiling.

**Creepypasta drift**: As the SCP Foundation's influence spread, the stylistic DNA mutated in downstream communities toward generic horror meme formats (Slender Man, Jeff the Killer, Smile Dog), which were explicitly categorized as lower-prestige "creepypasta" rather than proper SCP content. The Foundation wiki community invested significant energy in defending its brand against this aesthetic drift, which created ongoing tension between accessibility and quality-maintenance.

**Canon fragmentation**: Multiple competing "canons" exist within the SCP Foundation. The Foundation explicitly does not have a single overarching timeline; individual articles may contradict each other. Multiple "canon hubs" — the "Broken Masquerade" canon, the "Third Law" canon, the "Rat's Nest" canon — offer incompatible interpretations of Foundation lore. This is partly by design (to allow contributor freedom) and partly a failure of coordination at scale.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse: Internal Conflict, Financial Strain, Audience Decay
The THAC team's dissolution is a case study in creator-team conflict at the intersection of financial pressure and governance ambiguity. Key failure sequence:

1. Marble Hornets concluded in 2014. Without the flagship series, THAC pivoted to comedy content, bringing in a fourth member, Noah Scammon.
2. Financial tension increased: Patreon income, merchandise, and YouTube advertising revenue were now divided four ways while viewership declined from Marble Hornets peaks.
3. Joseph DeLage and Tim Sutton demanded Scammon be removed. Troy Wagner, as the nominal sole legal owner of THAC, overruled them by invoking sole ownership — directly violating an implicit three-way consensus governance norm the trio had operated under.
4. DeLage and Sutton departed in October 2015.
5. Clear Lakes 44, the planned Marble Hornets successor series that had been teased as a Kickstarter stretch goal, was cancelled in April 2016.

The broader Slenderverse fragmented because it had no architecture for shared maintenance. Each series depended entirely on its creator's individual motivation and health. When series went dormant — as most did by 2016–2018 — there was no institution to hold continuity. The mythos persisted in cultural memory but the active participatory ecosystem collapsed.

### 17776: No Ecosystem Failure (Intentional Closure)
The 17776 ecosystem cannot meaningfully fail because it has no participatory architecture to break. The fandom is entirely optional and additive. If Jon Bois never produces another installment, the existing works remain complete. The only risk is author-side: Bois's output schedule at Secret Base is irregular, and 20021 (the second part of the 20020 continuation) has been postponed indefinitely, creating fan frustration — but this is an authorship management issue, not an ecosystem design failure.

### The Magnus Archives: Parasocial Dependency, Platform Risk
Rusty Quill's risk profile centers on:
- **Creator dependency**: The Magnus Archives' entire canon authority resided in Jonathan Sims. If Sims had become unavailable, canon production halts. The Magnus Protocol (2024–) is co-created but still Rusty Quill-controlled, which mitigates — but doesn't eliminate — this risk.
- **Platform-risk for fan community**: AO3 faces periodic DDOS attacks and ongoing legal scrutiny. Tumblr has experienced multiple audience-exodus events (the 2018 adult-content ban drove significant user loss). Fan community concentration on specific platforms creates fragility.
- **CC-BY-NC-SA commercial restriction**: While protective of Rusty Quill's commercial interests, the noncommercial clause has created friction for fans who want to create physical merchandise, print zines for cost-recovery, or otherwise participate in the fan economy at small scale.

### The Silt Verses: Scale Ceiling
The Silt Verses' primary failure mode is not dysfunction but limitation: the ecosystem's intimacy is a product of its small scale, and that small scale is not scalable. If the show had achieved Magnus Archives-level listenership, the current direct creator-fan relationship architecture would have broken under volume. The show ended intentionally with the story complete, so this ceiling was never stress-tested.

### Steam Powered Giraffe: Personnel Instability, Canon Inconsistency
SPG experienced significant roster changes over the years — key figures departed, including Bunny Bennett (who had been instrumental in fan community development) and multiple Walter Workers — each causing community disruption. Because the mythology is partly embodied in the performers themselves (robots are performed by specific people), cast changes destabilize the fictional universe in a way that text-based collaborative fiction avoids. The Walter Workers role changed names (from "Walter Girls" to "Walter Workers") following community sensitivity feedback, creating a retroactive canon revision that some longtime fans contested.

### Mountain Goats: Parasocial Violation Incident
John Darnielle's 2018 Tumblr controversy — in which multiple young fans published accounts of emotional over-reliance and boundary violations in Darnielle's private correspondence with fans — illustrates the **parasocial boundary failure mode** specific to ecosystems where the creator is also a community participant. Darnielle's direct Tumblr engagement, which was a feature of the community, became a liability when the power differential between creator-celebrity and fan was violated. The incident resolved without permanent community rupture, but it established a cautionary precedent: high creator presence in fan community spaces creates responsibility as well as charm.

## 6. Original-vs-Derivative Tension

### SCP Foundation: "Mainlist vs. Tale vs. Canon Hub" Stratification
The SCP Foundation manages derivative content through formal categorical stratification:
- **SCP Articles** (mainlist entries, numbered) are the canonical core: anomaly documentation files. These receive the most community scrutiny and must pass the voting/critique filter to persist.
- **Tales** are prose fiction set in the SCP universe. These have more narrative freedom and lower voting scrutiny thresholds.
- **Canon Hubs** are explicitly opt-in extended narrative frameworks. Authors choose whether their articles participate in a given canon hub or not. No canon hub is more "official" than any other; they coexist as parallel expanded universes.
- **Joke SCPs** (SCP-J series) are explicitly non-serious and quarantined from mainlist canon.

This stratification solves the originality problem by making canonical gradient explicit: mainlist SCPs are prestige canon; tales and canon hubs are explicitly derivative and narratively subordinate.

### Slenderverse: Voluntary Shared Mythos
The Slenderverse managed derivative tension through voluntary mythos adoption. Each creator independently chose to use the Slender Man entity, but could interpret him freely. Marble Hornets established one aesthetic (cold, ambiguous, architectural horror). EverymanHYBRID incorporated him but eventually marginalized him in favor of the HABIT entity. TribeTwelve positioned Slender Man as one member of a larger supernatural Collective hierarchy. These divergent interpretations coexisted without conflict because there was no canon authority to enforce consistency.

The implicit rule was: **each series is its own canon; crossovers are bilateral treaties, not mergers.** The 2013 EMH/TribeTwelve crossover (The Envelope / Noah videos) was possible precisely because it was a limited event that did not require reconciling the two canons' cosmologies.

### 17776: Author Canon is Total; Fan Work is Explicitly Parallel
Jon Bois has never indicated that fan fiction about 17776 is canonical or that fans can contribute to the work's official storyline. Fan works on AO3 exist entirely outside canonical consideration. There is no tension to manage because the boundary is clear and unchallenged. The fandom produces works that are obviously tagged and shelved as "fanfic" — the Archive's own tagging system handles this.

### The Magnus Archives: Non-Commercial Fan Works Are Welcomed; Canon Remains Author-Owned
Rusty Quill's approach is the most explicitly managed in this set. Their [Fan Works Notice](https://rustyquill.com/news/2020-02-06-fan-works-notice/) and Creative Commons licensing together create a formal treaty between the company and the fan community:
- **Non-commercial fan works**: freely permitted, explicitly licensed.
- **Commercial fan works**: prohibited by the NC clause.
- **Fan works that might be mistaken for official Rusty Quill products**: prohibited (misleading attribution).
- **Fan canon vs. official canon**: never confused. The Fanfiction Showcase event explicitly framed fan works as "alternate universes" and "what-ifs" — creative in their own right but not official.

Notably, Rusty Quill's Rusty Fears horror writing competition (Season 7, announced November 2025) explicitly excludes Magnus universe content from submissions, clarifying that their commercial competitions and fan participation live in separate buckets.

### Steam Powered Giraffe: Fan Characters Can Become Canon
SPG's practice of absorbing fan characters into semi-official canon is the most permeable boundary in this set. The Walter Tech Carissa case — a fan costume formally acknowledged in lore — demonstrates that the boundary is deliberately permeable at the band's discretion. This creates an incentive structure where high-quality fan worldbuilding is rewarded with canonical recognition, but also creates ambiguity: which fan characters are "real" and which are informal headcanon?

### Mountain Goats: Interpretive Fan Theory is the Entire Point
Darnielle's songwriting deliberately invites fan interpretation of recurring mythological elements (the Beast, the Master, the Alpha Couple, the recurring protagonists of concept-album sequences). He maintains interpretive openness in interviews: confirming emotional truth while declining to fix narrative facts. This posture transforms fan theory from derivative to constitutive — the fan interpretation of Mountain Goats mythology *is* part of the mythological experience the work is designed to produce. There is no "incorrect" fan reading of In League With Dragons, and Darnielle's non-confirmation creates permanent canonical openness.

## 7. Token / Monetization Layer

### SCP Foundation: CC-BY-SA as the Monetization Framework
The SCP Foundation has never overlaid a token or Web3 layer. Its monetization architecture is unusual: CC-BY-SA means anyone — including commercial entities — can use SCP content for commercial purposes as long as they provide attribution and license derivatives under the same terms. This has generated a secondary economy of:
- Licensed video games (SCP: Containment Breach, SCP: Secret Laboratory)
- An unauthorized but CC-permitted film and novel adaptation market
- Redbubble and print-on-demand merchandise from individual artists

The Foundation itself receives no revenue from these commercial uses. The community runs on volunteer time; hosting costs are covered by donation. The Russian trademark crisis of 2018–2022 arose precisely because the CC framework has no trademark protection component — anyone could register the brand name as a trademark and attempt to extract commercial value from the SCP name.

**Lesson**: CC-BY-SA is powerful for enabling an ecosystem economy but creates a trademark vulnerability. The community's $140,000 legal fundraise to defend against Duksin demonstrates that the community *will* act collectively against commercial capture, but the defensive action required significant coordination and money.

### Jenkins the Valet / BAYC Writer's Room: Web3 Narrative NFT Failure
[Jenkins the Valet](https://www.jenkinsthevalet.com/), a narrative NFT project spun out of the Bored Ape Yacht Club ecosystem, is the closest direct comparison for a Web3 collaborative fiction attempt. Structure:
- Jenkins was an NFT character with an evolving narrative.
- The Writer's Room was a collection of 6,942 NFTs sold as "virtual compasses" granting holders influence over narrative direction.
- Author Neil Strauss was engaged to write the canonical novel in collaboration with the community.
- Community influence operated via voting on narrative branches by Writer's Room holders.

What failed: When the associated Azurians PFP (profile picture) NFT collection launched in September 2022, the art was widely criticized as poor quality. The team acknowledged the failure was partly a consequence of mismanaged expectations — they had "run too hot" in describing upcoming features, and their excitement had generated promises they could not deliver. Community trust was materially damaged. The team pivoted to a software platform called Avenue to rebuild credibility.

**Key failure pattern**: NFT-gated narrative participation creates a **pay-to-participate access requirement** that price-excludes community members without capital. When the NFT market declined after the 2022 crash, the community shrank to holders who had bought at high prices and now held underwater assets, creating a toxic incentive structure where holders wanted narrative decisions that would increase NFT floor price rather than serve the story.

### No Other Ecosystem Studied Has a Token Layer
Marble Hornets, 17776, Magnus Archives, Silt Verses, SPG, and Mountain Goats have none. Rusty Quill's Patreon is a subscription consumption reward, not a participation-governance token. The absence of token experiments across these otherwise creatively diverse ecosystems is itself a signal: none of these communities, which have deeply committed fans with demonstrated willingness to pay (Rusty Quill Kickstarter, SPG Engineer-eteers, Patreons), chose to route participation through a token mechanism.

## 8. Gate-vs-Onboarding Posture

### SCP Foundation: Consumption-First, Participation-Optional with Soft Gate
Reading the SCP Foundation requires zero friction: no account, no email, no wallet. The entire archive is publicly accessible. **Consuming is day-one for anyone.** Participation — upvoting, downvoting, posting — requires a free account. Authoring an SCP requires community participation in the Drafts forum before posting. This is a soft gate: not a paywall, not a token requirement, but a community-time investment requirement that filters casual contributors.

The site rules explicitly prohibit posting AI-generated content, which is a 2025-era gate that the community added to protect quality. This is a **conduct gate, not an access gate**.

### Marble Hornets / Slenderverse: Consumption-Only
There was no gate and no formal participation mechanism. You watched, and if you wanted to participate in community discussion you found a Reddit thread or forum. There was nothing to contribute to except commentary. Zero gate, zero contribution architecture.

### 17776: Consumption-Only
Same as Marble Hornets. Read the work, form your own opinions, write fan fiction somewhere. No gate, no participation apparatus.

### The Magnus Archives: Consumption-First, Patreon as Consumption-Upgrade
Listen for free via any podcast app — zero friction, zero gate, day-one access. Patreon provides an ad-free feed and early access as an upgrade tier. This is a **freemium consumption model**, not a participation gate. Fan creation is entirely optional and takes place on third-party platforms (AO3, Tumblr, Discord servers) under the CC license.

### The Silt Verses: Consumption-First, Creator-Adjacent for Backers
Free to listen on podcast platforms. Patreon provides RPG content and creator Q&A access — these are both consumption rewards and limited participation rewards. The RPG content, in particular, gives Patreon backers a structured way to engage with the fictional world's systems. This is the closest any audio-drama ecosystem comes to **participation-as-upgrade** rather than participation-as-requirement.

### Steam Powered Giraffe: Tiered Participation with Physical Gate
SPG has the most explicit participation ladder:
1. Free: Watch YouTube videos, read the webcomic online.
2. Paid (Engineer-eteer): Behind-the-scenes content, Cavalcadium forum access, art streams.
3. Physical attendance: Engage with Walter Workers, potentially have fan character acknowledged.
4. Community standing: Have fan character elevated to semi-canonical status.

The physical gate (attending shows in San Diego or convention touring locations) is a genuine barrier to the highest tiers of participation. This is a **geography-gated** participation model.

### Mountain Goats: Consumption-First, Parasocial-Optional
Albums are available on Spotify and Bandcamp, concerts are ticketed in the normal way. Fan community on Tumblr is entirely optional. Darnielle's presence on Tumblr creates a sense of accessible participation, but functionally, fan engagement is consumption + social-platform activity. No formal gate of any kind.

## 9. What LoreVault Should Learn — and Avoid

### Transferable Patterns (5–7)

**Pattern 1: Canonical gradient by formal category, not informal consensus**
The SCP Foundation's explicit distinction between mainlist SCP articles, Tales, and Canon Hubs gives contributors a legible map of what "counts" and how much. LoreVault should establish explicit lore tiers — Primary Canon, Expanded Canon, Fan Lore, Community Headcanon — and make the criteria for tier membership legible from day one. Fans will self-sort if the categories are clear. Without this, tier assignment happens by informal social negotiation, which creates resentment and status anxiety.

**Pattern 2: Consumption must be unconditionally day-one**
Every ecosystem studied that generates deep, long-running fan engagement offers zero-friction passive consumption from the first moment. SCP, 17776, Magnus Archives, Silt Verses — all free to read or listen. SPG's YouTube library and webcomic are free. The Cavalcadium participation tier is an upgrade, not a precondition. LoreVault's binding constraint — *passive collectors must work day-one, participation is upside never a tax* — is validated across every case. Any mechanism that makes a passive holder feel locked out of the story by their non-participation will generate resentment and churn.

**Pattern 3: Fan creation needs a formal, stable legal address**
Rusty Quill's Fan Works Notice is a small but structurally important document. It tells fans: here is exactly what you can make, here is what you cannot make, here is how to reach us if you're not sure. The SCP Foundation's CC-BY-SA provides an even more permissive but structurally similar clarity. LoreVault needs an explicit fan-creation policy published at launch — not vague "we love fan art!" social posts, but a durable policy document that addresses commercial fan works, cosplay, convention presence, and derivative media. Ambiguity about fan-creation rights consistently creates conflict when a fan effort becomes commercially visible.

**Pattern 4: Pre-publication critique scaffolding creates higher-quality contributions**
The SCP Foundation's draft critique pipeline — Ideas forum, then Draft forum, then publish — dramatically improves average contribution quality compared to pure open posting. The critique step functions as a community investment filter: it screens out contributors who are unwilling to receive feedback, and it improves the work of those who remain. LoreVault should build an equivalent "workshop" layer for fan lore submissions — a place where prospective contributions receive community critique before they are eligible for any kind of official recognition. This does not mean the workshop is required to *consume* content; it is required only to *contribute* to elevated tiers.

**Pattern 5: Creator-direct community presence is high-value but high-risk**
Darnielle's Tumblr presence is beloved and constitutive of the Mountain Goats fandom's character. Rusty Quill creators' Discord and Patreon Q&A participation generates intense loyalty. But both cases also illustrate the risk: creator-direct community presence creates parasocial expectations that the creator cannot always fulfill. Darnielle's 2018 incident; Rusty Quill's commercial restraint being occasionally misread as creator withdrawal — these are costs of the high-engagement model. LoreVault's creators should design specific structured touchpoints (Q&As, annotated reveals, lore commentary posts) rather than ambient social-media presence, to control the parasocial surface area.

**Pattern 6: Audio and image-first worlds build participation without editable documents**
The Magnus Archives, Silt Verses, and SPG demonstrate that rich fan participation ecosystems — extensive fan fiction, fan theory, fan art, fan cosplay, fan RPG campaigns — can emerge from **non-editable artifacts**: podcasts, recorded music, webcomics. The participation happens *around* the artifact, not *within* it. AO3, Tumblr, Discord, and Reddit serve as the participation layer for audio-first worlds; the wiki is built by fans after the fact, not before. For LoreVault, this means: your NFTs and lore documents are the immutable artifacts; the fan activity (interpretive work, derivative creation, community theory) is the participation ecosystem. The blockchain is the archive, not the wiki.

**Pattern 7: Social rewards outperform monetary rewards for community health**
Across every ecosystem studied, the highest-engagement communities — SCP, Magnus Archives, Silt Verses, SPG — offer primarily social and reputational rewards: attribution, creator acknowledgment, canonical recognition of fan characters, official spotlighting of fan works. The communities that have attempted monetary rewards (Jenkins the Valet Writer's Room) generated participation that was financialized and therefore subject to market incentives that compete with narrative quality. For LoreVault, the social-reward layer (official acknowledgment, canonical recognition, creator Q&A access, community spotlight) should be the primary participation incentive. Token rewards, if present, should be small and structured to avoid turning story participation into financial speculation.

---

### What LoreVault Should Explicitly NOT Do (Failure Modes to Pre-empt)

**Anti-Pattern 1: Token-gating any consumption or story access**
The Jenkins the Valet Writer's Room model — where NFT ownership was required to participate in narrative direction — created a pay-to-play creative participation layer that became toxic when the NFT market crashed. Writer's Room holders became underwater investors with misaligned story incentives. More fundamentally, it excluded everyone without capital from creative participation, collapsing the audience-to-community pipeline that is the engine of all the successful ecosystems studied. LoreVault must not replicate this. Tokens may offer *upgrades* (exclusive content, early lore drops, creator Q&A slots) but must never be the ticket to understanding or enjoying the story.

**Anti-Pattern 2: Unprotected trademark under an open content license**
The SCP Foundation's Russian trademark crisis is directly instructive. CC-BY-SA licensing for story content does not protect the brand name or logo. A bad actor spent $1,400 to register the brand and caused years of legal pain requiring $140,000 in community fundraising to resolve. LoreVault should secure trademark registration in key jurisdictions *before* going to scale under any open-content licensing framework. The trademark and the content license serve different functions; do not assume one covers the other.

**Anti-Pattern 3: Implicit consensus governance without explicit ownership documentation**
The THAC/Marble Hornets dissolution turned on a specific governance failure: Troy Wagner held sole legal ownership of THAC, but the three-person team operated on an implicit majority-vote norm. When Wagner invoked sole ownership to override DeLage and Sutton on a personnel decision, the governance ambiguity destroyed a creative team and a successor series. For LoreVault, any multi-creator, multi-contributor canonical system needs explicit documented governance: who holds veto power over canon, what the escalation path is when contributors disagree, how contributions are formally acknowledged. "We'll figure it out" is not governance.

**Anti-Pattern 4: Hype outrunning delivery (the credibility debt trap)**
Jenkins the Valet's team acknowledged explicitly that they had "run too hot" describing their project — generating expectations they could not meet, which damaged community trust severely enough that the project has never fully recovered. In collaborative mythology, where the community is your co-investor in the world's reality, credibility is structural. Understating and over-delivering is the safe posture. LoreVault should not announce lore features, canonical expansions, or fan participation programs until the scaffolding to support them is in place. Promising a Q&A, a lore drop, or a fan showcase and then failing to deliver is not just a PR problem — it damages the community's trust in the canonical authority of the world itself.

**Anti-Pattern 5: Single-creator canonical dependency without succession architecture**
Marble Hornets collapsed because THAC collapsed. Magnus Archives' entire canonical direction depended on Jonathan Sims as sole canon authority (mitigated but not eliminated in The Magnus Protocol). The Silt Verses' canon authority resided in Jon Ware alone. In each case, creator unavailability — whether from personal crisis, creative disagreement, or production closure — could (and in Marble Hornets' case, did) end the world entirely. LoreVault should design lore with modular canonical authority from the beginning: primary canon controlled by a defined body, expanded canon managed by a community editorial tier, fan canon clearly demarcated. If a single creator departs, the world should be able to continue under defined succession rules, rather than being held hostage to any individual's continued participation.
