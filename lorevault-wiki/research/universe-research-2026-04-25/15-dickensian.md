## 2. PD status

**Verdict: deeply, unambiguously public domain globally.**

- Dickens died **9 June 1870**. Under every major copyright regime (life+50, life+70, life+95-from-publication), every original Dickens text has been public domain for over a century. ([Charles Dickens Info](https://www.charlesdickensinfo.com/life/copyright-laws/))
- Pickwick (1836–7), Oliver Twist (1838), Christmas Carol (1843), David Copperfield (1850), Bleak House (1853), Hard Times (1854), Little Dorrit (1857), Tale of Two Cities (1859), Great Expectations (1861), Mystery of Edwin Drood (1870, unfinished) — all PD, every text variant including Dickens's own revisions.
- Stage performance, adaptation, derivative work, character usage: all unrestricted on the source text. ([LegalClarity — A Christmas Carol PD analysis](https://legalclarity.org/is-a-christmas-carol-in-the-public-domain/))

This is one of the cleanest PD profiles among LoreVault candidates — equal to Greek Myth or Arthurian for source-text freedom.

## 4. Pre-modern canonical PD register — Phiz + Cruikshank + Leech + Fildes

**This is the differentiator.** Dickens novels arrived in serial parts with original etched illustrations by named artists. These illustrators ARE the canonical visual Dickens — and they are deep PD.

- **Hablot Knight Browne ("Phiz", 1815–1882)** — the Dickens illustrator. Did **Pickwick Papers, Nicholas Nickleby, Martin Chuzzlewit, Dombey and Son, David Copperfield, Bleak House, Little Dorrit, A Tale of Two Cities** — ten of the major novels. Steel-etched cross-hatched style; "depicter of physiognomies" chosen to harmonize with Dickens's pen-name "Boz." ([Hablot Knight Browne — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hablot_Knight_Browne) / [Falvey Library — The Printed Image](https://blog.library.villanova.edu/2024/02/21/the-printed-image-phiz-and-the-illustrated-works-of-charles-dickens/))
- **George Cruikshank (1792–1878)** — illustrated **Oliver Twist**. The "Oliver asking for more," the Fagin-in-the-condemned-cell, the Artful Dodger — the iconic Oliver visuals are Cruikshank, not Lean and not Bart.
- **John Leech (1817–1864)** — illustrated **A Christmas Carol** original. The Marley's-Ghost, the Ghost of Christmas Present in green robe — Leech.
- **Luke Fildes (1843–1927)** — illustrated **The Mystery of Edwin Drood**, including the famous "The Empty Chair" (Dickens's vacated study after his death).
- **Marcus Stone, Sol Eytinge, Frederick Barnard** — secondary register.

### Recommendation: lean into Phiz + Cruikshank + Leech specifically

Every LoreVault Dickensian Moment renders in the **steel-etched, cross-hatched, lampblack-ink-on-cream-paper Victorian-illustration register**. This is:

- (a) maximally PD-safe — no actor-likeness, no Disney design, no BBC framing
- (b) historically authentic — these ARE the canonical visualizations Dickens approved
- (c) aesthetically distinctive — no other LoreVault Universe lives in this register
- (d) directly tonally aligned with **the Lattice and the Lampblack** — these illustrations are literally drawn in lampblack ink

This gives Dickensian Universe its native visual fingerprint. No competing Universe in Series 1 occupies cross-hatched Victorian etching territory.

## 5. Top 5 figures with Spine + Lampblack

### 1. Ebenezer Scrooge

- **Spine:** The bah-humbug miser is visited by his dead partner's ghost and three Christmases (Past, Present, Yet-to-Come), shown his own grave, and wakes redeemed.
- **Lampblack:** **The door-knocker that becomes Marley's face.** Single most haunting Lampblack-candidate object in the canon — a literal household fixture transmuted into the supernatural threshold. Also: Marley's chains; the candle-extinguisher cap; the empty chair beside Tiny Tim's stool.

### 2. Oliver Twist

- **Spine:** Workhouse orphan asks for more, is sold, is befriended by Fagin's gang, and (through the stolen-locket and Monks plot) is revealed to be heir to a respectable family.
- **Lampblack:** **The bowl** — empty, extended, the impossible request. Cruikshank's etching of this scene is the iconic image. The workhouse-as-hollow-shell biome.

### 3. Pip

- **Spine:** Blacksmith's apprentice raised to false-gentleman by mysterious benefactor he believes is Miss Havisham; learns the benefactor is the convict Magwitch from the marshes; great-expectations-undone.
- **Lampblack:** **Magwitch rising from the marsh-mist in chains.** The apprenticeship-bond. The forge cooling. The marsh gibbet. Pip's small-hands episode at Satis House.

### 4. Sydney Carton

- **Spine:** The dissipated lawyer-double of Charles Darnay; in love with Lucie; exchanges clothes and identity with Darnay in the Bastille to die in his place at the guillotine. "It is a far, far better thing that I do, than I have ever done."
- **Lampblack:** **The exchanged clothes; the guillotine; the tumbril; Madame Defarge's knitting**. One of the canon's purest sacrifice-arcs. The "I see a beautiful city" closing vision.

### 5. Miss Havisham

- **Spine:** Jilted at the altar at twenty to nine; lived the rest of her life in the wedding-gown, the cake left to rot on the table, every clock in Satis House stopped at the moment of betrayal; raised Estella as a weapon against men.
- **Lampblack:** **The wedding-cake under cobweb. The single yellow slipper. The stopped clocks. The dress. Eventually her own immolation at the hearth.** Maximum Gothic-PD figure; cross-tethers cleanly into Gothic Horror Universe as an EK Stepmother-shadow analog.

**Lampblack ranking:** door-knocker > the bowl > the wedding-cake > the chains > the guillotine. All five are object-level Lampblack candidates of the highest grade.

## 9. Recommendation

### STRONG candidate. Recommend OWN Universe — "London Pane" alongside Baker Street's Argument Pane.

**Rationale:**

- (a) **Source unambiguous PD globally.** No litigation surface on the text itself.
- (b) **Phiz + Cruikshank + Leech illustrative register is unique** to Dickensian — no other LoreVault Universe uses Victorian steel-etching as its native visual register. Maximum aesthetic differentiation.
- (c) **Mass legibility off the chart** — Christmas Carol annual cycle gives a zero-cost cultural pre-load every December.
- (d) **Cross-tether richness** — natively cross-tethers Wonderland (triads), Gothic Horror (Havisham/Quilp/Krook), Baker Street (shared Victorian London), Greek Myth (Fates/three-ghosts), Arthurian (sacrifice-arc).
- (e) **Hollow-Institution biome is novel** — workhouse, debtors' prison, Yorkshire-boarding-school is a new biome class.

**Alternative considered: fold Dickensian into Baker Street as "Victorian London" mega-Universe.**
- REJECTED. Holmes's London is the Argument-Pane (deduction, abductive method, Watson-narrator). Dickens's London is the Lampblack-Pane (institutional cruelty, ghost-of-redemption, sentimental moral arc). The two share a city but not a register. Folding them collapses the differentiator.

**Recommended sequencing:**
- **Series 1:** five locked Universes proceed
- **Series 2 candidate slot:** Dickensian as "London Pane" — pairs with Baker Street as twin Victorian-London Universes with distinct registers
- **Series 3 cross-Universe Moments:** Holmes-investigates-Magwitch; Scrooge's Three Ghosts as Wonderland Three-Queens echo; Miss Havisham as EK Stepmother-shadow

**Drop format:** ship Dickensian Moments in a "monthly serial part" wrapper that mimics the original 1836–1870 publishing format. Distinctive format, free from any rival Universe.

## 10. Cultural sensitivity — ODIN ESCALATION

### Fagin antisemitism — foundational concern. Cannot ship a Fagin Moment without explicit address.

**The facts:**

- In the first 38 chapters of the original 1838 Oliver Twist, Fagin is referred to as "the Jew" **over 250 times**, and called by his name far less. ([Fagin — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fagin))
- In **1863, Eliza Davis** (a Jewish reader) wrote to Dickens accusing him of having "encouraged a vile prejudice against the despised Hebrew." Dickens took the criticism seriously. **He halted printing of Oliver Twist and revised the un-set portions of the text** — which is why Fagin is called "the Jew" 257 times in chapters 1–38, but barely at all in the 179 references after. He also wrote the philosemitic Mr. Riah in Our Mutual Friend (1864–5) explicitly as compensatory. ([The Jewish Chronicle](https://www.thejc.com/life/fagins-back-the-villain-charles-dickens-tried-to-cancel-yinua24k))
- The **1948 David Lean / Alec Guinness Fagin** (large prosthetic nose, three years post-Holocaust) was deemed by the New York Board of Rabbis "a vehicle of blatant antisemitism" and US release was postponed and edited. ([Film Cred — Antisemitism and Villainy](https://film-cred.com/the-case-of-dickens-oliver-twist-antisemitism-and-villainy-in-visual-media/))
- **Will Eisner's Fagin the Jew (2003)** and **Allison Epstein's Fagin the Thief (2025)** are the modern serious counter-readings, retelling from Fagin's interiority. ([Forward](https://forward.com/culture/books/701713/fagin-the-thief-oliver-twist-charles-dickens-antisemitism/))

**LoreVault stance — non-negotiable rules:**

1. **No Cruikshank-Fagin caricature visuals.** The original Cruikshank Fagin etchings, while PD, render the antisemitic caricature features (large hooked nose, stooped posture, red hair). LoreVault must commission **original art** for any Fagin presence, drawing from the Phiz-register etched style WITHOUT the specific caricature features.
2. **Never use "the Jew" descriptor.** Fagin's character text on LoreVault cards uses his name only.
3. **If Fagin ships at all, ship him in the Will Eisner / Allison Epstein register** — Fagin as a Jew driven into receiving-stolen-goods by the antisemitic structures of pre-emancipation London, not as the cackling caricature. This requires **named Jewish creative consultation** on the writing of any Fagin-bearing Moment.
4. **Default recommendation: do not ship Fagin in Series-1-style first-wave Dickensian drop.** Ship Oliver, the Dodger, Bumble, Sikes, Nancy first. Fagin requires a separate dedicated drop with explicit framing — or omit entirely. **Treat Fagin as a deferred / framed-only figure, never as a casual Common-rarity Moment.**
5. **Public framing required.** Any Dickensian Universe launch announcement must explicitly acknowledge the Fagin-caricature problem and LoreVault's editorial stance. This is not optional. The 2025 publication of Fagin the Thief means the conversation is current; LoreVault entering Dickensian without addressing it would be read as either ignorance or endorsement.

### Other sensitivity flags

- **Working-class poverty as picturesque** — Dickens's portrayal of slum life and the workhouse is sympathetic but also aestheticizes poverty. LoreVault should avoid Moments that frame Cratchit-poverty as cozy or Tiny-Tim-disability as inspirational decoration.
- **Tale of Two Cities — French Revolution stereotypes.** Madame Defarge / the bloodthirsty-mob caricature was already a contested portrayal in 1859 and reads worse now. Frame Carton's sacrifice without leaning into mob-caricature visuals.
- **Disability — Tiny Tim and Smike.** Disability-as-pathos is a Dickens default; consult disability-community sensitivity readers on Tiny Tim Moments. Tim should not be reduced to crutch-and-blessing.
- **Quilp (Old Curiosity Shop) — disability villain.** The dwarf-as-monster trope. Avoid or reframe.

### Marshalsea / autobiography note

- Dickens's father John Dickens was imprisoned in the Marshalsea in 1824; 12-year-old Charles was sent to Warren's Blacking Factory. Mr. Micawber (David Copperfield) is John Dickens. Little Dorrit's Marshalsea is direct autobiography. ([Historic UK — Marshalsea](https://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofBritain/Marshalsea-Debtors-Prison-Dickens/) / [Victorian Web — Debtors](https://victorianweb.org/authors/dickens/debtorsprisons.html)) — this is a useful authenticity-anchor for any Marshalsea Moment.
