# LoreVault Candidate Universe Research — Arabian Nights / 1001 Nights

**Date:** 2026-04-25
**Researcher:** Claude (Opus 4.7, 1M context)
**Status:** STRONG candidate, Series 3+ recommended (NOT Series 1 — Disney aesthetic minefield is second only to Snow White; needs careful differentiation runway and cultural-advisory set-up before deployment)
**ODIN ESCALATION FLAG RAISED** — see §3 and §10. Disney's Aladdin trademark + trade-dress portfolio is one of the most aggressively-defended in their library; the underlying tale is PD but every visual shorthand a 2026 audience reaches for is Disney-owned.

## 1. Canon enumeration

### Top 20 figures

1. **Scheherazade (Shahrazad)** — the vizier's daughter who tells a tale every night to defer her execution; the frame-narrator of the entire 1001 Nights cycle. The single most structurally-important character in the corpus.
2. **King Shahryar (the Sultan)** — the betrayed king who marries-and-kills until Scheherazade breaks the cycle.
3. **Dunyazad** — Scheherazade's sister, who triggers each night's story by asking for one.
4. **Aladdin (ʿAlāʾ al-Dīn)** — the impoverished youth of the "orphan tale" (Diyab/Galland 1709–1712); set in "China" in the source text.
5. **The Jinn of the Lamp** — bound spirit of monstrous power, slave to whoever rubs the vessel; in Galland's original a fearsome figure, not a comic blue companion.
6. **The Jinn of the Ring** — secondary, lesser jinn bound to Aladdin's signet ring (often forgotten in adaptations).
7. **Princess Badroulbadour ("Badr al-Budūr," Full-Moon-of-Full-Moons)** — Aladdin's princess; her name is canonical PD (Disney's "Jasmine" is invented).
8. **The Maghrebi Sorcerer (Wicked Magician)** — the African/North-African necromancer-villain of the Aladdin tale; uses Aladdin to retrieve the lamp.
9. **The Sorcerer's Brother** — a second antagonist disguised as a holy woman; underused in adaptations.
10. **Ali Baba** — the woodcutter who witnesses the thieves' password.
11. **Cassim (Kassim)** — Ali Baba's greedy brother, dismembered in the cave.
12. **Morgiana (Marjana)** — the slave-girl whose intelligence kills the Forty Thieves; arguably the cleverest non-Scheherazade figure in the entire corpus.
13. **The Forty Thieves and their Captain** — bandit-collective with the cave-treasure.
14. **Sinbad the Sailor (Sindbād al-Baḥrī)** — Baghdadi merchant of the Seven Voyages.
15. **Sinbad the Porter (Sindbād al-Ḥammāl)** — the land-bound listener-double; a frame-pair often dropped in adaptations.
16. **The Old Man of the Sea** — the parasite-rider of Sinbad's fifth voyage.
17. **The Roc (Rukh)** — the world-mountain bird whose egg is bigger than a dome.
18. **Caliph Harun al-Rashid** — historical Abbasid caliph (r. 786–809) mythologized as a night-walker who disguises himself among the people. Iraqi national-cultural heritage.
19. **Vizier Ja'afar al-Barmaki** — Harun's historical vizier, narrative companion. Note: Disney's "Jafar" is the *sorcerer*; the canonical Ja'afar is the *good* vizier-companion of the Caliph. This is a useful differentiation lever.
20. **Masrur** — Harun's executioner-companion on the night-walks.

**Honorable mentions for tier-2 inclusion:** Maruf the Cobbler; Prince Ahmed and the Peri Banu; Abu Hassan the Wag (the "sleeper awakened"); the Three Calenders (mendicant princes); the Hunchback / Tailor / Barber (frame within frame); Hasan al-Basri; Princess Parizade and her two brothers; the Fisherman and the Jinni-in-the-Bottle; Camar al-Zaman and Princess Budur; Nur al-Din and Anis al-Jalis.

### Top 10–15 Moments

1. **Scheherazade tells the first night's tale** — the originating moment of the whole frame.
2. **Aladdin descends into the cave and finds the lamp.**
3. **The Genie is released from the lamp.**
4. **Aladdin builds his palace overnight** (the wish that triggers the sorcerer's return).
5. **The sorcerer steals the palace** ("new lamps for old").
6. **Ali Baba speaks "Open Sesame"** at the cave mouth.
7. **Morgiana pours boiling oil into the forty jars.**
8. **Morgiana dances and stabs the Captain** at the feast.
9. **Sinbad straps himself to the Roc's leg** to escape the diamond valley (Voyage 2).
10. **The Old Man of the Sea climbs onto Sinbad's shoulders** (Voyage 5).
11. **The Fisherman releases the jinni from the brass jar** sealed with Solomon's seal.
12. **The Three Apples** — Harun al-Rashid investigates a murder.
13. **The City of Brass** — desert expedition to a dead city sealed by Suleiman.
14. **The Sleeper Awakened** — Abu Hassan placed on the throne for a day.
15. **Prince Ahmed buys the magic carpet, the magic apple, the magic tube.**

### Props

Lamp; ring; **flying carpet** (originally Prince Ahmed's, NOT Aladdin's — Disney conflated); scimitar; the cave sealed by "Open Sesame"; **brass jar** sealed with the Seal of Solomon; **bottle**; **rukh's egg**; **Solomon's hexagram seal** (Khātam Sulaymān); turban; coin-purse; hookah/narghile; oud; pomegranate; rose-water flask; senet/shatranj board; mosaic-tiled fountain.

### Places

Baghdad (Madinat al-Salam); Basra (Sinbad's home port); Cairo; Damascus; Samarkand; Persia (Isfahan, Shiraz); the Maghreb (sorcerer's origin); the Indian Ocean voyage-path; the imagined "China" of the Aladdin frame; the Cave of the Forty Thieves; the Diamond Valley; the Island of the Roc; the **City of Brass** in the desert; the harbor; the courtyard with fountain; the souk; the hammam; the caravanserai; the desert.

## 2. PD status

**CONFIRMED PD:**

- **Antoine Galland, *Les Mille et une nuits*, 12 vols., 1704–1717** — the European foundation. Deep PD globally. ([Wikipedia: Les Mille et Une Nuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_mille_et_une_nuits))
- **Edward William Lane translation, 1840** — PD globally.
- **Sir Richard Burton, *The Book of the Thousand Nights and a Night*, 1885** (16 vols.) — PD globally.
- **J.C. Mardrus French translation, 1898–1904** — PD (author d. 1949; PD in life+70 jurisdictions as of 2020; PD in US by pre-1928 publication).
- **Andrew Lang, *Blue Fairy Book* (1889) and successor "color" Fairy Books** — PD; Lang d. 1912. ([Wikipedia: The Langs' Fairy Books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Langs'_Fairy_Books))
- **Underlying Arabic / Persian source manuscripts** (Galland's Syrian manuscript; the Bulaq 1835 and Calcutta II 1839–1842 editions) — millennium-old, deep PD.
- **The Hanna Diyab "orphan tales"** — Aladdin and Ali Baba do NOT appear in any Arabic manuscript predating Galland; they were dictated by the Maronite Syrian storyteller Hanna Diyab to Galland in Paris in 1709. PD via Galland publication 1712 (Aladdin) and 1717 (Ali Baba). Modern scholarship (Bottigheimer, Horta) increasingly credits Diyab as the originating author. ([Aladdin — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aladdin); [Galland and Diyab](https://www.rachellouiselawrence.com/antoine_galland_and_hanna_diyab.html))

**KEY POINT:** The *names* "Aladdin," "Sinbad," "Ali Baba," "Scheherazade," "Jafar," "Genie/Jinn," "Open Sesame," "Magic Carpet," "Cave of Wonders," "the Forty Thieves" are all PD. Disney does NOT own these names. Disney owns specific *visual designs* and specific *character expressions* (see §3). The distinction between PD-name and trademarked-look is the strategic seam.

**UNCLEAR:** Trademark filings exist for "DISNEY ALADDIN" (Serial 88451026, filed 2019) covering entertainment services. The bare word "Aladdin" without "Disney" is unlikely to be trademark-defensible for a card-collectible product, but Disney has demonstrated willingness to send aggressive cease-and-desists at perceived infringers. ([Justia Trademarks: DISNEY ALADDIN](https://trademarks.justia.com/884/51/n-88451026.html))

## 4. Pre-Disney canonical PD register

The strategic answer to the Disney problem is to inherit a *deeper* visual canon — the pre-1992 illustrated tradition. This is where LoreVault wins.

1. **Edmund Dulac, *Stories from the Arabian Nights*, retold by Laurence Housman, Hodder & Stoughton 1907.** 50 watercolor plates. THE canonical register: muted blue-violet-gold palette, Persian-miniature compositional logic married to Art Nouveau line. Sold out at the Leicester Galleries before public opening. PD CONFIRMED — pre-1931 publication; Dulac d. 1953. ([Wikisource: Stories from the Arabian Nights](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Stories_from_the_Arabian_Nights_retold_by_Laurence_Housman_with_drawings_by_Edmund_Dulac_(London_1907)); [Nocloo gallery](https://www.nocloo.com/edmund-dulac-stories-from-arabian-nights-1907/)) **PRIMARY register.**

2. **Léon Carré (with Mohammed Racim ornaments), *Le Livre des mille nuits et une nuit*, Mardrus translation, Editions d'Art H. Piazza, 1926–1932.** 144 color watercolor plates by Carré + 85 ornamental plates by Racim across 12 volumes; new pochoir/stencil color process by Saudé. Vivid Art Deco-meets-Mughal-meets-North-African register. Eight-year production. ([Edition-Originale: Carré 1926](https://edition-originale.com/en/works/literature-1/illustrated-books-18/carre-le-livre-des-mille-et-une-nuits-1926-77870); [BnF: Mille et une nuits](https://expositions.bnf.fr/1001nuits/grand/mil_063.htm)) **PD STATUS: UNCLEAR for full set in some jurisdictions** — Carré died 1942, Racim died 1975. In US, pre-1929 volumes are PD via pre-1929 publication; volumes published 1929–1932 require checking renewal records (under 1909 Copyright Act, foreign works may have entered PD via non-renewal, but URAA 1996 may have restored some). **FLAG FOR LEGAL REVIEW** before using post-1928 plates. The Racim ornamental plates may have separate issues — Racim d. 1975, life+70 PD in 2046 in EU. **SECONDARY register, US-only and pre-1929 plates safest.**

3. **Maxfield Parrish, *The Arabian Nights: Their Best-Known Tales*, Wiggin & Smith eds., Charles Scribner's Sons, October 1909.** 12 color plates. Famous "Parrish blue" luminous skies, surreal-architectural backdrops. PD CONFIRMED — pre-1931 publication; Parrish d. 1966. ([Library of Congress](https://www.loc.gov/item/09028132/); [AnimationResources](https://animationresources.org/illustration-maxfield-parrishs-arabian-nights-1909-2/)) **TERTIARY register** — strong for the dreamlike-mountain-cave / desert-vista plates (Cassim in the Cave; Old Man of the Sea).

4. **Henry Justice Ford illustrations for Andrew Lang's *Blue Fairy Book* (1889) and successor color Fairy Books.** Ford illustrated Aladdin and other Nights tales for Lang. PD CONFIRMED — Ford d. 1941, all pre-1929 work US-PD. ([Wikipedia: Langs' Fairy Books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Langs'_Fairy_Books); [Wikimedia Blue Fairy Book PDF](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:The_Blue_Fairy_Book_-_Lang_-_1889.pdf)) **SUPPORTING register** — engraving-like line work, useful for B/W variants and "hard frames."

5. **Persian and Mughal miniatures (Safavid 16th–17th c.; Mughal 16th–18th c.; Qajar 19th c.).** Deep PD globally. Specific exemplars: Shahnameh of Shah Tahmasp, Hamzanama, Padshahnama, Akbarnama, Nizami's Khamsa, Khamsa of Amir Khusraw, the Behzad school. Visual logic: flat-perspective gold-ground, jewel-tone palette (lapis, malachite, vermilion, saffron, ivory), patterned tile-and-textile backgrounds, hieratic pose, calligraphic margin. **THIS IS THE LOREVAULT-DIFFERENTIATION REGISTER.** Sourced through Met OA, V&A OA, BnF Gallica, British Library Endangered Archives.

6. **Arabic illuminated manuscripts** — Mamluk, Abbasid, Ottoman traditions of Qur'anic and secular illumination; Maqamat al-Hariri (13th-c. al-Wasiti illustrations, BnF MS Arabe 5847). Deep PD.

7. **Eric Pape; René Bull; W. Heath Robinson 1899 Nights; Virginia Frances Sterrett 1928 Tanglewood Tales / 1928 Old French Fairy Tales** — all PD; useful tier-2 references.

**Recommendation — visual canon inheritance for LoreVault Arabian Nights:**

> **Primary: Dulac 1907 (lyrical, painterly).
> Secondary: Persian/Mughal miniature corpus (compositional grammar — flat gold-ground, hieratic, calligraphic margin).
> Tertiary: Léon Carré pre-1929 plates (Art Deco vivacity).
> Supporting: Parrish 1909 (dreamlike skies for outdoor moments).**

This combination is BOTH visually richer than Disney's 1992 register AND legally clean. It signals "this is from a deeper, more authentic well than the cartoon you grew up with."

## 5. Top 5 figures with Spine + Lampblack

**1. Scheherazade — the Storyteller-as-Survivor.**
- **Spine:** Narrative is life-extension; the woman who weaves stories nightly to defer her own death. The Lattice's most natural in-universe narrator-figure. She speaks tales *of other Universes* without knowing it.
- **Lampblack:** Her unfinished sentence at dawn — the cliffhanger as survival mechanism. Visual: lamp-flame guttering at the moment of pause; hand raised mid-gesture; ink un-dried on the page.

**2. The Jinn of the Lamp — Bound Power, Three Wishes, Eternal Restlessness.**
- **Spine:** Vast capacity contained in small vessel; freedom-as-curse (granted, the jinn must serve again); the lamp itself is the seal-of-imprisonment.
- **Lampblack:** The smoke-uncoiling silhouette; the lamp's wick still warm. Visual: copper-skinned multi-armed figure rising from oil-smoke, fire-halo, eyes lapis-blue.

**3. Sinbad the Sailor — Sailor of Seven Voyages, Returns Transformed.**
- **Spine:** Each voyage strips and rebuilds; he keeps going *because* he survived; the Roc, the Old Man, the Diamond Valley, the magnetic island, the elephant graveyard — each Moment is a near-death-into-wealth.
- **Lampblack:** A single quill-feather of the Roc; a salt-stained map; the cargo that came back when the merchant didn't.

**4. Ali Baba — the Witness Made Wealthy.**
- **Spine:** Wealth-by-accident-of-witnessing; the password discovered, not earned; the moral economy of having-seen-something-you-shouldn't-have.
- **Lampblack:** "Open Sesame" inscribed on stone; the cave's sealed mouth; a single sesame-seed.

**5. Morgiana — the Underestimated Woman as Savior.**
- **Spine:** A slave-girl outwits forty thieves twice — boiling-oil-in-the-jars, then dance-and-dagger. The cleverest non-Scheherazade figure in the corpus. The enslaved interior eye that sees what the free men miss.
- **Lampblack:** A sealed jar; the boiling cauldron; the dagger-hidden-in-the-veil; the dancer's coin-belt.

**Cross-Universe Lampblack candidates:**
- Lamp-as-vessel (binding artifact).
- "Open Sesame" as cross-Pane password (portal-key shared with Wonderland's "drink me," Baker Street's "the game's afoot," etc.).
- Roc-feather (giant quill — natural cross-tether to Wonderland's flamingoes, Oz's flying monkeys, Verne's maritime monsters).
- Brass jar sealed with the **Seal of Solomon** (Khātam Sulaymān) — six-pointed star, the binding-glyph for jinn. **Strong candidate for cross-Universe set-glyph or rare-rarity-key.**
- The Lampblack residue itself — soot from lit oil-lamps as the ink that writes the next night's tale.

## 6. LoreVault original — unique expressions

1. **Scheherazade-as-Lattice-Narrator.** Across LoreVault's five (then more) Universes, Scheherazade is the *only* canonical figure whose explicit narrative function is to tell stories from outside her own life. Set her up as the Lattice's in-universe narrator: the cards she "tells" can be from Wonderland, from Baker Street, from any Universe — she dreams them on Night 47, Night 213, Night 909. This solves the Lattice's "who is the storyteller?" problem in-canon.

2. **The Jinn of the Lamp = Lampblack-Incarnate.** The black smoke, the bound power, the wish-granting that always twists, the residue-on-the-lamp-rim. The Jinn is the perfect personification of the Lampblack mechanic across the whole game. A "Jinn-Bound" rarity tier could be a cross-Universe overlay.

3. **"Open Sesame" as Cross-Pane Portal-Key.** Ali Baba's password becomes a card-mechanic: certain cards "open" when paired with this Moment — a trans-Universe unlock.

4. **Sinbad as Proto-Captain-Nemo.** Cross-tether to a future Verne/Wells SF Universe: Sinbad's seven voyages prefigure Nemo's submarine wandering. The Roc prefigures Wells's Martian tripods. Useful Lattice bridge for Series 4+.

5. **Morgiana as Wonderland-Alice's-Shadow.** The shrewd-female-witness who survives by pattern-recognition — direct Lampblack-bridge to Alice. Two Universes share a "underestimated-young-woman-who-sees-the-pattern" archetype; the cards can resonate.

## 7. Aesthetic totalism

**Palette:** Mughal-blue (lapis #1F4788), saffron (#F4C430), bronze-copper (#B87333), ivory (#FFFFF0), ruby-red (#9B111E), lapis (deeper, #26619C), jasmine-cream (#F8DE7E), henna-orange (#D2691E), gold-leaf (#D4AF37), pomegranate-pink (#9F1D35), date-brown (#3B2417), turquoise-tile (#30D5C8 — sparing, as accent), kohl-black (#1A1110). NO Disney-Aladdin-purple-and-turquoise as primaries.

**Biome:** souk (covered bazaar with hanging lanterns); palace courtyard with central fountain; desert with caravan; **City of Pillars** (City of Brass, dead-city); Indian Ocean voyage (dhow ships, monsoon palette); mountain cave; harbor at Basra; rooftop-of-Baghdad night-walk; hammam (bath); caravanserai; mosque-courtyard; library/scriptorium of Bayt al-Hikma.

**Iconography:** oil lamp (the *zayt* lamp, NOT a magic-lamp-shape — slipper-form clay or bronze); the lamp's smoke-curl; scimitar (saif); the qamar moon (crescent); fez/turban (turban primary, fez sparingly — fez is post-Ottoman); flying carpet (with woven tribal-Persian patterning, NO eyes); hookah (narghile); dome-and-minaret silhouette; **Solomon's hexagram (Khātam Sulaymān)** as binding-glyph; scarab-of-Suleiman; peacock; fountain (with mosaic surround); muqarnas (honeycomb-vault); arabesque tile pattern; Arabic naskh / thuluth calligraphy as marginal flourish (NEVER as primary text — see §10 cultural sensitivity); brass jar; Roc-feather; coin-purse; pomegranate.

**Typography:** Latin body face with Arabic-calligraphy-margin running vertically (decorative, not primary text); ornate illumination caps in the Mamluk/Mughal manuscript tradition; gold-leaf accents; thuluth-script flourishes at card titles; rubrication (red ink) for headers, mimicking Persian manuscript convention.

**Set-glyph:** A single oil-lamp silhouette (slipper-clay-form, NOT Disney teapot-form) with one curl of smoke rising into a six-pointed Solomon-seal. Ties Lampblack mechanic to the Universe's central artifact.

**Sample Moment at C/R/L/U:**
- **Common: "The Fisherman Casts His Net."** Coastal scene, dawn light. Pulls up a brass jar. Dulac-register palette.
- **Rare: "The Jinni Rises from the Bottle."** Smoke-form, copper-skinned, multi-armed, fire-halo. Mughal-miniature compositional logic.
- **Legendary: "Open Sesame — the Cave Splits."** Ali Baba in shadow, the cleft-opening glowing with treasure-light, forty thieves' silhouettes in middle distance. Carré-register vivacity.
- **Ultra: "Scheherazade's 1001st Night."** Dawn through latticed window, Shahryar's hand un-clenching from the sword, Dunyazad asleep, Scheherazade still mid-sentence, three sons at her feet (canonical detail), the lamp guttering. Dulac register, gold-leaf accents.

## 8. Audience legibility

- **Mass legibility: VERY HIGH.** Disney 1992 + 2019 saturated global awareness. "Aladdin," "Sinbad," "Genie," "Magic Carpet," "Open Sesame," "1001 Nights" — all near-universally recognized. **THIS IS ALSO THE PROBLEM:** mass recognition is filtered through Disney; if LoreVault looks even slightly Disney-like, it triggers either Disney's lawyers or audience resistance.
- **Specialist legibility: VERY HIGH.** Middle Eastern literature scholars; Persianate-culture historians; Arabic-language readers (who have a strong relationship to *Alf Laylah wa-Laylah* as both literature and folk-canon); SWANA diaspora communities; comparative-literature specialists (the Burton vs. Lane vs. Mardrus textual debates are still alive).
- **Cultural pre-loads:**
  - Ongoing Middle East geopolitical conversation makes Orientalist depictions actively dangerous.
  - Disney's continued exploitation (2019 live-action) keeps the IP and the controversy in current circulation.
  - Active SWANA community pushback against "Westernized Aladdin" (Anthropology Review, REFRACT Magazine, MEE coverage) — this is a *current* discourse, not a historical one. ([Anthropology Review: Said and Aladdin](https://anthropologyreview.org/anthropology-explainers/edward-said-orientalism-and-aladdin/); [Middle East Eye on Arabian Nights orientalism](https://www.middleeasteye.net/discover/arabian-nights-orientalism-exoticism-deception-story); [REFRACT: Unlearning Arabian Nights](https://www.refractmagazine.com/writing/2021/9/15/unlearning-arabian-nights))

## 9. Recommendation

**STRONG candidate for Series 3+ (NOT Series 1 or 2). VERDICT: include but stage carefully.**

Rationale:
1. **Canon depth is enormous** — top-20 figures, 15+ Moments, 9 props, 9+ places. Comfortably 100+ cards across the series.
2. **PD foundation is rock-solid** — Galland, Burton, Lane, Lang all deep PD; the orphan tales are PD via Galland 1712/1717.
3. **Visual differentiation runway is wide** — Dulac + Carré + Mughal miniatures provide a deeper, more authentic register than Disney's saturation. This is a *competitive moat*.
4. **MTG precedent (Wizards 1993 Arabian Nights expansion)** demonstrates a major collectible-card publisher already shipped against PD source without Disney intervention.
5. **Scheherazade-as-Lattice-Narrator is a genuine LoreVault innovation** — it solves the meta-narrative problem of "who tells these cross-Universe stories?" in-canon.
6. **Why Series 3+, not 1:** the Disney aesthetic shadow plus the cultural-sensitivity load (§10) means launch needs (a) a confident, visually-distinct house style already established by earlier Series, (b) a vetted SWANA cultural-advisory panel in place, and (c) audience trust that LoreVault is *not* "Disney with extra steps." All three are end-of-Series-2 / start-of-Series-3 conditions.

## 10. Cultural sensitivity — VERY HIGH PRIORITY

**This is the most culturally-loaded Universe in the planned LoreVault portfolio after — possibly equal to — Snow White's Disney problem, but along a different axis.** Snow White is Disney-locked; Arabian Nights is *both* Disney-locked *and* Orientalism-loaded.

**The Said framework is foundational here.** Edward Said's *Orientalism* (1978) explicitly used the Arabian Nights tradition as Exhibit A of how Western academic and artistic projects construct "the Orient" as a sexualized, lazy, exotic, monolithic Other to justify Western dominance. ([Anthropology Review: Said and Aladdin](https://anthropologyreview.org/anthropology-explainers/edward-said-orientalism-and-aladdin/)) Any LoreVault Arabian Nights product that does not engage with this is going to land badly.

**Cultural traditions involved:** Arab (Iraqi, Egyptian, Syrian, Levantine, Maghrebi); Persian/Iranian; South Asian Mughal (Indian, Pakistani, Bangladeshi); Ottoman/Turkish; Andalusian. These are not interchangeable. Disney-orientalism collapses them; LoreVault must NOT.

**Orientalist tropes to AVOID outright:**
- Harem-fantasy (sexualized seraglio, dancing-girls, slave-bazaar).
- Lazy-Arab caricature (the comic-greedy-merchant; the bumbling-villain-with-accent).
- Hook-nose villainy (Jafar's Disney design has been specifically critiqued on this axis).
- Monolithic-MidEast (collapsing distinct cultures into one "Arabian" pastiche).
- "Desert savage" trope (the Forty Thieves as untamed-tribal).
- "Princess as prize" framing (any tale that ends with the woman handed over).
- Sword-swallowers, fakirs-on-nails, fire-eaters as ambient color.

**Operational requirements:**
1. **CONSULT a SWANA cultural-advisory panel before production.** Minimum: one Iraqi/Levantine literature specialist; one Persianate-culture art historian; one South Asian Mughal-art curator; one SWANA diaspora cultural critic. Pay them, credit them, take their notes.
2. **Use canonical PD names** — Badroulbadour not Jasmine, Ja'afar al-Barmaki (the historical good-vizier) explicitly distinct from "the Maghrebi Sorcerer," "Khwāja al-Sindbād" not "Sinbad-as-Brad-Pitt."
3. **The historical Caliph Harun al-Rashid is Iraqi national-cultural heritage.** He is mythologized but he was real. Treat with the gravity Iraqi audiences expect — closer to "King Arthur" than "Disney Sultan."
4. **Commit to authentic Mughal/Persianate visual register** — work directly from Met OA / V&A OA / BnF Gallica reference. NOT Hollywood-shorthand, NOT Disney-shorthand, NOT 1001-Nights-themed-restaurant-shorthand.
5. **Arabic calligraphy must be CORRECT.** If the card uses Arabic script, hire a calligrapher or licensed font from a Middle Eastern foundry. Garbled "fake-Arabic" decorative type is the single fastest way to ship a humiliating product. (Disney's *Aladdin* signage was infamously gibberish for decades.)
6. **Female agency must be foregrounded.** Scheherazade and Morgiana are the sharpest figures in the corpus. The default cards should feature them centrally, not as accessories. This is canonically defensible AND the Orientalism-counter-move.
7. **Consider commissioning illustrators of SWANA heritage** for the original LoreVault art (where original art is needed beyond the PD register inheritance). This is both ethical and a strong story-of-the-product.

## ODIN ESCALATION SUMMARY

Disney's Aladdin trademark + trade-dress portfolio is one of the most aggressively-defended in their library. Cease-and-desists have been sent over much smaller infringements than a digital collectibles series. **ABSOLUTE REDLINES:**

- **NAMES — likely safe:** Aladdin, Sinbad, Ali Baba, Scheherazade, Genie, Jinn, Magic Carpet, Open Sesame, Forty Thieves, Cave of Wonders. (Disney has a "DISNEY ALADDIN" trademark; they do NOT own bare "Aladdin.")
- **VISUAL DESIGNS — DO NOT TOUCH:** the 1992 Aladdin silhouette; Williams Genie; Jasmine outfit; Jafar's specific design; Iago; Abu; eyed Magic Carpet; tiger-skull Cave of Wonders; Sultan design; "Agrabah" as setting name.
- **COMPLIANCE PRECONDITIONS:** legal review before any concept art ships; cultural-advisory panel (§10); Léon Carré post-1928 plates flagged for separate review; calligraphy verified by a Middle Eastern foundry/calligrapher.
- **PRECEDENT TAILWIND:** Wizards of the Coast shipped *Magic: The Gathering — Arabian Nights* (December 1993) drawing from PD source without Disney intervention. The path has been walked.

The strategic answer is to **lean HARD into the Dulac + Carré + Mughal-miniature register** and to make LoreVault's Arabian Nights *visibly, deliberately not Disney* — older, deeper, more authentic, more text-faithful. That positioning is both legally protective and narratively distinctive.
